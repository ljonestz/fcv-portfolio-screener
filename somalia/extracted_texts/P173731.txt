I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT 
 
A. Country Context 
 
1. 
Somalia is currently on a path of political stabilization and reconstruction, after more than twenty years of 
conflict. Since the collapse of the Siad Barre government in 1991, cycles of conflict have fragmented the country, 
destroyed legitimate institutions and large segments of the economy, displaced millions of people, and hence created 
widespread vulnerability to external shocks, such as pandemics and droughts. The global spread of coronavirus disease 
(COVID-19) is the latest shock to affect Somalia and is likely to lead to: (i) a reduction in consumption, as flows of 
remittances decline which are a lifeline for many households and prices of imported goods rise; (ii) losses in export 
earnings;1 (iii) and a fall in fiscal revenues, particularly from trade-related taxes. This evolving context stresses the 
importance of developing Somali-wide responses to the pandemic, which considers the needs of the Federal 
Government of Somalia (FGS), the Federal Member States (FMS), and Somaliland.  
 
2. 
The adoption of the Provisional Constitution in 2012, peaceful presidential elections in 2012 and 2017, and a 
broader regularization of Somalia’s political processes represent important milestones. The Provisional Constitution 
established the basis for a federal political system, which includes the establishment of the five FMS.2. A series of legal 
and institutional reforms have been implemented to gradually develop core public sector functionalities. However, key 
decisions related to the sharing of revenues and the assignment of functions at the federal and subnational levels are 
still to be reached and remain fundamental to reaching a political settlement. The context around the evolving COVID-
19 situation is providing a further test for the role of the FGS as leader in responding to the crisis in an equitable way in 
the interests of all subnational jurisdictions, and must be underpinned by inter-governmental coordination and dialogue.  
 
3. 
Several examples demonstrate incremental progress in implementing financial and fiscal management related 
reforms. Improved fiscal management capacity has enabled the FGS to more than double its revenue collection and 
total spending between 2013 and 2019, and increasingly absorb external grants. The Central Bank of Somalia (CBS) is 
building its financial supervision capacity, enabling a significant expansion of the banking system.3 In March 2020, 
Somalia qualified for the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative Decision Point, thereby clearing arrears to 
International Financial Institutions (IFIs) and reducing the debt-to-gross domestic product (GDP) ratio from 113 percent 
in 2018 to 70 percent in 2020. This milestone reopens access to regular concessional resources from IDA and other IFIs, 
together with investment of private capital from the International Finance Corporation (IFC).4 Sustaining a positive 
trajectory will require predictable financing and improved institutions (amongst other factors) as numerous challenges 
prevail, such as weak government capacity, asymmetric federal structures, security concerns, human capital deficits, 
and low levels of state legitimacy. 
 
4. 
Although the Somali economy has been recovering at a modest pace, there is a high degree of susceptibility to 
shocks; the COVID-19 pandemic is expected to result in the contracting of the economy. Between 2013 and 2018, the 
economy grew by an average annual rate of 2.6 percent (Figure 1). The services sector, including telecommunications, 
transport, money transfer services, domestic trade-related activities are the main growth drivers. However, agriculture

consumption is severely affected.  
 
5. 
Poverty remains widespread and deep. Nearly 70 percent of the population lives below the poverty line (US$1.90 
a day in purchasing power parity terms), and another 10 percent live close to the poverty line.5 Almost nine of 10 Somali 
households are deprived of at least one fundamental dimension: monetary, electricity, education, or water and 
sanitation. Access to services is particularly limited for those living in rural areas, including nomads and internally 
displaced settlements, where poverty is deepest and markets, health clinics and schools are scarce. Investing in 
Somalia’s human capital, particularly in education and health, will be essential for the country to move out from a 
fragility trap of economic exclusion and vulnerability. However, human capital is likely to experience substantial strain 
in light of the COVID-19 pandemic, given the fragility of the health system and low levels of capacity. Furthermore, 
COVID-19 is likely to be another shock to household income, as informal sector activities decline, particularly in areas 
such as transportation and informal trade, and remittances fall.  
 
6. 
The impact of COVID-19 will be compounded by other interlinked shocks including floods, drought and the 
locust outbreak. Heavy rains and wet soils have supported the uncontrolled development and spread of desert locusts 
across Somalia and much of East Africa. An estimated 1.1 million people are experiencing crisis or worse levels of food 
insecurity and the number in need of urgent food assistance is expected to increase by 40 percent over the next six 
months. Millions of farmers and herders risk losing their crops, pasture, and main source of income. For the desert locust 
response, Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) estimates nearly US$57 million in total needs for Somalia (through 
early 2021), with a funding gap of US$34 million. 
 
7. 
In late 2019 parts of Somalia experienced some of the worst flooding in the country’s recent history, causing 
more than US$270 million in damages. In December 2019, the World Bank and FGS initiated a joint exercise to assess 
the losses and damages caused by the floods and to develop a strategy for immediate recovery and longer-term 
resilience building. The resulting Somalia Flood Impact and Needs Assessment (FINA), assessed and quantified flood 
recovery and resilience building needs across eight sectors. Sectoral needs in health (US$46 million), and education 
(US$35 million) were among the highest, underscoring the importance of continued investments in these sectors 
through Recurrent Cost and Reform Financing (RCRF) III, combined with other operations including the Somalia Crisis 
Recovery Project (SCRP, P173315). 
 
8. 
Somalia recently adopted its 9th National Development Plan (NDP9) (2020-2024), which outlines the country’s 
priorities for programs to reduce poverty and boost inclusive growth.6 It aims at promoting human development, boost 
economic recovery, strengthening governance, establishing peace and security and making politics more inclusive. The 
plan sets an ambitious agenda. Addressing these needs will require substantial financial resources, sound financial 
management (FM) systems, improved inter-governmental coordination between FGS and FMS, and a responsive citizen-
centric state system.

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
 
Until the onset of COVID-19, revenues were increasing, but not at a pace sufficient to meet the country’s needs 
 
9. 
While the Provisional Constitution set out the basis for a federal model, functioning public sector systems were 
largely absent, or unevenly developed across the subnational jurisdictions. The situation in 2012 was characterized by 
accumulating arrears for the civil service and security payroll, and no spending on public service delivery. Basic services 
were delivered by a combination of unregulated private providers and ad hoc humanitarian assistance. Limited revenues 
from trade-related taxes were used to finance activities in the federal government, which were handled in cash and 
went largely unaccounted for. Levels of public sector capacity have been and are still highly asymmetric across Somalia. 
While Puntland had a well-established civil service and associated payroll and public financial management (PFM) 
systems, the newly emerging member states had to develop their government systems from scratch.7 
 
10. 
The COVID-19 pandemic is expected to adversely affect revenue collection, reneging on recent improvements 
to tax collection. Starting from a very low baseline, the federal government has been rapidly increasing its domestic 
revenue collection from 1.7 percent of GDP in 2013 to a projected 4.5 percent of GDP in 2020, pre-COVID-19 estimate. 
Taxes on international trade dominate, accounting for an estimated two-thirds of the federal government’s revenues.8 
However, the closure of airports across Somalia is expected to contribute to lower revenues, as taxes on cargo, overflight 
fees and other administrative fees are foregone. Other tax exemption measures announced to reduce prices of basic 
staples will also lead to losses in trade-related taxes. Estimated revenue losses for the federal government could reduce 
revenues from US$234 million in the budget to US$185 million in 2020. Estimations of losses across the FMS vary, with 
states that are more dependent on international trade taxes expected to be particularly hard hit. To support Somalia to 
weather the shortfall in fiscal resources from the COVID-19 pandemic, grants are expected to substantially rise, 
potentially to as much as 9.6 percent of GDP in 2020, with Somalia’s multilateral partners, the World Bank, the African 
Development Bank (AfDB) and the European Union (EU) all expected to increase budget support on an exceptional basis. 
This situation underscores the need to redouble domestic resource mobilization efforts as the effects of the pandemic 
stabilize.  
 
11. 
As the federal government’s domestic resource mobilization is amongst the lowest in the world, and set to 
further decline with the effects of COVID-19, the state has insufficient financial means to meet its growing needs. 
Public spending remains low, with roughly half allocated to administration, across the FGS and FMS. Expenditures on 
personnel account for approximately half of the budget at the FGS level, going over 75 percent for certain FMS such as 
Jubaland and Galmudug; a feature typical of many fragile states.9 As Somalia continues the process of state-building, 
support to the wage bill has been on a sliding scale basis, to encourage the FGS to assume greater responsibility over 
time, thus enhancing sustainability. Through the RCRF approach, there are two types of financing to the wage bill.10 The 
first type is advanced financing which is paid upfront (referred as ‘baseline’ financing) and the second type is 
performance based where there are earmarked expenditures (following the achievement of Performance-Based 
Conditions (PBCs)). Both types of financing for the wage bill supported under RCRF are on a declining scale (Figure 2), so

Somalia faces multiple challenges of rebuilding and delivering education and health services 
  
16. 
The overthrow of the Barre government in 1991 and the following civil war not only disrupted economic life 
but destroyed most social service delivery systems, such as schools and health centers. Many professionals 
disappeared. In post-conflict Somalia, health and education systems remain weak, poorly resourced and inequitably 
distributed. Despite recent improvements, the country performs below Sub-Saharan Africa average and some other 
Fragility, Conflict and Violence (FCV) countries, such as Liberia, Afghanistan and Iraq, on key human capital indicators. 
Somalia’s school enrollment rates are among the lowest in the world (gross enrolment ratio for primary school: 32 
percent in 2015/16). The country ranks among the highest for maternal mortality ratio (732 per 100,000 live births in 
2016) and life expectancy at birth is only 58.5 years (2017). The estimated fertility rate (6.2), second highest globally, 
further strains already weak service delivery systems.13 The challenges in both sectors are complex and multidimensional 
including the areas of training and shortage of medical and educational staff, school and health infrastructure, 
curriculum, unregulated private actors, and finance. COVID-19 could further stretch the fragile healthcare system (the 
first case of COVID-19 was reported on March 16, 2020, and by May 28 official figures suggested that total confirmed 
cases numbered 1,828 and total deaths stood at 72). 
 
17. 
Investing in human capital will be essential if Somalia is to return to prosperity. The crucial 40 percent of Somalis 
who are now aged six to 18 need to acquire the knowledge, health, and skills to become productive contributors to the 
country’s economy and thus break the cycle of conflict, exclusion, and vulnerability.14 Therefore, RCRFII has provided 
important support to FGS and FMS to rebuild and deliver tangible education and health services to citizens. 
 
State legitimacy vis-à-vis Somalia’s citizens remains low 
 
18. 
The newly emerged institutions are still weak, contested and captured by groups of elites, such that patterns 
of fragility may be reinforced. For almost three quarters of the Somali population (73 percent or 11.3 million citizens, 
UN estimates 2018), i.e. Somalis under the age of thirty, state collapse is the only political order they have ever known 
for most of their lives. During the conflict, local informal governance structures, consisting mostly of militias and 
traditional and religious leaders, filled the gap of lacking basic state functions. Distrust of the population towards the 
central authority, fueled in the pre-war period, has been entrenched during the civil war. Low citizens’ trust combined 
with inadequate resources are preventing the state from providing the public goods and services necessary to 
consolidate its legitimacy. Citizens must see a rapid peace or regime change dividend, with a government performing its

C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives 
 
19. 
RCRF Phase II (P154875) and Additional Financing (AF, P167224) have provided significant support during the 
implementation of Somalia’s 8th National Development Plan (NDP8, 2017-2020), and helped Somalia reaching the 
Enhanced Heavily Indebted Countries (HIPC) Initiative Decision Point in March 2020. The RCRF III will continue to 
contribute to the Country Partnership Framework (CPF) (Report No. 124734-SO for 2019 – 2021) focus area of building 
institutions to deliver services and provide critical support for the implementation of Somalia’s NDP9 (2020-2024), which 
identified the promotion of human and economic development, strengthening governance, and making politics more 
inclusive as pressing needs.15 In doing so, RCRFIII will support the Government on its path towards reaching the HIPC 
Completion Point in about three years’ time. 
 
20. 
More specifically, the RCRFIII will contribute to four main higher-level objectives. Firstly, it will support macro-
fiscal stability by providing predictable financing (salary payments and other recurrent costs for the given period). 
Second, the operation will provide strengthened incentives for achievement of important reforms under the Turn 
Around Regime (TAR) and HIPC Completion Point Triggers. Third, the operation will contribute to the further 
establishment of a nascent system of inter-governmental fiscal relations within the federation. Finally, it will increase 
state legitimacy and credibility through support of service delivery by FGS and FMS authorities and associated inter-
governmental fiscal transfers, as well as helping to ensure that on-budget resources for education and health flow to 
schools and communities for frontline service delivery. In this light, an improved citizen-state dialogue through citizen 
engagement activities will also be key to increasing citizens’ trust in the newly formed institutions.

B. Project Components 
 
22. 
The Overall Approach to RCRF III is one of continuity with RCRF II, but with additional measures to providing a 
strengthened response to the COVID-19 pandemic and an increase in financing for transfers to FMS. The three-core 
components established under RCRF II are continued, namely: i) Recurrent cost finance to reform resource management 
systems; ii) Strengthen inter-governmental fiscal relations; and iii) Transfers for core government functions and 
foundational education and health service delivery mechanisms in eligible FMS. The primary additions include, first, DLIs 
(now referred to as “PBCs” following the recent issuance of new IPF-PBC Guidance) 16 were introduced in Component 1 
of RCRF II in 2018 to strengthen the reform incentives within RCRF and support strengthened policy dialogue around 
key reforms to meet HIPC Decision and Completion Points. RCRF III will introduce Performance Based Conditions (PBCs) 
to incentivize reforms and associated funding within the FMS for improved governance and education and health service 
delivery (Sub-Component 2.2). Second, reducing the number of FGS reform benchmarks (Sub-Component 1.2) from 
seven under RCRF II Disbursement Linked Indicator (DLIs) to five under RCRF III (PBCs), the operation creates a smaller 
and better focused set of reform incentives for FGS. Third, RCRF III will provide resources for inter-governmental system 
strengthening, including support to improved inter-governmental fiscal reporting (Sub-Component 2.3). Fourth, RCRF III 
will support a change of approach to recurrent cost financing in the education sector, moving gradually from the existing 
RCRF II teacher payroll financing approach to a school performance grants approach (Sub-Component 3.2). Finally, RCRF 
III introduces a new Component on Transparency and Citizen Engagement for Improved Service Delivery, including 
investments in impact evaluation. 
 
23. 
The project will contribute to COVID-19 response and recovery in the following ways: 
a. Sustained fiscal support to FGS through wage bill financing: RCRF III Component 1 provides US$7.3 million of 
direct wage bill financing to FGS, plus up to US$6.3 million of further financing subject to attainment of PBCs, 
which will help to mitigate the projected FGS fiscal shortfalls over the medium-term. 
b. Scaled-up fiscal support to FMS through fiscal transfers: RCRF III Sub-Component 3.1 provides US$10.8 
million of financing for transfers to FMS for core government functions, which will help to mitigate the 
projected FMS-level fiscal shortfalls over the medium-term. In addition, RCRF III will make available up to 
US$11.7 million of fiscal transfers for education and health service delivery subject to achievement of PBCs, 
including flexible funds for health services (including CIOVID-19 response) in the second half of 2020. 
c. Scale-up of the Marwo Caafimaad Female Health Worker (FHW) Program and re-training for COVID-19 
response: RCRF III will provide US$10.1 million of health sector financing, including supporting scale-up of the 
FHW program from 377 workers in three regions in 2019, starting in 2020 and reaching 3,000 workers across 
all FMS and Benadir by 2023 and essential investments in government contract management capabilities. 
Further, the FGS and FMS ministries of health and World Bank team are currently working to re-focus the FHW 
program on the COVID-19 response. Options under discussion include: 
i. FHW role in detecting, isolating, and reporting cases as well as community awareness raising; 
ii. Support for system of case reporting and response; 
iii. Support for increased supervision of the FHW system; 
iv. Personal protective equipment (PPE) for FHWs and possibly other supplies (primarily masks), and; 
v. Accelerating roll-out of new FHWs in at-risk districts.

24. 
Adapting operational implementation to ensure public sector continuity during crises such as COVID-19 will also 
be important. The overall plan for operational implementation is also sensitive to the COVID-19 pandemic in terms of 
the implementation plan – as Table 1 demonstrates, activities in 2020 are focused almost exclusively on helping FMS 
mitigate the effects of COVID-19 through a Health Service Delivery Transfer. In addition to helping the public sector to 
mitigate the COVID-19 pandemic’s health and fiscal effects, RCRF III will support changes to public sector working 
practices. This will include investments in videoconferencing facilities for ministries of finance, education and health. 
RCRF supervision and fiduciary assurance arrangements are already designed to overcome severe access restrictions 
resulting from the security situation in Somalia. Monitoring Agent (MA) review of documentation together with desk-
based FM review, supported by enhanced direct interaction with government officials remotely (WebEx etc), will 
continue to provide significant assurance for both RCRF II and RCRF III. In addition, the team will draw on lessons from 
targeted audits during the Ebola crisis in Sierra Leone and Liberia, including introducing periodic risk-based preliminary 
reviews. 
 
25.

FGS and FMS under Component 1 and 2 will contribute to the 4th TAR strategic goal, towards a more resilient, inclusive 
and stable socio-economic environment, including helping Somalia to remain on track with HIPC Completion Point 
Triggers. Component 2 will contribute to the 3rd TAR strategic goal, on improved inter-governmental relations and 
resource sharing, helping to cement the emerging inter-governmental fiscal settlement. Component 3 will scale-up 
transfers to FMS for increased pro-poor budget allocations for service delivery in health and education (TAR goal #4).

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design 
 
66. 
Importance of financial incentives to support reforms in Somalia. The RCRF financing and policy dialogue has 
been used effectively to support both broad policy objectives, for example improved regional fiscal integration, as well 
as in support of sectoral and administrative reform programs for education and health and public FM. The financing 
instrument has provided incentives and grounds for technical policy dialogue, and technical support has turned the 
ambition to reality, and has been strongly supported by the FGS. Therefore, PBCs are a key design feature of the project. 
 
67. 
Need to incentivize and strengthen FGS/FMS relations. In previous RCRF operations, the share allocated to fiscal 
federalism strengthening was minimal compared to the other components (Component 2). Moreover, FMS were not 
incentivized financially for good performance. Consequently, the current operation has introduced PBCs for FMS, which 
is seen as important to cascade good public FM practices and an increased financing of social sectors.  
 
68. 
Going beyond input-based investments and towards outcomes. The RCRF II education and health activities were 
initially very input focused, notably the teacher wage bill support. RCRF II AF began to introduce a greater focus on 
quality of service delivery, notably with the design of the Female Health Worker program. This operation continues the 
move more towards improved education and health quality, rather than purely input focused. In education, this 
operation will introduce a system of school grants based on performance. Moreover, impact evaluation will be carried 
out in social sectors to better understand in a country like Somalia what can be achieved to improve education and/or 
health access and quality in a very constrained environment. 
 
69. 
Strengthening citizens feedback to improve service delivery and state legitimacy. Previous operations were 
more state-centred due to the importance of (re)building state systems. Due to recurrent state legitimacy problems and 
increasing demand from citizens for accountability, this operation includes a new component to facilitate citizens 
discussions on budget issues but also citizen feedback and corrective measures on education/health at local level (in 
selected locations) using several methods to collect feedback. It should kickstart a process to better link reforms and 
citizens to ensure an increased development impact.

B. Results Monitoring and Evaluation Arrangements 
 
79. 
Project M&E is to be guided by the results framework that has been developed for the Project. The purpose of 
this framework is to track performance of the overall PDO. Unlike the framework used to measure performance for the 
annual review process, which is measured once each year to determine the next year’s spending needs and budget 
allocations, the indicators in the results framework are designed to measure progress throughout the duration of the 
project. Each indicator has a baseline and end target value, as well as target values for each year. The indicators specify 
the frequency of monitoring, sources of data and instruments through which data will be collected. The framework is 
presented in Section III. 
 
80. 
Progress and achievement of project development objectives will be monitored and assessed through the 
following activities: 
a. At the overall project level, the project implementation unit (PIU) is responsible for tracking progress against the 
results framework indicators, drawing on submissions from FMS project managers. At the Quarterly Project 
Management meetings the PIU will regularly review and report on progress made to the PSC and the World Bank 
and will flag any issues requiring attention. Results will be fed into recalibrating further implementation efforts, 
including necessary mid-course corrections. The World Bank M&E specialist is also responsible for reporting on 
the results framework by publishing Implementation Status Reports (ISR).  
b. Apart from the outcome level indicators, a set of specific, relevant, and measurable intermediate outcome 
indicators will also be monitored. Information will be collected by the PIU on the performance of all participating 
institutions as per the results framework. 
c. At the component level, TIUs will be responsible for M&E of specific component activities being implemented in 
their ministries and agencies. TIUs will submit reports to the Project Coordinator. The Project Coordinator will 
consolidate reports and submit to the PSC and the World Bank.

Predictable financing for human development and building the social contract. While the FGS is realizing

scaled-up system for preventive action are estimated to be close to US$5 billion per year. In the most optimistic scenario, 
the net savings is almost US$70 billion per year. A recent World Bank cost-benefit analysis of donor investments in 
conflict prevention estimated substantial cost savings26.  For example, if prevention cost US$100 million per intervention 
with a success rate of 75 percent, the cost savings are estimated to be so large that the system would pay for itself 
within a few years, as a result of reduced spending requirements on aid.

Technical Analysis 
 
90. 
The proposed RCRFIII design builds on lessons learned from RCRFII and AF and on an extensive body of recent 
analytical work conducted in Somalia. Through PBC-based financing, the project design ensures that financing is linked 
to reform progress. Reform incentives are offered in the form of variable levels of financing for the FGS civil service wage 
bill conditional upon achievement of the reform benchmarks focused on the core areas, namely: revenue, PFM and 
payment processes, and inter-governmental fiscal relations. The project’s technical design takes into account the 
country’s weak capacity and the right balance between rapid results, i.e., payment of salaries of non-security staff for 
the establishment of public services, and medium-term objectives to ensure sustainability. An internal control 
framework to institute controls in payments of salaries and non-salary recurrent cost expenditures in the civil service is 
included in the design. It incorporates the Government’s development priorities, especially its evolving priorities to 
expand service delivery in the social sectors education and health. The technical features of the project design have 
been examined by World Bank staff over the course of project preparation and are considered sound and robust. This 
combined approach which focuses on service delivery with strong fiduciary controls is also important for building trust 
in Somalia’s institutions with citizens and international partners.
