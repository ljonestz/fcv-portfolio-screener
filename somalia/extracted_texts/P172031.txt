I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT 
 
A. Country Context 
 
1. 
Somalia has experienced prolonged conflict and is among the poorest countries in the world with a nominal 
gross domestic product (GDP) per capita of US$327.1  An estimated 69 percent of the Somali population live in poverty 
(below US$1.90 per day, 2018 estimate) with many more living just above the poverty line.2 This is further compounded 
by a high population growth rate of 2.83 percent (2015-2020)3, with an annual economic growth rate of approximately 
2.8 percent (2016-2020).  Due to the coronavirus disease of 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic and associated disruptions of 
economic activity, the economy is expected to contract by 1.5 percent in 2020. The growth outlook for  2021 is projected 
at 2.5 percent4, compared to a pre-COVID 19 forecast of 3.2 percent. The economy is highly dependent on foreign 
remittances, which are estimated to make up 31.2 percent of the country’s GDP (2020), with less access to remittances 
among rural and poor populations.  Fiscal capacity remains low, with a government expenditure to GDP ratio of 12.4 
percent (2020), underlined by nascent tax mobilization systems with a tax-to-GDP ratio of 2.5 percent (2020), which 
further constrains service delivery.5 
2. 
Somalia is transitioning towards increased stability through institutional and political reforms, which began 
with the adoption of a provisional constitution and a Federal Government in 2012.  The federal system established under 
the 2012 provisional constitution carved out four new Federal Member States (FMS), resulting in a total of six under the 
Federal Government of Somalia (FGS).6  In the transition to greater political stability, the Government has improved 
financial transparency and institutional structures through the adoption of reforms and improved governance.  However, 
an active armed insurgency, unresolved constitutional issues, weak service delivery capacity, fiscal capacity constraints, 
and continual humanitarian crises remain. Somaliland has a politically complex relationship with the FGS, which is 
unresolved and impacting constructive relations towards economic  development.   
3. 
In March 2020, Somalia qualified for the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative Decision Point. 
Somalia has been working to maintain macroeconomic stability, implement a poverty reduction strategy, and put in place 
reforms focused on fiscal stability, improving governance and debt management, strengthening social conditions, and 
supporting inclusive growth. These successful reforms have laid the foundation for inclusive economic growth and for 
addressing the needs of the country’s most vulnerable people.  After 30 years outside the international financial system, 
Somalia has achieved the HIPC milestone, reopening access to regular concessional financing from the International 
Development Association (IDA) and other International Financing Institutions, along with investments of private capital, 
including those from the International Finance Corporation (IFC).    
4. 
Somalia’s recurrent natural disasters and conflict hamper development efforts. The country faced the impact of 
crippling droughts from 2016 to 2017 and again in 2019, together with protracted and ongoing armed conflicts, resulting 
in the internal displacement of more than 2.6 million people and widespread food insecurity and hunger. Cyclical floods 
and droughts are increasingly common features and their co-existence poses a continuous state of crisis. With most 
Somalis dependent on agriculture (primarily livestock) and forestry, continued climate and conflict challenges destabilize 
daily life. The interlinkages between climate and environmental change, cyclical drought, poverty, fragility, severe food

5. 
The Somali population is growing rapidly with an annual population growth rate of almost 3 percent, resulting 
in the doubling of the population approximately every 24 years. While there is no recent census data, Somalia’s total 
population is estimated to be 15.4 million7, with a high fertility rate of 6.9 births per woman in 2019.8  Substantial 
population growth has resulted in a young population, with 66 percent of the population under 19 years of age and 78 
percent under the age of 30.9  Youth unemployment is high and educational attainment limited, with only 16 percent of 
the population completing primary school and 7 percent completing secondary school.10 As of 2019, 46 percent of the 
population lived in urban areas with rural and nomadic populations constituting the remaining 54 percent.11 
6. 
Gender disparities in Somalia are among the worst in the world, characterized by limited access to formal 
education, high rates of gender-based violence (GBV), as well as a nearly universal prevalence of female genital mutilation 
(FGM) at 99.2 percent prevalence among women.  By 14 years of age, almost all girls have experienced FGM, with little 
variation across age and education groups. Women also face barriers to formal economic participation. Seventy five 
percent of women have received no education.12 A 2016 GBV survey commissioned by the World Bank and the United 
Nations (UN) partners found that 17 percent of women have experienced some form of non-partner physical or sexual 
violence, while intimate partner violence is a common lifetime experience, affecting 36  percent of women interviewed.  
The impacts and costs of GBV on human potential, as well as health and well-being, are severe and present a significant 
barrier to development. 
 
B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
 
7. 
Despite some progress, Somalia’s human capital is likely among the world’s lowest.  The Human Capital Index 
(HCI)13, developed by the World Bank, has yet to be calculated for Somalia due to a lack of data on educational outcomes. 
However, Somalia’s human capital  level likely lags behind neighboring countries as underperforming human development 
capital indicators underline the need to invest in the quality and coverage of services for human development. 
Improvements in human capital  are demonstrated to accelerate sustainable growth and prosperity in countries.  
8. 
Somalia’s lagging health outcomes reflect the country’s insecurity, vulnerability, and deep-rooted poverty, 
limiting opportunities for people to access basic social services, including education and health. As seen in Table 1, 
Somalia’s health indicators remain among the worst in the world, with an average life expectancy of 56 years. Other 
indicators lag behind those in the World Health Organization’s Regional Office for Africa (WHO AFRO) as well as most 
indicators in select, comparable Fragility, Conflict, and Violence (FCV) impacted countries in World Health Organization’s

The 2009 Essential Package of Health Services (EPHS)21, which is currently being implemented, does not reach 
the entire population.  EPHS implementation started in 2013 through the Joint Health and Nutrition Programme (JHNP) 
and the Health Consortium for Somali People.22  While the full package was not delivered under the JHNP due to limitations 
in available financing and implementation capacity, the JHNP coordinated health service delivery between partners.  
However, following the closure of the JHNP after the end of  the UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office’s 
(FCDO; previously Department for International Development (DFID)) financing in 2016, the previously coordinated EPHS 
implementation became fragmented, with partial geographic coverage and varying package components supported by 
different funding partners. According to 2017 WHO figures, approximately 47 out of 89 districts (5.7 million people) were 
covered by part of the EPHS, representing approximately 41 percent of the population. In 2020, the Government updated 
the EPHS, developing a comprehensive package of health services supported by the development partners including the 
World Bank under the leadership of the Government.  
12. 
The private sector is an important health service provider.  At least 60 percent of health services and 70 percent 
of the country’s medicines are estimated to be delivered by the private sector, primarily in urban areas. However, due to 
Somalia’s nascent health sector regulatory capacity, there are no quality standards nor functional regulatory bodies for 
health services or pharmaceutical products. As a result, the private sector is largely unregulated, and providers can operate 
without sufficient  oversight which may lead to services and products of unknown quality. This limits the full potential of 
the private sector as formal private sector investment requires functional regulations to be effective.  
13. 
There are gaps in essential supplies such as health workers, essential medicines, and medical equipment, 
particularly in public facilities.  Somalia’s public health worker density is 0.43 per 1,000 people and private health worker 
density is 0.49 per 1,000 people. The combined health worker density figure (0.92) is significantly below the WHO’s cut-
off for “critical” human resource shortages, which is 2.28 health workers per 1000 people. The availability of qualified

15. 
Socio-cultural factors play a significant role in the health seeking behaviors and status of women in Somalia.  
Responsibility for decisions related to health-seeking behavior, such as when to get treatment at a clinic, resides primarily 
with men and contributes to delays in seeking care. According to the 2020 SHDS, 42 percent of women report needing 
permission to access services as a barrier to healthcare during pregnancy.  Furthermore, early marriage is prevalent and a 
significant factor contributing to early first pregnancies, high fertility rates and the high maternal mortality ratio. Thirty six 
percent of women between 20 and 24 years old were married before 18 years of age, and average age at first marriage is 
20 for women and 23 for men.23  
16. 
The global COVID-19 pandemic is a pressing problem in Somalia, exacerbated by a weak primary health system, 
nascent surveillance and laboratory capacity, and limited hospital services.  As of May 25, 2021, Somalia had 14,632 
confirmed coronavirus cases and 767 deaths, with anecdotal reports of additional, unconfirmed cases and quickly rising 
deaths.24 In early February 2021, the country faced a surge in new cases with suspected new COVID-19 variants. This is 
expected to further constrain Somalia’s already fragile health system. With support from partners, Somalia successfully 
applied to the COVID-19 Vaccines Global Access (COVAX) Facility on December 7, 2020.  In the application, the Government 
selected Astra Zeneca/Oxford University as the vaccine of choice. The COVAX Facility will provide free vaccines for 20 
percent of the population (approximately six million doses for Somalia- each patient is expected to receive two doses). 
The first 300,000 doses  arrived in March 2021, and the vaccination rollout is ongoing. The Government and partners, 
including the World Bank, are mobilizing resources to further support the rollout of vaccines. The Government has also 
taken important steps to improve Somalia’s COVID-19 response, including establishing testing capacity at the National 
Public Health and Reference Laboratory in Mogadishu, developing rapid response teams, and increasing the country’s 
intensive care unit bed capacity from 15 at the onset of the outbreak to 47 today.25  However, existing health system 
capacity limitations constrain the COVID-19 response and with the current surge, the Government has identified the 
following as major constraints to mounting an effective response: (i) limited oxygen production and distribution capacity; 
(ii) limited operational capacity of the Ministries of Health (both at FGS and FMS level);  and (iii) constrained prevention 
capacity i.e. ineffective surveillance programs and other prevention measures.  A description of the World Bank’s support

HEALTH FINANCING 
17. 
Service delivery challenges are underpinned by very limited financing for health in Somalia. The per capita 
expenditure on health is approximately US$6 per person per year (2019), far below Sub-Saharan Africa’s average of US$81 
(2019).26  Government expenditure on health as a percentage of per capita health expenditure is 17 percent (2019). Donor 
financing is an important source of health expenditure, comprising 50 percent of per capita expenditure (2019) and much 
of this is off-treasury.27  The high proportion of off-treasury donor resources has limited the Government’s involvement 
in many aspects of health sector programming, constraining the ability to increase efficiency in spending and Government 
leadership in the sector. 
18. 
Out-of-pocket payments (OOP) as a percentage of per capita health expenditure in Somalia are high at 46 
percent.  Average annual household OOP on health is estimated at US$2 per capita out of a total of US$6 (2019), and 
varies substantially between the richest quintile and the poorest quintile, indicating that households are accessing 
healthcare services based on the ability to pay instead of their healthcare needs, resulting in health inequities.28 The 2020 
SHDS survey found that 48 percent of households used their income to pay for health expenditures and another 25 percent 
used resources from relatives or friends. Remittances are likely an important source of OOP for health, but there is little 
data on the extent of their contribution. There are almost no formal risk-protection mechanisms in the country: health 
insurance is limited to private plans for international companies and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and 
estimated to be 1 percent of total health expenditures.   
HEALTH SECTOR STEWARDSHIP 
19. 
Somalia’s Federal MoH (FMoH) is nascent and at the early stages of building capacity to manage health services, 
including contract management. The FMoH was established in 2012 along with the formation of the federal government, 
and stewardship, leadership, and managerial functions are still developing. The Government‘s role in health service 
delivery is limited, with most services delivered by NGOs financed by partners.  As a result, key functions including 
planning, budgeting, finance and governance are not fully developed.  The FMoH is highly reliant on external support for 
its operations. Approximately 25 percent of the FMoH’s over 500 employees are civil servants; the remainder are donor 
financed and are primarily contracted on a short-term basis. While the FMoH has leadership structures and some policies 
and procedures in place, its decision-making process and management systems are not fully defined, and capacity to 
manage programs independently of partners is limited.29  
20. 
Against this backdrop, Somalia’s FMoH is facing significant governance challenges.  In 2019, Transparency 
International’s Corruption Perceptions Index ranked Somalia at the bottom of all 180 countries.  Weak financial control 
systems was highlighted by an Auditor General’s Office report, indicating governance challenges within the FMoH. The 
current Government has demonstrated a commitment to fight corruption, as evidenced by the passage of anti-corruption 
legislation in September 2019, and continues to work toward establishing transparent and credible government systems.  
21.

ministerial structures and limited capacity, with few resources on-treasury (around US$100,000 a year), low budget 
execution rates, and few to no civil servants. Policies and procedures for health service delivery are in development. These

The Project contributes to the World Bank’s twin goals to end extreme poverty and boost shared prosperity. A

become available and accessible.

Box 3: Government Contracting of Health Service Delivery to Non-state Actors to Improve Health Outcomes 
 
Government contracting of non-state actors to deliver health services is a means to successfully improve health outcomes by 
rapidly expanding service delivery in situations where the Government has limited capacity and there is ongoing insecurity. 
Afghanistan (see Box 5) and Cambodia had notable success expanding health service coverage through a contracting out model. 
For example, in Cambodia, health service visits of sick, low-income individuals in areas where health services were contracted out 
increased from five percent at baseline to 33 percent in two and a half years, in comparison to the control where the Government 
directly delivered health services and visits increased from six percent to nine percent in the same period.   Learning from health 
service delivery lessons in Somalia as well as in FCV countries, such as Afghanistan and South Sudan, where a Government 
contracting model was used (see Box 5 and Table 7.3 in Annex 7), the Project’s primary service delivery modality will be 
Government contracting of NGOs for the delivery of health services.  Key features of this contracting arrangement, which aim to 
improve health service outcomes and set the model apart from current health service delivery are: 
 
1.) Robust data systems for health service delivery improvements:  use of reliable data is essential to improve health 
outcomes and establish accountability.  The Project will develop robust data systems through TPM and Government 
HMIS capacity development. Data use for decision making will allow the Government to work with implementers to 
improve health outcomes.  Currently, there is very little health service data in the country, making it difficult to make 
improvements based on data.  Routine performance management, including ongoing review of both DHIS2 and TPM 
data and work with contracted NGOs to improve performance, will be a critical Government contract management 
function.  
2.) Accountability for health service outcomes:  Increased availability of data will allow the Government to hold NGOs 
accountable for health service outcomes.  This focus on health outcomes instead of inputs is a critical factor in the 
success of Government health service delivery contracting, which has helped to accelerate results in fragile contexts such 
as Afghanistan and Cambodia.  The Damal Caafimaad Project will also lay the foundation for results-based contracting.  
While data and payment systems are not yet sufficiently routinized to use results-based contracting as a primary 
payment method, NGO providers will have the opportunity to earn a bonus for health service improvements based on 
the health facility assessment, which is a step towards a results based contracting system to further strengthen 
accountability. 
3.) Autonomy for results and innovation: Health service providers will be given autonomy to identify measures and 
innovations to improve health service outcomes in their coverage areas, beyond minimum standards established in their 
contracts.  Providers will be held accountable for outcomes and not specific methods, which may not work to improve 
health outcomes within their coverage areas.   
4.) Government contracting of NGOs to improve sustainability, coordination, and oversight for results: Fragmentation of 
service delivery has created gaps and overlaps in health service delivery, compromising health outcomes.  Further, the 
lack of Government engagement in health service delivery has limited oversight of service delivery and accountability for 
outcomes, impacting health services.  Through the Damal Caafimaad Project, the Government will contract NGOs to 
deliver health services.  Currently, NGOs are contracted by partners outside of Government systems.  Government 
contracting aims to create a system through which service providers are accountable to the Government, ensure there is 
alignment between partners, and the Government develops long-term capacity to contract for health service delivery.   
5.) Service delivery by contracted NGOs to maximize resources and outcomes: To maximize resources allocated to service 
delivery and improve management systems, contracted NGOs will be expected to directly provide at least 80 percent of 
services within their coverage areas. Contracted NGOs are planned to be national governmental or non-governmental 
organizations or international NGOs, of which there is a core group in Somalia with a demonstrated track record of 
service delivery in the country context and strong local knowledge. The ability of these NGOs to effectively deliver 
services is anticipated to be strengthened by the service delivery model. Sub-contracting will be limited to no more than 
20 percent of contracts, based on lessons from Afghanistan and South Sudan which found, respectively, that sub-
contracting reduced resources to service delivery and diluted field-level management. Contractors will have the 
opportunity to enter joint proposals with other firms. Sub-contracting (up to 20 percent) under service delivery contracts

E. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners 
 
49. 
The World Bank’s engagement in Somalia’s health sector is well-timed to support the Government, in 
collaboration with other partners, to address core challenges in the Somalia health system: reducing health sector 
fragmentation, developing institutional capacity, and strengthening PFM. In light of increased donor fragmentation in 
the Somali health sector, exacerbated by the collapse of the JHNP, there is substantial momentum among partners to 
support the Government to reestablish effective health sector coordination.  The GFF’s experience and focus on 
Government-led coordination and alignment of resources around government priorities, along with the World Bank’s 
experience supporting Governments to coordinate partners, position the World Bank and GFF to support the Government 
in these areas.  The Government is leading partner coordination for EPHS implementation, with support from the GFF.  
Initially, it is anticipated that Damal Caafimaad’s Project coverage areas will complement areas where FCDO supports EPHS 
implementation, in close coordination with Gavi and the Global Fund who support aspects of the EPHS.  In addition, given 
the MoH’s nascent state, the Government has requested a strong focus on institutional capacity development.  The World 
Bank’s global experience in supporting health systems strengthening and enhancing the use of Government systems is 
well-placed to support the Government’s efforts in institutional capacity development, particularly in comparison with 
development partners that primarily support vertical programs and provide resources off-Government systems.  Finally, 
in light of the FMoH’s governance challenges, the Government has requested The World Bank’s assistance in 
strengthening PFM within the FMoH.  The World Bank supports Government-wide PFM in Somalia through the Second 
Public Financial Management Capacity Strengthening Project (P151492), and has substantial experience supporting PFM 
in the health sector within other countries, demonstrating a clear comparative advantage for World Bank PFM support.  
 
F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design 
 
50. 
The Project incorporates lessons learned from other health projects supported by the World Bank in FCV 
contexts and lessons from World Bank-financed projects in Somalia.  Lessons from other health projects, including 
Afghanistan and South Sudan, demonstrate the potential for a results-based contracting modality, in which the 
Government contracts NGOs, to rapidly scale up health service delivery and improve health outcomes. The Afghan model 
provided the blueprint for the country’s current service delivery modality, which is recognized as innovative and 
successfully achieving results despite decades of conflict (See Box 5). The South Sudan model, which was based on 
Afghanistan’s model, had initial successes, but suffered from implementation and fiduciary challenges, eventually leading 
the World Bank to support the Government through a model in which health service delivery funds flow directly from the 
World Bank to UN agencies, who manage service delivery.   
 
51. 
There are important lessons on contracting non-state actors for service delivery from both the South Sudan and 
Afghan model, as well as the application of the Afghan model in other contexts.  Key lessons learned from the Afghan 
and South Sudan models include the need to establish a defined contract management function in a Project Management 
Unit (PMU) with qualified contract management specialists; initial performance based contract elements must be simple 
and either possible to verify in the absence of regular supervisory field visits and/ or ensure regular independent audits of 
the project, contractors, and sub-contractors.  In addition, lessons learned also point to the need to consider the level of 
capacity in the country when determining the level of flexibility and autonomy in a performance-based contract.  In 
Afghanistan, considerable autonomy was given to NGOs to deliver services, which helped NGOs adapt to the context and 
needs to deliver results.  However, the same level of autonomy in South Sudan resulted in service delivery and fiduciary 
shortcomings.  Clearly defining contract elements and fiduciary requirements, including minimum staffing, supply, 
equipment, supervision, and training requirements, will be important.  A detailed comparison of the contracting

Box 5: Lessons Learned from the Afghanistan Health Sector Experience 
Prior to 2002, coverage of primary health care services in Afghanistan was low and inconsistent. Health services were 
provided by NGOs, each with their own mandates and funded through different external sources, leaving activities 
uncoordinated, unfocused and without government involvement. The situation was exacerbated by a lack of clear 
objectives, unclear geographic coverage areas among organizations, staff shortages in rural areas, and an absence of 
explicit national priorities.  A 2003 multiple indicator cluster survey (MICS) found coverage of key health services such as 
skilled birth attendance, antenatal care, and child immunization was only 15 percent.  
Since 2001, Afghanistan has made notable progress in improving maternal, newborn, and child survival despite still 
experiencing insecurity and unstable governance. Coverage of maternal and child health service indicators improved 
significantly from 2001-2015.  For example, the immunization rate for DPT3/Penta and measles increased to 70 percent  
by 2015, due to Ministry of Public Health (MOPH) key policies. Among the most important policy change include defining 
a package of priority health services (i.e. basic package of health services), contracting NGOs to deliver the basic package 
of services, and prioritizing monitoring and evaluation of health sector performance. The three key lessons from the 
Afghanistan experience are listed below: 
1. Establishing a Basic Package of Health Services (BPHS) was key: The BPHS successfully unified the sector in delivering 
a set of high impact interventions through an over-arching strategy for the sector with clearly defined priorities. The 
MOPH leveraged its stewardship and the BPHS to strategically direct the health sector. 
2. Contracting of NGOs for service delivery, while allowing flexibility and autonomy: Contracting of NGOs while 
allowing for autonomy and flexibility was critical, as it allowed the government to rapidly scale up service delivery, 
while still allowing NGOs the agility to respond to communities’ needs and providing services in rapidly changing 
insecure areas. By setting key priorities, allocating geographical responsibility, providing financing, and carefully 
monitoring performance, the MOPH has been able to provide direction to what was previously an uncoordinated and 
chaotic effort, while ensuring alignment to the BPHS’ strategic priorities.  
3. Independent, frequent, and robust monitoring and evaluation: The health sector benefited enormously from 
frequent, robust, and independent data collected for performance monitoring and evaluation. Using different sources 
of data ensured the accountability of service providers and allowed the MOPH to identify problems, act quickly to 
resolve them, and track whether progress had been achieved. 
52. 
The Project incorporates lessons from partner implementation of the Somalia EPHS.  A core challenge of EPHS 
implementation has been the lack of coordination between partners.  To address this, the World Bank, with GFF support 
for coordination, is working closely with partners along with the Government to harmonize EPHS planning and 
implementation.  A second challenge is fragmentation and limited data on EPHS implementation, making it difficult to 
understand levels of service coverage and improve health service delivery.  Damal Caafimaad’s focus on development of 
the country’s DHIS2 system, through sub-component 2.1 and coupled with TPM, will support annual household and health 
facility surveys with the aim to increase the availability and use of data for decision making.  Finally, the Project’s TPM 
arrangements, particularly nationwide facility and household surveys at the beginning and end of project, along with 
regular coordination meetings to share and discuss results, aim to increase accountability for health service delivery under 
the Government’s leadership.

III. IMPLEMENTATION ARRANGEMENTS 
 
A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements 
 
55. 
The Damal Caafimaad Project arrangements aim to strengthen the institutional, operational, and technical 
capacity of Somalia’s MoHs. Due to governance issues in April 2020 as previously described, the FMoH requested 
immediate support from the World Bank to strengthen fiduciary capacity.  Subsequently, a roadmap to support PFM 
capacity building in the short, medium and long-term was developed. The World Bank  mobilized various resources to 
respond to the FMoH’s immediate needs: short-term activities including the hiring of fiduciary consultants and TA has 
been supported during Project preparation using funds from the ongoing RCRF-II project, the Multi Partner Fund (MPF), 
and the Pandemic Emergency Financing Facility (PEF).  The PEF COVID-19 country payout supported the FMoH to address 
internal fiduciary challenges which occurred following the FMoH’s receipt of COVID-19 funds at the pandemic’s onset.  
The Project’s implementation arrangements, described below, incorporate the vision and longer-term activities required 
to develop credible PFM systems in Somalia’s MoHs in a consistent and phased approach, while ensuring the MoH’s 
capacity is enhanced to fulfil its role in fiduciary and technical leadership as the Project’s implementing agency.  
 
56. 
Project Coordination and Implementation Unit (PCIU) at FGS-MoH. Under the Project, a Project Coordination 
and Implementation Unit (PCIU) will be established as the responsible implementing entity in the FMoH. To ensure 
sufficient support to the Government to implement the project, given the nascent state of FMoH structures, the 
establishment of the PCIU is a condition of Project effectiveness. In the long term, the PCIU aims to serve as a single 
coordination and management unit for development partner financing and activities in the FMoH, to support the 
Government’s objective of increasing on-treasury resources and coordination. The PCIU will be responsible for overall 
Project coordination, implementation, and day-to-day management and monitoring of the project. This includes: (i) 
ensuring Project activities are implemented as agreed and in compliance with the World Bank technical and fiduciary 
guidelines; (ii) leading technical, operational, and fiduciary functions, especially contracting and contract management; 
and (iii) coordinating and overseeing Project implementation/ management in the respective FMS, including monitoring 
and evaluation of Project activities, capacity building, and ensuring compliance to social and environmental safeguard 
requirements. The PCIU will also prepare the annual work plan and budget for the Project, by no later than November 1 
of each year, commencing on November 1, 2021. 
 
57. 
Project Management Team at FMS-MoH. Each FMS will enter into a subsidiary agreement with the FGS to ensure 
Project implementation and monitoring at the designated FMS-MoH level, and secure the FGS-FMS collaboration under 
the Project.  Each FMS will form a PMT at the FMS-MoH. The PMT will be primarily responsible for Project management 
at FMS-level, including managing and tracking implementation progress, identifying opportunities for implementation 
improvements and solving day-to-day issues that may delay implementation.  Key responsibilities of the PMT include 
contract management of EPHS service providers, reviewing Project activity design, technically supporting implementation 
agencies, Project M&E, and coordinating efforts within their respective governments, across other World Bank-financed 
projects in the health sector, as well as between the FGS and the FMS.

for submission to the World Bank for No Objection within two months of Project effectiveness. The POM will describe the 
Project components and activities; implementation modalities for each Project component; fiduciary/disbursement and 
environmental and social safeguard responsibilities and arrangements; and coordination mechanisms at different levels.
