IDA PROGRAM DOCUMENT FOR A PROPOSED GRANT TO THE FEDERAL REPUBLIC OF SOMALIA 
 
1. INTRODUCTION AND COUNTRY CONTEXT 
 
1. 
This Program Document proposes the Second Inclusive Growth Development Policy Financing 
(DPF) for US$75 million in a programmatic series of two to support inclusive growth in the Federal Republic 
of Somalia. The proposed operation has two pillar objectives. The pillar 1 objective is to strengthen 
intergovernmental fiscal relations and transparency, which is critical for enhancing stability in Somalia’s 
context of fragility, conflict and violence (FCV) through building trust between the Federal Government of 
Somalia (FGS) and the Federal Member States (FMS). Improved stability can increase certainty for the private 
sector, help to renew the social contract, and raise revenues. The pillar 2 objective is to enhance economic 
resilience and investment through implementing structural reforms to create jobs, support private sector-
led growth, and increase the coping mechanisms of the poor. The reforms supported in this operation are 
anchored in the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) Initiative, through which Somalia is seeking debt 
relief.1 The first Development Policy Operation (DPO) grant in this series was approved by the World Bank’s 
Board of Executive Directors in July 2022.  
 
2. 
Somalia continues to face multiple and overlapping shocks, which affect economic growth and 
contribute to widespread poverty. Recurrent episodes of drought and floods, and other crises, like the 
COVID-19 pandemic and domestic terrorist incidents have contributed to negative per capita growth rates. 
Between 2017–22, economic growth rates averaged only 2.2 percent, compared with a population growth 
rate of 2.9 percent. Only one third of men and 12 percent of women are actively engaged in the labor market. 
In 2022, an estimated 55 percent of the Somali population lived below the national poverty line of US$741 
per person per year.2  
 
3. 
The Somali government is establishing the foundations for inclusive growth through implementing 
an ambitious reform program. The Somali government has strong ownership of the debt relief process as 
demonstrated by the implementation of reforms that are supported by this proposed operation and HIPC 
Completion Point triggers (see annex 6). Strengthening state and institution building is a critical foundation 
for economic growth in Somalia’s FCV context. Reforms supported under pillar 1 aim to reduce fragmentation 
through the harmonization of customs administrations and enhance accountability of public resources to 
build trust between the government and citizens. Reforms under pillar 2 strengthen the prospects for sectors 
that can be drivers of growth such as fisheries and renewable energy through establishing the rules of the 
game in a context of high risk and uncertainty.3 Developing digital identification and investment promotion 
can facilitate access to international finance through establishing the building blocks for correspondent 
banking relationships, which is critical given the country’s vast investment needs.  
 
4. 
The reform program supported by this operation is helping the country adapt to climate change

5. 
The reforms supported in this proposed operation complement the World Bank Group’s (WBG’s) 
engagement in Somalia. The Somalia Recurrent Cost and Reform Financing (RCRF) Project – Phase 3 
(P177900) supports intergovernmental fiscal cooperation. The Somalia Enhancing Public Resource 
Management Project (SERP, P177298) was approved in December 2022 and will strengthen domestic 
revenue mobilization and PFM arrangements. Support to financial inclusion and the Anti-Money 
Laundering/Combatting of Financing for Terrorism (AML/CFT) agenda is being provided under the Somalia 
Capacity Advancement, Livelihoods and Entrepreneurship, through Digital Uplift Project (SCALED-UP, 
P168115). To support the fisheries sector, the Somali Sustainable Fisheries and Blue Economy Development 
Project (P178032) is under preparation for delivery in FY24. The Somali Electricity Sector Recovery Project 
(P173088) is supporting new investments in the electricity sector. Somalia’s response to climate-related 
crises is being strengthened through the Somalia Crisis Recovery Project (P173315) and the Baxnaano cash 
transfer program, with the support of the Somalia Shock Responsive Safety Net for Human Capital Project 
(P178730). Barwaaqo - Somalia Water for Rural Resilience Project (P167826) is newly approved and will 
develop water, agriculture, and environmental services for rural communities in Somalia’s drylands. The 
COVID-19 Emergency Vaccination Project (P176956) is supporting the deployment of vaccinations. 
Investments in human capital are being supported through the Improving Healthcare Services in Somalia 
Project (P172031) and the Somalia Education for Human Capital Development Project (P172434). The 
reforms in this proposed operation complement the WBG’s ongoing assistance. Furthermore, this operation 
aligns with WBG corporate commitments by delivering climate co-benefits, reducing gender gaps, 
maximizing finance for development, and supporting Somalia’s transition from its FCV status. 
 
2. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK 
 
2.1. RECENT ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENTS 
6. 
The Somali economy continues to be affected by multiple sources of shocks. Economic growth was 
estimated at 1.7 percent in 2022, which is a downgrade by 0.5 percentage points compared with projections 
six months prior. Economic recovery after the COVID-19 pandemic has been affected by other global and 
regional shocks such as a severe drought. From the demand side, the main driver of growth is private

7. 
Consumer prices have been moderating in the first quarter of 2023 following earlier spikes in 
inflation in 2022. Somalia’s economy is de facto dollarized, which in times of low and stable global 
commodity prices can be a source of stability. In the Banadir region, home to the capital city Mogadishu, 
inflation averaged 6.8 per cent in 2022, mostly due to higher food and fuel prices. Commodity prices have 
been on an upward trend since July 2021 due to bottlenecks in supply chains. The start of 2023 has seen 
inflation moderate to 5.4 percent. Inflation is projected to average 4.2 percent in 2023. Domestic agricultural 
production capacity is limited, as enabling infrastructure such as irrigation, energy and roads was largely 
destroyed during the war. Drought conditions have further reduced the production of local crops, 
constraining the potential for finding viable food substitutes.

8. 
Somalia runs a chronically high structural trade deficit, which is financed by grants and remittances. 
Somalia is reliant on imports for major products such as food and fuel. Imports were estimated at 96 percent 
of GDP in 2022. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine contributed to higher costs of imported items in 2022. Unlike 
the severe drought of 2016/17, the volume of humanitarian food imports has reduced, while prices have 
increased, as fundraising efforts to support the current drought have been less effective in a context of 
multiple global shocks and competing demands for official development assistance (ODA). Somalia’s export 
goods basket is concentrated in livestock to Gulf Cooperation Council countries, and total exports in 2022 
are estimated at 17 percent of GDP. Drought conditions have reduced the quality of livestock for export. The 
trade deficit is financed by external flows, including grants, remittances, and foreign direct investment (FDI).  
9. 
The CBS is focused on developing its regulatory capacity to ensure stable growth of the financial 
sector. Financial intermediation is weak. Less than nine percent of the population has a bank account.

10. 
The CBS is strengthening the AML/CFT framework. Somalia is isolated from the global financial system 
due to money laundering and terrorism financing concerns, which have resulted in tenuous correspondent 
banking relationships that adversely affect access to finance. The National Risk Assessment (NRA), which was 
published in May 2022, identified the lack of a verifiable source of identification as an important risk, as this 
constrains the availability of know-your-customer (KYC) protocols.9 The issuance of digital identification, 
which is planned in 2024, is expected to improve the ability of financial institutions to assess risks, and 
eventually increase access to finance. The development of credit and collateral registries is expected to

11. 
The FGS is maintaining broad-based fiscal stability. The safeguarding of fiscal sustainability is being 
supported through IDA’s Sustainable Development Finance Policy (SDFP). Somalia has two approved FY23 
performance and policy actions (PPAs), which are to adhere to a zero non-concessional borrowing limit and 
for the Ministry of Finance to issue regulations on the Customs Reference Values and Customs Declarations. 
These PPAs have been achieved and are supported by this proposed operation. In addition, various fiscal 
targets have been established under the IMF’s Extended Credit Facility (ECF) arrangement, which are being 
adhered to. The FGS’ core expenditures are estimated to exceed domestic revenue collection by 
approximately US$10-12 million each month, which is around half of the average monthly revenue collection. 
Thus, the FGS is dependent on external financing, through budget support grants to maintain a balanced 
budget. In 2022, IDA, the European Union (EU), and Türkiye disbursed budget support. In June 2023, the EU 
again disbursed budget support. In 2023, a small deficit of 0.5 percent of GDP is expected.

Public expenditures are dominated by current spending, as Somalia tries to establish basic functions 
of the state. As part of continuous efforts to maintain a balanced budget, in 2022 expenditures on the wage 
bill and compensation of employees were lower than the amounts projected in the 2022 budget. Social 
expenditures accounted for 27 percent of total spending in 2022, including cash transfers administered under 
the Baxnaano social safety net program, financed by IDA grants that use country systems. Intergovernmental 
transfers to the FMS accounted for 15.2 percent of expenditures in 2022, reflecting in part the sharing of the 
grant from the first operation in this series. By the end of first quarter of 2023, the FGS had spent US$84 
million on core operational costs, which is 16 percent of the amount in the 2023 the Appropriation Act. The 
FGS and the FMS are improving the transparency of public expenditures through the quarterly publication of 
consolidated government accounts. Over time, and as fiscal space allows, the FGS is increasing expenditures 
for development priorities in the ninth National Development Plan (NDP9), which includes higher allocations 
to the education sector for example.    
 
14. 
The FGS is strengthening its debt management capacity. The Ministry of Finance established a Debt 
Management Unit (DMU) in December 2015. The African Development Bank (AfDB) financed the installation 
of a debt recording system and trained staff, primarily to support the reconstruction of loan records. The 
debt recording system is being upgraded to the Commonwealth Meridien System, which is a cloud-based IT 
system that will allow for an in-depth review of the existing portfolio. The DMU has been issuing quarterly 
debt bulletins since the end of 2020. Coordination between the DMU and other departments is improving. 
For the preparation of 2022 budget, the DMU prepared an information annex on debt, to enhance debt 
transparency, which was a PPA in 2022. The 2023 Budget Policy Framework paper also included a section on 
debt statistics to enhance transparency. A joint IMF-WB technical assistance mission which was finalized at 
the end of 2021 helped to establish a roadmap for strengthening debt management capacity, focusing on 
the legal framework, the institutional framework, as well as debt recording, monitoring, and reporting.

2.2. MACROECONOMIC OUTLOOK AND DEBT SUSTAINABILITY 
 
16. 
The economy is expected to make a modest recovery in 2023. The economy is projected to grow by 
2.8 percent in 2023, which is still lower than the population growth rate of 2.9 percent. Growth would have 
been stronger were it not for the worsening drought conditions. Growth projections in 2023 are anchored in 
the prospect of Somalia continuing to make progress in reaching the HIPC Completion Point. The enactment 
of new legislation in sectors such as fisheries, electricity, digital identification, and investor protection, 
supported by this operation is expected to encourage new investments to support growth and job creation, 
although these may take time to materialize given Somalia’s high-risk environment. The agriculture sector is 
likely to remain vulnerable to climate-related shocks, as efforts to strengthen climate resilience through 
improved water management will likely pay off over the medium-term. Over the medium-term, economic 
growth is projected to be between 3.7–4.3 percent. However, unanticipated shocks could slowdown 
economic growth, while an improvement in political conditions and intergovernmental cooperation could 
reduce risks for investors and support the country’s development prospects.  
17. 
The current account deficit is projected at 16.4 percent of GDP in 2023, following declining exports. 
Projections of the current account deficit have worsened as drought conditions have prolonged, which has 
affected livestock. Over the medium term, an improvement in access to affordable and renewable energy 
supply could support the establishment of a cold chain which can help develop new products for export, 
including seafood, as well as upgrade existing agricultural value chains. It is expected that Somalia will remain 
heavily import-dependent over the medium-term, as it will take time to rebuild the economy’s productive 
capacity. Official grants and private remittances will continue to finance most of the trade deficit. 
18. 
Inflation is projected to moderate in 2023 and stabilize over the medium-term. In 2023, inflation is 
projected at 3.8 percent, reflecting a moderation of global commodity prices. Over the medium-term, prices 
are projected to decline in line with historical trends, provided the supply of international products improve. 
De facto dollarization should continue to provide relative price stability, given dependence on imports.  
19.

advance AML/CFT reforms to support Somalia’s integration with the global financial sector. Over the 
medium-term, the CBS has intentions to issue legitimate Somali shillings, which would require securing 
agreement for a national currency from all FMS as well as identifying financing for this initiative. Several 
regulatory interventions are ongoing to provide the institutional basis for regulating and formalizing the 
financial sector, including the National Payment Systems Law, the Insurance Bill, an updated Financial

2.3. IMF RELATIONS 
27. 
Upon reaching the HIPC Decision Point in March 2020, Somalia entered a three-year ECF 
arrangement with the IMF. The program provided financing in March 2020 to facilitate clearance of

mobilization, wage bill controls, the financial sector, and central bank operations. Five ECF reviews have been

3. GOVERNMENT PROGRAM 
28. 
Somalia’s poverty reduction strategy, the NDP9, was approved in September 2019. The NDP9 is a 
nationally owned strategy for poverty reduction and inclusive growth. It builds on the progress made and 
lessons learned from 2017–19 NDP, Somalia’s eight development plan, and is the first to be crafted by the 
central government since 1986. Underpinning the NDP9 is an in-depth analysis of the drivers of poverty, 
fragility, insecurity and how resilience can be strengthened. The NDP9 was developed through extensive 
consultations with civil society, private sector representatives, FGS and FMS line ministries, national and state 
parliamentarians, members of the judiciary, and development partners. A summary of the consultative 
process is included in the NDP9 document.  
29. 
 The NDP9 presents a comprehensive strategy for development that emphasizes inclusive growth 
and poverty reduction. There are four pillars. Pillar 1 is Inclusive Politics which focuses on deepening the 
federal system, ratifying the constitution, building trust, and the delivery of fair and credible elections. Pillar 
2 is Improved Security and the Rule of Law, which aims to establish a unified, capable, accountable, and rights-
based Somali federal security institutions, as well as securing and improving access to justice. Pillar 3 is 
Economic Development, which aims to create an enabling environment for economic growth through 
regulatory changes, strengthening economic infrastructure, diversifying the private sector, and sustainably 
developing natural resources. Pillar 4 is Social and Human Development, which seeks to improve health and 
education outcomes to strengthen resilience and reduce poverty. The Somali Ministry of Planning is building 
its monitoring and evaluation capabilities and issued an Annual Progress Report on the implementation of 
NDP9 in 2020 in April 2022.

Pillar 1: Strengthening intergovernmental fiscal relations and transparency 
33. 
This pillar supports actions to strengthen intergovernmental cooperation and transparency to 
enhance state stabilization, which is a critical foundation for economic growth. Somalia’s Provisional 
Constitution of 2012 set the basis for a federal system, but did not define how resources from inland taxes, 
customs, and natural resources would be shared amongst the FGS and the FMS. It also left unspecified the 
distribution and sharing of several major functions and personnel. Decisions on how resources will be shared 
and governed, alongside the allocation of functions among the regions are under discussion, as part of the 
broader dialogue on state building. 
 
Policy area: Harmonization of customs rates and valuation across Somalia 
Prior Action #1: With the ultimate goal of harmonizing the customs regimes at the federal and state levels 
through the consistent and transparent valuation of imported goods to support an increase in revenues, the 
Ministry of Finance issued Regulations on Customs Reference Values and Customs Declarations. 
 
34. 
Rationale. Somalia’s emerging federal system is characterized by inequities in access to resources, 
which lowers trust and reflects the country’s fragmentation. Trade-related taxes, collected by states with 
major ports (Mogadishu, Kismayo, and Bosaso), account for almost half of all domestically mobilized 
revenues, yet states with major ports, in addition to the FGS for the port of Mogadishu, collect and administer 
their own duties. This has had several adverse effects. There are reduced incentives to share trade-related 
information between the FGS and FMS. The private sector must navigate different procedures in each state 
and faces higher transaction costs. Applying different duties at the ports could result in competition between 
the FGS and FMS to increase trade flows, which can have adverse consequences for state revenues. As part 
of the reforms supported by DPO1, an inter-ministerial agreement was reached in 2020 to harmonize import 
tariff rates at the Mogadishu, Kismayo, and Bosaso ports. Subsequently, the FGS submitted the harmonized 
National Tariff Schedule to parliament as part of the 2022 Budget Appropriation package, which was also an 
IMF structural benchmark. To increase domestically mobilized revenues, the FGS raised rates in May 2023, 
which need to be harmonized with the FMS for the HIPC Completion Point trigger to be met.  
 
35. 
Substance of the reform. To continue the process of harmonization of import tariffs supported by 
DPO1, the FGS issued regulations to establish the policy basis for common customs valuation to ensure that 
goods are valued in a consistent manner across the ports. Furthermore, regulations for customs declaration 
will be issued to support compliance of the harmonization of import tariffs. The issuance of these regulations 
are critical steps for the implementation of ad-valorem tariffs across all the major ports. As a next step, the 
FGS and the FMS are expected to agree on the valuation tables, which will be entered in the Somali Customs 
Administration System (SOMCAS).  
 
36. 
Expected outcome. Key benefits of harmonized customs tariffs include improved ease of compliance

Policy area: Modernization of the audit framework and external audit of intergovernmental grants 
Prior Action #2: To ensure the independent oversight of the use of public resources at the federal and state 
level, including for intergovernmental transfers, the Recipient has submitted the Audit Bill to the Parliament 
that specifies the checks and balances for appointing and dismissing the Auditor General and delineates the 
responsibilities of federal and state auditors general related to special audits of grant transfers. 
 
37. 
Rationale. While all FMSs have passed their own Audit Acts that meet the supreme audit institution 
(SAI) standards, the FGS external audit function is governed by an outdated Magistrates of Accounts Act No. 
34 of 1972 with limited provisions regarding independence of the auditor general. Legislation to replace it 
has been pending. Moreover, modalities are not in place to govern the external audit of intergovernmental 
transfers, raising concerns over the effective use of public resources and there has been limited cooperation 
between federal and state auditors general. Furthermore, the dismissal of the Auditor General in February 
2023 without a clear process raised concern over the independence of the external audit function and 
security of tenure for the person holding the position. The obsolete external audit framework raises concerns 
about the overall safeguards on transparency and accountability of public spending in Somalia, and the 
governance environment more broadly.  
 
38. 
Substance of the reform. The FGS Cabinet submitted an Audit Bill to parliament at the end of June 
2023. Once enacted, the Audit Law is expected to define the division of labor between FGS and FMS audit 
functions for the special audits of grant transfers, introduce appropriate procedures for the appointment and 
dismissal of the Auditor General, and provide security of tenure to the Auditor General in discharging his or 
her duties. In DPO1, the trigger was for the Finance Ministers’ Fiscal Forum to issue a directive to the Office 
of the Auditor General to audit health and education expenditures for the FGS and the FMS, which was 
achieved at the end of 2022. The updated prior action was introduced to strengthen the institutional 
framework for the independence of external audit function through the submission of the Audit Bill to 
parliament. A commitment from the highest offices has been obtained to prioritize the reading of an Audit 
Bill that conforms with good international practices as outlined in the Mexico declaration on SAI 
independence, which states that Heads of SAI should be appointed, reappointed, or removed by a process 
that ensures their independence from the Executive. The commitment to prioritize the adoption of the Audit 
Bill as a critical measure for strengthening financial integrity was communicated in a directive from the Office 
of the Prime Minister issued in May 2023.

Pillar 2. Enhancing economic resilience and investment 
 
40. 
Addressing Somalia’s susceptibility to shocks will require enhancing resilience and diversifying 
sources of livelihoods through advancing structural reforms. Somalia is highly exposed to climate-related 
shocks that contribute to internal displacement and rapid urbanization. For example, between April and June 
2023, 6.5 million people in Somalia are expected to face crisis or acute food insecurity outcomes. Climate-
related shocks disproportionately impact poor and vulnerable households. Adaptive safety nets are critical 
for enhancing the resilience of poor and vulnerable households, particularly for women who are more likely 
to develop negative coping mechanisms. Safety net interventions also promote and protect human capital 
contributing to inclusive development. While agriculture and livestock are important for the rural economy 
and exports, the sector is exposed to climatic shocks, which can lead to a loss of livelihoods, food insecurity 
and loss of export earnings. Sustainable and resilient development of sectors that provide alternative sources 
of livelihoods, such as fisheries for example, can promote job creation and nutrition. Establishing new sources 
of growth and job creation will require advancing structural reforms, such as access to affordable electricity 
to support the development of a cold chain, which can leverage Somalia’s substantial potential in renewable 
energy. Gaining access to finance and investment can also support the development of new sources of 
growth, which requires advancing AML/CFT reforms and improving the business environment.  
 
Policy area: Shock-mitigating social safety nets 
Prior Action #3: To support the transfer of data of applicants from a third party to the government’s unified 
social registry, the Recipient has enacted the Data Protection and Data Privacy Act which establishes 
principles governing the processing of personal data, the rights of a data subject, provisions for data security 
and enforcement mechanisms. 
 
41. 
Rationale. Somalia’s social protection system is anchored in the government’s Baxnaano shock-
responsive safety net program and the unified social registry (USR). The Baxnaano program supports the 
chronic poor and vulnerable in times of crises, including climate related shocks, with priority given to female-
headed households, as women are particularly vulnerable to shocks. The USR applies a household welfare 
scoring system, which considers socioeconomic characteristics and includes location-specific data so that 
households affected by climatic shocks can receive assistance in a timely way, which was supported by DPO1. 
The Baxnaano program and the USR are currently implemented by third party agencies, which results in

42. 
Substance of the prior action. The Data Protection and Data Privacy Act was enacted in March 2023. 
There are five objectives of the Act, which are to protect data subjects from risks related to the processing

The prior action is expected to reduce gender gaps, as women and girls in Somalia have been 
disproportionately impacted by multiple crises and food insecurity. Shocks such as rises in food prices and

Policy area: Fisheries management 
Prior Action #5: To support the sustainable development of the Somali fisheries sector, the Ministry of 
Fisheries and Blue Economy has issued the Federal Fisheries Act’s implementing regulations, establishing the 
procedures for licensing, monitoring, controlling and surveilling fishing vessels in the Somali Exclusive 
Economic Zone.  
 
49. 
Rationale. The Somali fisheries sector is of strategic importance to the country and, if well developed, 
could become an important source of jobs, income, and economic activity. Fisheries could support diversified 
sources of nutrition and improved food security, expand Somalia’s export basket, and provide new sources 
of livelihoods for rural communities beyond traditional agricultural activities which are often exposed to 
climate hazards. Realizing the potential of the fisheries sector requires establishing a formal governance 
framework to allow for the sustainable exploitation of marine resources, including by foreign fishing vessels 
operating under offshore licenses issued by the Ministry of Fisheries and Blue Economy (MFBE) of the FGS. 
The institutional environment needs to be established to protect marine resources and their habitats, to 
avoid illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing, conflicts between foreign and domestic fishers and 
unsustainable exploitation of some resources, which can have negative impacts on the marine ecosystem.  
 
50. 
Substance of the prior action. The enactment of the Federal Fisheries Act in March 2023, supported 
by DPO1, presented an important step in establishing the governance framework. In April 2023, the MFBE 
issued implementing regulations the Federal Fisheries Act on Licensing, Monitoring, Control and Surveillance 
Regulation No.01/2023 for the Exclusive Economic Zone and Somali Registered Fishing Vessels Operating in 
Areas beyond National Jurisdiction. The regulations frame the scope of activities in the Fisheries Exclusive 
Economic Zone with clear terms and conditions. The Fisheries Exclusive Economic Zone is part of the Exclusive 
Economic Zone that is adjacent to the Fisheries Protection Zone and extends seawards to 200 nautical miles. 
These legislative and regulatory reforms to the fisheries sector build on earlier efforts to strengthen

developments in the fisheries sector are expected to be implemented over the medium-term. 
 
52. 
Improved fisheries management is expected to contribute to climate adaptation and mitigation.27 
Protecting healthy fish stocks and the critical marine habitats on which they rely enhances the resilience of 
marine resources to the effects of increasing sea temperatures and acidity; and improves carbon 
sequestration in marine and coastal habitats. The creation of specific maritime zones in the Federal Fisheries 
Act and implementing regulations contributes to better conservation and healthier ecosystem conservation. 
Improved fisheries management supports climate mitigation through reduced habitat degradation, therefore 
increasing the potential of the marine ecosystem to store carbon. The prohibition of trawling reduces impacts 
on the sea floor and preserves the integrity of the ecosystem, reduces GHG emissions by the fishing trawlers 
which is a high fuel consumption fishing method, and lowers bycatch and fish waste to reduce carbon 
emissions from decomposing biomass. In addition, the prohibition of large-scale fishing vessels within 24 
nautical miles can also potentially lower GHG emissions as smaller vessels are active in coastal areas. 
Furthermore, improved fisheries management supports adaptation to climate change by encouraging fishing 
activities towards stocks that are not overfished, supporting post-harvest activities that provide a buffer to 
coastal communities, and providing alternative livelihoods to coastal communities.

Reforms supported in pillar 2 are also expected to support growth and formal wage employment. 
PA#5 on the Somali fisheries sector is expected to be positive. The Federal Act on Fisheries prevents large, 
foreign-owned vessels to fish close to the shore and compete with artisanal fisheries, thereby protecting 
income and livelihood of small-scale fishers. The domestic marine fishery subsector is currently modest in 
size, with an estimated 20,000 fishers engaged in the sector, although there is substantial potential for the 
sector to grow with enabling infrastructure in place and improved governance arrangements. Greater 
availability of fish for domestic consumption could help reduce food insecurity and provide an alternative 
source of nutrition, given that fish consumption in Somalia remains one of the lowest in Africa.29 PA#6 to 
establish an institutional framework for the electricity sector is expected to increase transparency over how 
tariffs are determined, which can help to increase investments and lower costs. Results from a pilot program 
in Somaliland indicates that ESPs have been able to reduce the consumer tariffs by about 34 percent.30 PA#7 
on the Investors and Investments Protection Act is expected to be distribution neutral in the short run. In 
the medium and long run, this action expected to have positive impact through the development of the 
formal private sector and the wage employment.

and ministries in charge of the environment from the various Federal Member States, with a view of sharing 
responsibilities for managing environmental issues, including licensing procedures for development projects.

5.3. PFM, DISBURSEMENT AND AUDITING ASPECTS 
 
69. 
The FGS has been progressively rebuilding its core PFM systems, including transparency of its fiscal 
reports.  The FGS has been building a modern legal framework (Public Procurement and Concessions Act 
2016, PFM Act 2019). Budget documentation and the structure of the budget covers the basic elements to 
meet minimum levels of transparency despite delays during election years. The implementation of the 
Somalia Financial Management Information system (SFMIS) has enabled the government to produce financial 
reports covering all expenses in the budget, quarterly fiscal reports, and International Public Sector 
Accounting Standards (IPSAS) compliant annual financial statements from the system. The government 
continues to take significant steps towards strengthening cash management, operationalizing a Treasury 
Single Account (TSA), consolidating all bank balances of ministries, departments, and agencies (MDAs) via 
the SFMIS. These initiatives have significantly reduced cash advances to MDAs for non-salary expenses. The 
transparency of fiscal reports has substantially improved. The FGS has published audited financial statements 
for 2019 and 2020, which is also a HIPC Completion Point trigger. In 2021, as part of the Budget Policy 
Framework Paper, the FGS published an aggregated budget for the first time, presenting budgets of the FGS, 
five FMS and the Banadir Regional Administration (BRA), which remain continuously available on the FGS 
Ministry of Finance external website.32  
 
70. 
Efforts have been made to improve the legal framework for public procurement, but much work 
remains to be done on its operationalization and promoting understanding of the new law among MDAs.

The CBS is making significant reforms to improve governance. The IMF concluded a safeguards

5.4. MONITORING, EVALUATION AND ACCOUNTABILITY 
 
75. 
The Ministry of Finance has the lead responsibility for the coordination, monitoring, and ensuring 
ultimate completion of the prior actions. Monitoring results from the proposed operation will build on 
mechanisms that the ministry has developed for managing the IMF ECF program and the HIPC Completion 
Point commitments. Given capacity constraints, prior actions for the proposed operation were selected to 
complement the ongoing support provided under World Bank, IMF and EU supported projects to minimize 
the additional workload associated with monitoring the actions in this proposed operation.

Grievance Redress. Communities and individuals who believe that they are adversely affected by 
specific country policies supported as Prior Actions or tranche release conditions under a World Bank 
Development Policy Financing may submit complaints to the responsible country authorities, appropriate 
local/national grievance mechanisms, or the Bank’s Grievance Redress Service (GRS). The GRS ensures that 
complaints received are promptly reviewed in order to address pertinent concerns. Project affected 
communities and individuals may submit their complaint to the Bank’s independent Accountability 
Mechanism (AM). The AM houses the Inspection Panel, which determines whether harm occurred, or could 
occur, as a result of Bank non-compliance with its policies and procedures, and the Dispute Resolution 
Service, which provides communities and borrowers with the opportunity to address complaints through 
dispute resolution. Complaints may be submitted at any time after concerns have been brought directly to 
the World Bank’s attention, and Bank Management has been given an opportunity to respond. For 
information on how to submit complaints to the World Bank’s corporate Grievance Redress Service (GRS), 
please visit http://www.worldbank.org/GRS. For information on how to submit complaints to the Bank’s 
Accountability Mechanism, please visit https://accountability.worldbank.org.
