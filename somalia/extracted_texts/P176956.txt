I. 
PROGRAM CONTEXT 
A. MPA Program Context 
1. 
This Project Appraisal Document (PAD) describes the proposed support to Somalia to expand affordable and 
equitable access to COVID-19 vaccines under the COVID-19 Strategic Preparedness and Response Program (SPRP) using 
the Multiphase Programmatic Approach (Global COVID-19 MPA), approved by the World Bank’s Board of Executive 
Directors on April 2, 2020, and the vaccine Additional Financing (AF) to the SPRP approved on October 13, 2020 with 
an overall Program financing envelope of up to US$18 billion.  The World Bank’s Global COVID-19 MPA AF is expected 
to enable vaccination for up to 1 billion people, including in some of the poorest countries such as Somalia.  An approval 
was granted to the Republic of Somalia to benefit from the SPRP MPA. 
 
2. 
The Coronavirus Disease (COVID-19) has been spreading rapidly across the world since December 2019, 
resulting in dramatic losses in lives and livelihoods.  In March 2020, the World Health Organization (WHO) declared 
COVID-19 a public health emergency of international concern.  As of July 26, 2021, the pandemic has resulted in roughly 
194 million cases and nearly 4.2 million deaths in over 140 countries.  The pandemic has caused the largest global 
economic contraction since the Great Depression, resulting in over US$12 trillion in economic losses and driving 
millions of people into poverty.  Globally, the pandemic continues unabated with recurrent surges, emergent new 
strains that are more lethal, and slow rollout of COVID-19 vaccination programs in most low- and middle-income 
countries (LMICs).  These issues reinforce the urgency of accelerating deployment of COVID-19 vaccines in all LMICs, 
including Somalia.  There is broad based recognition that economies will not recover fully until people feel they can 
live, socialize, work, and travel with confidence.  The proposed Project to expand equitable access to safe and effective 
COVID-19 vaccines in Somalia will form part of the World Bank’s emergency health response for one of the most fragile 
countries in the world.

C. Learning Agenda 
5. 
The Project under the MPA Program will support adaptive learning throughout implementation, as well as from 
international organizations including United Nations Children’s Emergency Fund (UNICEF) and WHO.  The Global 
COVID-19 MPA aims to support the following types of activities which will also benefit Somalia through cross-country 
learning: (i) Vaccination readiness assessments: applying the Vaccine Readiness Assessment Framework (VRAF) and the 
Vaccine Introduction Readiness Assessment Tool (VIRAT) to identify key gaps and remedial actions; (ii) Risk 
communication strategy: developing strategies and tools to ensure adequate awareness of the benefits of the 
vaccination program and to address  vaccine hesitancy; and (iii) Performance monitoring: developing tools for tracking 
coverage; acceptability; and adverse effects.  Somalia will benefit from sharing experiences and early lessons with other 
countries and tailoring strategies to its unique context. 
 
II. 
CONTEXT AND RELEVANCE 
 
A. Country Context 
 
6. 
Somalia is among the poorest countries in the world with nearly 70 percent of the population living in poverty.  
The country faces multiple challenges, including an ongoing armed insurgency, and frequent natural disasters (e.g., 
droughts, floods, locust infestations), which have created humanitarian crises with millions displaced internally and 
millions more living precariously.  Poverty, fragility, and insecurity are compounded by political crises and weak 
institutional capacities, which hinder the delivery of basic services.  Fiscal capacity remains low with limited ability to 
expand domestic resource mobilization.  With a population growth rate of almost 3 percent, the number of Somalis is 
doubling every 24 years, placing higher demands on government’s limited ability to provide critical health and social 
services. 
 
7. 
Somalia has been implementing a poverty reduction strategy and putting in place reforms focused on 
macroeconomic and fiscal stability. These reforms aim to lay the foundation for inclusive economic growth and to 
address the needs of the most vulnerable groups.  In March 2020, Somalia reached the Heavily Indebted Poor Countries 
(HIPC) Initiative Decision Point, reopening access to concessional financing from the World Bank Group, including the 
Somalia Crisis and Recovery Project (SCRP) (P173315), which has a US$20.5 million component to support the COVID-
19 emergency health response and is providing assistance to train and finance female health workers in rural areas and 
to pilot behavioral nudges through virtual technologies; a recently approved US$100 million health operation 
(Improving Healthcare Services in Somalia—Damal Caafimaad) (P172031), which represents the cornerstone of the 
World Bank’s engagement in Somalia, and the proposed Project that will support  the COVID-19 vaccination program. 
 
8. 
Somalia is also striving to enhance stability through institutional and political reforms, which began with the 
adoption of a provisional constitution, and establishment of a Federal Government in 2012.  Four new Federal 
Member States (FMS) were established, resulting in a total of five under the Federal Government of Somalia (FGS).1  
Service delivery capacity of the states is constrained by several factors, including limited human resources, inadequate

9. 
The COVID-19 pandemic runs the risk of undermining the modest recent progress on economic and political 
reforms in one of the most challenging country contexts in the world.  While Somalia has reported a cumulative total 
of only 15,230 cases and roughly 800 deaths as of July 26, 2021, these figures likely underestimate the actual scope 
and magnitude of the pandemic.  The cumulative positivity rate is 5.5 percent, and the cumulative case fatality rate is 
estimated at 3.2 percent with males representing 76 percent of all cases. To tackle the pandemic, the government has: 
(i) strengthened testing capacity at the National Public Health and Reference Laboratory in Mogadishu and established 
testing capacity in Somaliland and Puntland; (ii) put in place rapid response teams; and (iii) expanded the country’s 
intensive care unit bed capacity. Since the first case was confirmed in March 2020, there has been widespread local 
transmission to all states, diverting limited resources towards the COVID-19 health response, triggering economic 
disruptions, and exacerbating long-standing vulnerabilities and disparities.  Border closures have hurt trade, livestock 
exports, and household incomes.2  Due to disruptions in economic activity stemming from lock down measures, the 
economy contracted by about 1.5 percent in 2020. The growth outlook for 2021 is projected at around 2 percent, 
compared to a pre-COVID 19 forecast of 3.2 percent.  
 
10. 
The situation appears to be worsening since February 2021, as Somalia has been experiencing a third wave 
(Figure 1) with widespread and intense community transmission in all regions, and suspected emergence of new 
variants.  The vaccination program is underway with the first 300,000 doses of the Astra Zeneca vaccines received in 
March 2021 through the Covid Vaccine Access (COVAX) Facility that is expected to fund vaccines for 20 percent of the 
population.3  Somalia has also received 200,000 doses of the Sinopharm vaccine from China and 2,500 doses of the 
Sputnik vaccine from Russia. France also donated 108,000 doses of Astra Zeneca vaccine in July 2021. To ensure 
equitable access, the allocation of the initial 300,000 doses was based on population of each federal member state 
including Somaliland. As the rollout progressed, and different federal members states had varying acceptance rates, 
there was a reallocation from low uptake areas to  Somaliland which enjoyed a higher uptake.  This process minimized 
the risk of vaccine expiration, improved absorption rates, and augurs well for future deployment of vaccines in Somalia.  
Out of the vaccines received from the COVAX Facility, nearly 80 percent was administered by early June 2021 with 
Somalia doing relatively well in comparison to other countries in the region despite its multiple constraints (Figure 2).  
Moreover, by mid-July over 90 percent had been administered.  Overall, roughly 23 percent of health workers; 43 
percent of frontline workers; and 34 percent of the elderly have been vaccinated as of mid-July.  In total, less than 1 
percent of the population has been fully vaccinated and 1.5 percent received at least one dose to date.  The majority 
of the vaccinated who received the first dose consists of males (roughly 75 percent) who constitute the bulk of the 
initial target groups (i.e., teachers, police, municipal officers and point of entry staff) and those residing in urban 
settlements (92.5 percent) with easier access to health facilities.  Only a small fraction (less than 1 percent) of people 
living in nomadic settlements and in camps for Internally Displaced Persons (IDPs) have been reached to date as mobile 
clinics and outreach campaigns have only just started.  Women face various barriers, including limited social mobility, 
and low health literacy with social mobilization efforts planned to support them to benefit from outreach activities 
during next phase.  Modelling and projections show an elevated risk of transmission, underscoring the urgency of 
accelerating the roll out of the COVID-19 vaccination program.

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
11. 
Despite modest improvements over the past 15 years, Somalia’s health indicators lag both regional and global 
averages.  Life expectancy is 56 years, maternal mortality is a staggering 692 per 100,000 live births, fertility remains 
one of the highest in the world (about 7.0 births per woman), while stunting has been rising and is currently estimated 
at 28 percent.  The burden of non-communicable diseases (NCDs) is rising and represents nearly 22 percent of Daily 
Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) lost with these diseases mostly affecting those over 50 years, who are among the highest 
at risk for COVID-19.  Women face multiple challenges, including gender-based violence (i.e. 12 percent of ever married 
women reported physical violence during the 2020 Demographic and Health Survey even though this figure is likely to 
underestimate the actual levels), and difficulties accessing services with men responsible for decisions around health 
seeking behavior.  Coverage of essential health services remains extremely low due to both supply and demand side 
constraints with only 21 percent of births occurring in health facilities; 31 percent of women receiving antenatal care; 
and a meagre 11 percent of children fully immunized. 
 
12. 
Health sector capacity is limited and concentrated in urban areas.  Public health facilities are inadequate, 
inequitably distributed and poorly equipped to provide quality care.  The availability of human resources (0.92 health 
workers/1000 people) is below the WHO’s minimum standard (2.3 health workers/1000 people).  The national supply 
chain faces ongoing challenges, but there have been some modest improvements in vaccine management, including 
storage capacity, equipment and transport, and management information (Effective Vaccine Management Assessment, 
2016).  The Government’s role in the provision of health services is limited with most services delivered by Non-
Governmental Organizations (NGOs).  As a result of both supply and demand side impediments health service 
utilization is low, particularly in the public sector, where it is estimated at 0.23 outpatient visits per person per year 
and 0.81 hospital discharges per one hundred people per year Service Availability Readiness Assessment (SARA) in 
2016.  By contrast, the private sector plays a key role in the delivery of health services in Somalia (providing roughly 60 
percent of services and delivering 70 percent of medicines), primarily in urban areas, with patients preferring these 
facilities due to the perceived higher quality and availability of drugs.  Pharmacies are one of the most accessible health 
care delivery platforms for many Somalis.  Clan structures play a major role in determining where people access care, 
highlighting the importance of involving community leaders in the COVID-19 immunization program.  Likewise, religious

13. 
Institutional capacity in the health sector is growing but remains modest.  The relatively young Federal Ministry 
of Health (FMoH) established less than a decade ago aims to play its stewardship role in a challenging context of 
managing multiple actors with limited human and financial resources.  While the FMoH has leadership structures and 
some policies and procedures in place, its decision-making processes and management systems are not fully defined, 
and capacity to manage programs independently is limited.  Reliance on external financial and technical assistance 
remains high.  The capacity to regulate the private sector and to contract NGOs is limited and governance problems 
have impeded the transparent management of the operations of the FMoH in the past.  The current Government has 
demonstrated commitment to fight corruption, as evidenced by the passage of anti-corruption legislation in September 
2019.  
 
14. 
Somalia’s Federal Member States’ (FMS) Ministry of Health (MoH) capacity is similarly weak. The capacities of 
the FMS are variable with the four recently established states (Jubaland, South West, Hirshabelle, Galmudug) having 
relatively modest capacities with few resources on-treasury (around US$100,000 a year), low budget execution rates, 
few to no civil servants, and a high dependence on partners to provide stewardship for service delivery.  Puntland and 
the self-declared independent territory of Somaliland are somewhat better off in comparison.  Puntland’s MoH is more 
experienced in managing some health programs but also remains highly dependent on partner support with civil 
servants representing only 26 percent of MoH staffing.  Somaliland has the strongest MoH capacity.  There is a need to 
further delineate the roles and responsibilities of the FGS and FMS Ministries of Health (‘functional assignment’), to 
make optimal use of the limited resources in the country.  The new US$100 million World Bank funded operation, 
Improving Healthcare Services in Somalia Project (Damal Caafimaad), will support the FMoH to strengthen its 
managerial, planning, budgeting, contracting and oversight capacities and will assist the FMS to bolster their 
capacities.4 
 
15. 
The FMoH with support of partners is enhancing readiness to scale up the COVID-19 vaccination campaign.

A National Deployment and Vaccination Plan (NDVP) for COVID-19 Vaccines (February 2021) has been 
elaborated in collaboration with key partners supporting the government, namely WHO and UNICEF (Box 1).

includes a timetable of key actions.  The NDVP discusses the phased approach to the roll out of the COVID-19

financing (e.g., COVAX Facility, Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance, World Bank).  It outlines appropriate vaccination 
delivery strategies for reaching different target groups and provides details on the deployment and 
administration of COVID-19 vaccines (e.g. cold chain and personal protective equipment (PPE) requirements; 
storage and distribution to Central Vaccine Store and sub-national stores).  The NDVP addresses human 
resource needs, including training, mentorship, and supervisory support. Finally, the NDVP includes a detailed

A COVID-19 Vaccine Survey published in June 2021 by the Somali Institute for Development Research and 
Analysis (SIDRA) found a relatively high overall level of readiness (77 percent) to accept the vaccine once 
available with some respondents citing concerns with vaccine effectiveness, and side effects. The major source 
of COVID-19 information was social mobilizers (43 percent), followed by radio (15 percent) and health workers 
(14 percent).  Another survey conducted by the Africa’s Voices Foundation in May 2021 using its multi-media 
platform (Imaqal--- Listen to me) on urban youth found that women expressed more positive attitudes towards 
vaccination (over 70 percent) than men (56 percent); and 14 percent of respondents mentioned trusting Allah, 
highlighting the importance of the religious lens for vaccination-related communication, as was done during 
the early COVID-19 prevention communication strategy.  Both studies noted the importance of developing 
better targeted solutions, including greater use of trusted sources such as religious leaders, nomadic elders, 
and influential figures who can serve as role models and expanded use of multiple platforms (e.g. radio, social 
media, women’s groups, and the Somalia Social Mobilization Network successfully used for the polio 
eradication campaigns).  A nationwide High Frequency Phone Survey conducted in January 2021, which had a 
large sample size and was nationally representative found a high acceptance rate of COVID-19 vaccines if the 
services are offered for free.  Acceptance rates were high for all groups, including females (91 percent), nomads 
(95 percent), and IDPs (86 percent).  Acceptance rates ranged from 90 percent in Hirshabelle to 94 percent in 
South West State.  Despite the positive views expressed in various surveys vaccine hesitancy was initially 
problematic and required a redoubling of efforts that will need to be sustained. 
• 
The FMoH with support of partners has developed a National Vaccine Coverage and Purchase Plan (Table 2) 
which is being continuously refined and updated.  Somali authorities aim to use a combination of strategies for 
securing sufficient vaccines and ensuring equitable access, including use of the COVAX facility and the African 
Vaccine Acquisition Trust (AVAT) convened by the African Union as well as donations from countries with 
surplus stockpiles. 
• 
Authorities have set a bold target of vaccinating 70 percent of the population (roughly 11 million people) 
through a combination of fixed and outreach sites.  Priority groups have been identified in line with the WHO 
SAGE framework (Table 3), namely: (i) health and other frontline workers; (ii) elderly (over 50 years); (iii) people 
with co-morbidities (e.g., cardiovascular diseases, hypertension and diabetes); and (iv) all remaining adults over 
18 years of age, including people with disabilities and IDPs with community health workers and community 
leaders expected to play key roles in identifying and registering difficult to reach groups.  Discussions are 
underway on strategies for ensuring equitable distribution of vaccines considering the complex political 
context.  To date, each member state was allocated a proportion of the vaccines received based on population 
size and all have now administered most doses received.  Given the variable health system capacity as well as 
the uneven uptake of the COVID-19 vaccine across member states, vaccine specific state deployment micro 
plans are being developed and will need to be continuously updated.

Coordinating mechanisms have been established, including a National Coordination Committee (NCC), a 
National Technical Working Group (NTWG), and an Incident Management Support Team (IMST) for the COVID-
19 vaccination program.  The NCC and TWG collaborate closely with the IMST which coordinates the national 
response to the pandemic, including the vaccination campaign. At the sub-national level, each state is to 
establish sub committees which will be responsible for: planning and implementation; tracking progress on the 
VIRAT and sharing information with the NCC and NTWG; identifying target groups for the vaccination program; 
assessing the cold chain needs and ensuring functionality; investigating Adverse Event Following 
Immunizations (AEFIs) in collaboration with National AEFI committee; and supporting data collections.  The 
head of the FMoH Extended Program of Immunization (EPI) is the focal point who leads vaccine deployment 
and planning in Somalia. The FMoH has been working in close collaboration with the State Ministries of Health 
to ensure broad-based ownership.  The main roles and responsibilities of the FMoH EPI program has been to 
prepare the application to the COVAX Facility; identify cold chain needs and make request to GAVI; lead 
development of operational plans; and develop and share training guidelines, recording and reporting tools, 
and guidelines on AEFI. 
• 
A National Regulatory Authority (NRA) has very recently been established with a mandate to ensure rapid 
approval of COVID-19 vaccines. 
• 
According to the NDVP, there are modalities in place to enter into indemnity and liability agreements for 
COVID-19 vaccines (Box 2).  
• 
The United Nations Mission to Somalia has established a One UN platform (UN COVAX Taskforce) to ensure the 
coordinated rollout of COVID-19 vaccines, including support for supply chain and logistics, training, 
communications, and resource mobilization.  UNICEF is providing support to monitor vaccine utilization and 
minimize the risk of wastage and to track cold chain functionality and vaccine stock levels. 
• 
Strategies to ensure accountability, traceability and security of the COVID-19 vaccines have been identified, 
including plans to deploy independent groups to facilitate third party monitoring. 
• 
A mobile telephone based electronic registration system, CommCare, was introduced to record patient 
information and to handle vaccine safety surveillance (i.e., monitoring of adverse events) and to send SMS 
reminders for second doses.  
• 
Deployment has been impeded by both demand and supply side constraints, including vaccine hesitancy fueled 
by misinformation, and accessibility and security challenges.  In the start-up phase, the focus was on urban 
areas and facility-based vaccination, and most recently this has been expanded to include outreach activities 
tailored to different contexts.  The hybrid approach (facility-based and outreach) will enable the program to 
reach in an equitable manner a larger proportion of the most vulnerable groups through multiple platforms 
and customized vaccine uptake strategies.

Box 2:  Liability and Indemnification Issues in Vaccine Acquisition 
Key Points: 
• 
The rapid development of vaccines increases manufacturers’ potential liability for AEFI. 
• 
Manufacturers want to protect themselves from this risk by including immunity from suit and liability clauses, 
indemnification provisions, and other limitation of liability clauses in their supply contracts. 
• 
Contractual provisions and domestic legal frameworks can all operate to allocate that risk among market 
participants, but no mechanism will eliminate this risk entirely. 
 
For COVAX-financed vaccines for AMC countries: 
• 
COVAX has negotiated model indemnification provisions with manufacturers for vaccines purchased and supplied 
under the COVAX AMC. 
• 
In providing vaccines through COVAX AMC, COVAX requests COVAX AMC Participants to have in place an 
indemnity agreement directly with manufacturers, and the necessary indemnity and liability frameworks for that 
purpose – either in the form of the COVAX model indemnification arrangements or prior bilateral arrangements 
with manufacturers. 
• 
The COVAX Facility will have a no-fault compensation scheme for AMC countries as part of its risk mitigation 
strategy. This will cover vaccines supplied only through COVAX AMC. 
• 
Somalia will have to consider what it will take to implement these indemnification provisions (including statutory 
implementation) and how they can avail of the benefits of the no-fault compensation scheme. 
 
For vaccines purchased outside of COVAX: 
• 
Somalia will need to enter direct indemnification arrangements with manufacturers. 
• 
Somalia does not currently have legislation in place to provide statutory immunity for manufacturers. Somalia 
does not have national no-fault compensation scheme.  The recently established NMRA is filling the gap on the 
authorization of importation, relying on recommendations of WHO EUL and other countries that have SRAs. 
• 
Adoption of any such indemnification provisions or compensation scheme would have to be in accordance with 
Somalia’s own national strategy and framework. 
 
Possible World Bank support to Somalia, depending on needs, may include: 
Information sharing on: (i) statutory frameworks in Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development countries 
and other developing countries; and (ii) overall experience in other countries; (ii) training and workshops for government 
officials to familiarize them with the issues; and (iii) provision of Hands on Enhanced Implementation Support( HEIS )for 
World Bank-financed contracts.

C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives  
16. 
The Project is aligned with World Bank Group (WBG) strategic priorities, particularly the WBG’s mission to end 
extreme poverty and boost shared prosperity.  The Project will support the cost of expanding affordable and equitable 
access to safe and effective Project COVID-19 vaccines in Somalia under the COVID-19 SPRP using the MPA, approved 
by the Board on April 2, 2020, and the vaccine Additional Financing (AF) to the SPRP approved on October 13, 2020.  
The SPRP is focused on strengthening preparedness while the AF is focused on improving access to COVID-19 vaccines.  
The Project is aligned with the World Bank’s support for national plans and global commitments to strengthen COVID-
19 vaccination.  The Project complements both WBG and development partner investments in the COVID-19 
emergency health response and broader health systems strengthening in Somalia.  The WBG remains committed to 
providing a fast and flexible response to the COVID-19 epidemic, including accelerated roll out of COVID-19 vaccination 
programs, utilizing all WBG operational and policy instruments and working in close partnership with Somali 
authorities and representatives of other agencies (e.g., COVAX Facility, African Vaccine Acquisition Trust, UNICEF, 
WHO). The Project is consistent with the World Bank Group Country Partnership Framework for Somalia (FY19-22), 
discussed by the Board on September 25, 2018 (Report No. 124734-SO), including the objective of improving the 
quality and equity of basic services. The Project will contribute primarily to the objective to “Improve delivery systems 
for more inclusive social services” (Objective 1.3) by expanding equitable access to life saving COVID-19 vaccines to 
groups at greatest risk of getting COVID-19.  The Project is also aligned with the World Bank Group Strategy for 
Fragility, Conflict, and Violence (FCV) 2020-2025 (February 2020), that underscores the importance of inclusive and 
effective social sector service delivery. To this end, the rapid scale up of the COVID-19 vaccination campaign is critical 
as part of the World Bank’s broader objective of strengthening the social contract and the legitimacy of the state and 
ultimately helping Somalia transition out of fragility over the long-term. 
 
17. 
   The proposed national COVID-19 vaccination program is in line with this vision to the extent that it proposes 
to expand access to efficacious vaccines in an equitable fashion and free of charge to all vulnerable groups, using 
multiple delivery channels (e.g. community outreach, health facilities, NGOs, private sector).  The Project is also 
consistent with the Somalia’s Ninth National Development Plan which prioritizes improving health access and 
outcomes and emphasizes collaboration and cooperation with non-state actors for health service delivery, which is 
consistent with some of the key implementation modalities proposed under the Project. 
D. Lessons Learned  
18. 
Early lessons from the COVID-19 health response in Somalia as highlighted in the NDVP, and lessons from 
other FCV countries have been incorporated into the design and rollout of the vaccination program.  Key lessons 
relate to the importance of: (i) carrying out social mobilization efforts to educate health workers and community 
members about COVID-19, including the benefits of the vaccination program to address concerns over vaccine 
hesitancy  and improve vaccine uptake; (ii) training health workers and community-based workers on the health 
response and vaccination campaign to ensure readiness to deploy vaccines; (iii) strengthening coordination between 
government agencies, implementing partners, and communities; (iv) contracting experienced organizations to support 
key activities (e.g. security, implementation support); (v) designing a multi-pronged strategy for deploying vaccines in 
a complex and diverse environment taking into account both demand and supply side impediments; (vi) protecting 
health workers to ensure their safety; and (vii) maintaining close community contact, using community and health 
facility based workers as well as volunteers.

A. Institutional and Implementation Arrangements  
32. 
Given the emergency nature of the COVID-19 vaccination campaign and the limited government capacity to 
deliver services and provide stewardship to the sector, the proposed institutional arrangements will rely primarily 
on engaging a limited number of UN agencies (i.e., UNICEF and WHO) that will work under the government’s 
technical leadership.  The added value of the United Nations agencies is four-fold.  First, these agencies will help 
reinforce Government leadership and strengthen capacity of key structures, enabling Somali authorities to assume 
greater responsibility in the medium to long-term while the Damal Caafimaad Project builds stewardship capacity at 
national and state levels, including contracting health services to non-state actors and strengthening regulatory 
functions. Second, given the mandates of the UN agencies and the pivotal role they currently play in the ongoing 
deployment of COVID-19 vaccines they are well placed to provide technical and implementation support.  The two UN 
agencies have been effectively supporting the COVID-19 vaccination rollout with the Project representing the 
continuation of reliance on well-functioning structures and systems for serving the FMoH, FMS, as well as Somaliland.  
Third, the UN agencies are well positioned to promote cross fertilization of experiences and lessons on COVID-19 
vaccination between Somalia and other countries.  Fourth, the UN agencies have established mechanisms to support 
service delivery in Somaliland where the current political impasse makes it impossible for the FGS MoH to directly 
support service delivery or channel funds from the national level.  Hence, all funds would be channeled through the 
two UN agencies which will be responsible for accounting on how funds are spent to the World Bank.  In the case of 
the FMoH, UNICEF will pay suppliers directly on behalf of the FMoH. 
 
33. 
The Project will leverage capacity of two UN agencies which have been collaborating with the Ministry of 
Health to provide continuous support for the implementation of the COVID-19 vaccination program which will be

contracted by the Government to provide technical and operational support and ensure equitable access to COVID-
19 vaccines.  Both institutions are well placed to provide technical support given their strong track record supporting 
the government’s national COVID-19 health response, and their in-depth knowledge of Somalia.  UNICEF, through a 
contract with the  FMoH and in close coordination with the FMS, will be responsible for supporting the quantification 
and forecasting of supply needs; facilitating the acquisition and distribution of vaccines, managing the procurement 
and installation of cold chain equipment, supporting the communication strategy to ensure accurate information 
about COVID-19 vaccines, strengthen vaccine demand, and bolster uptake, and conducting related training for the 
COVID-19 vaccination roll out.  UNICEF will also transfer funds to the MoH of Puntland, and Somaliland for the last 
mile distribution of vaccines.  The WHO will also pass on funds to Somaliland for supervision of vaccination sites.  WHO 
will continue working closely with the FMoH and the FMS, as well as with the MoH Somaliland, providing technical 
support for the vaccination campaign, including support to the national Immunization Technical Advisory Group for

to quality and acceptability.  These indicators will be disaggregated by sex and will consider citizen engagement.  The 
main PDO and intermediate indicators will be as follows:

• 
Proportion of vaccine doses administered against allocations, by state. 
•

• 
Number of Effective Vaccine Management (EVM) assessments completed with composite scores of 80

An AEFI system is put in place and is capable of reporting adverse effects.

 PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY 
A. Technical, Economic and Financial Analysis  
39. 
There is a strong economic case for investment in the COVID-19 vaccination and for public provision and 
financing considering the substantial health and economic benefits and given the high public good nature of 
immunization.  It is well recognized that disease outbreaks cause severe economic shocks, including loss of jobs, drops 
in productivity and economic growth; and inflict lasting damage on stocks of both human and physical capital leading 
to drops in economic productivity and slowing long-term growth (UNDP Human Development Report, 2014).  As noted 
in the Global Economic Prospects (June 2020), the ongoing pandemic has triggered a global crisis that, in addition to 
the enormous human toll, is leading to the deepest global recession since the Second World War, pushing millions 
into extreme poverty and causing trillions of dollars in economic losses.  Cumulative global losses across 2020 and 
2021 are estimated to exceed US$12 trillion.  While the full impact of the pandemic in Somalia may not yet be fully 
understood or documented the economic outlook has been worsened by the pandemic and livelihoods have been 
seriously disrupted in a country where most of the population is already living in poverty.  The successful deployment 
of COVID-19 vaccines in Somalia represents the best option for saving lives, protecting livelihoods, and restoring 
growth with benefits expected to exceed vaccine-related costs.  The effective administration of COVID-19 vaccines 
will also avoid the health care costs of additional cases of infection and protect the modest gains made in recent years 
in essential health services.   
 
40. 
The value added of the World Bank's support to Somalia is substantial.  The value added is three-fold.  First, 
World Bank financing is expected to play a major role in supporting the national program in this fragile context where 
most development partners provide humanitarian assistance and where both governments and NGOs depend heavily 
on external financing.  Second, World Bank technical assistance, working in close collaboration with other agencies 
(i.e., UNICEF, WHO) will be critical to guiding and informing the roll out of the COVID-19 vaccination program which 
requires regular information on vaccine acquisition strategies and opportunities.  Third, as noted above, Somalia’s 
participation in the MPA will provide numerous opportunities for learning and knowledge sharing with the other 
participating countries.  The learning agenda is substantial and includes a wide range of issues around vaccine 
readiness assessments, risk communications, and performance monitoring.
