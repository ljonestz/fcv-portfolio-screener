FCV Portfolio Screener  |  Methodology Validation

Can an AI score the same document consistently?

Large language models are probabilistic — ask the same question twice and you may get a different answer. Before the World Bank's AI-powered FCV screening tool can be used for formal analysis, we need to know whether its scores are reproducible. We ran the same project document through the screener ten times and measured how much the results varied.

Published March 19, 2026 Test project: P148850 — Ethiopia Expressway Development 10 independent screening runs

Every week, World Bank teams appraise projects in some of the world's most difficult operating environments — places affected by fragility, conflict and violence (FCV). Getting those projects right matters enormously. Miss a conflict risk, and a road-building programme might inadvertently inflame land grievances. Miss an elite-capture vulnerability, and aid money ends up with the wrong people.

The FCV Portfolio Screener is an AI tool designed to assess how well World Bank project documents address these risks. It reads a project's appraisal document and assigns scores across eight analytical dimensions — covering everything from whether the project diagnoses the conflict context correctly, to whether its results framework includes indicators that work in volatile environments.

But there is an obvious concern with any AI-based analytical tool: does it give the same answer twice? Unlike a human expert who can be held accountable for a judgment, a large language model introduces randomness at every step. Two runs of the same document might yield different conclusions — and if scores shift enough to change a project's overall rating or classification, the methodology loses its credibility.

This report documents the first of a planned series of stability tests. We took a single World Bank project document and fed it to the FCV screener ten times, independently, as if each run were a first-ever assessment. We then measured how much the scores varied.

The short answer: the screener is highly stable.

The test

The project chosen for this first test is P148850: the Ethiopia Expressway Development Support Project, a US$370 million infrastructure loan approved in 2015 to build a greenfield highway through the Oromia and SNNPR regions. It was selected deliberately as a floor case — a project with very low FCV integration scores, which tests whether the screener is consistent at the lower end of the scale.

Why a floor case first? Because low-scoring projects are often the most diagnostically important — they are the ones that need attention — and also because a project with near-zero FCV content offers little ambiguous evidence for the model to interpret differently across runs. If variance is low here, it builds confidence. If it is high, that would be a fundamental problem.

"The FCV cross-cutting area box is explicitly unchecked in the PAD data sheet. There is no conflict diagnostic, no political economy analysis of the corridor, and no FCV-sensitive indicators anywhere in the results framework."
— Summary judgment shared by all ten independent screening agents

Each of the ten runs used an identical copy of the extracted document text and the same screening rubric, but was executed by a completely independent AI agent with no memory of previous runs. The "RUN N OF 15" label in each prompt ensured that prompt-caching could not short-circuit the process.

Composite scores across 10 runs

The FCV screener produces two composite scores for each project: a Sensitivity score (does the project understand the FCV context?) and a Responsiveness score (does its design adapt to that context?). Both run from 1 to 10, with scores below 4 classified as Not Addressed, 4–6.9 as Partially Addressed, and 7 or above as Substantially Addressed.

Across ten runs, the Sensitivity score ranged from 2.50 to 3.00, with a mean of 2.72 and a standard deviation of 0.18. Responsiveness ranged from 2.60 to 3.20, with a mean of 2.88 and a standard deviation of 0.25. Both composites remained in the Not Addressed category in every single run — there was not a single instance where a different run produced a different rating.

Dimension-level stability

Behind the two composite scores sit eight individual dimensions, each scored 1–10. Six of the eight showed standard deviations below 0.5 — the threshold for "stable". Two — D6 (Implementation and Operational Flexibility) and D7 (Results Framework and Adaptive Management) — showed slightly higher variance, with SDs of 0.63 and 0.52 respectively. Both remain below the 1.0 threshold for "unstable".

The variance in D6 is worth examining. In most runs, the score was 3; in one run it rose to 4. This reflects a genuine ambiguity in the document: the PAD includes standard multi-donor coordination arrangements that, depending on the reader's interpretation, could be credited as a weak form of operational flexibility. A human reviewer would face the same interpretive choice. The fact that the AI lands on one side of this judgment roughly 80% of the time, and on the other 20%, is not a flaw — it is an honest signal about the evidence.

D5 (Design and Targeting) showed zero variance — every single run returned a score of exactly 3. This is the tightest possible result: when the evidence is unambiguous, the model is completely consistent.

Red flag consistency

In addition to scores, the FCV screener checks for five binary red flags — specific design failures that warrant direct attention regardless of a project's overall score. These are: unmitigated conflict risk (RF1), missing distributional analysis (RF2), weak handling of engagement with non-constitutional authorities (RF3), unmitigated elite capture risk (RF4), and an unrealistic macro framework (RF5).

Four of the five flags were perfectly consistent across all ten runs. RF5 — the macro framework flag — triggered in every single run without exception. The PAD assumes 15% annual traffic growth over 20 years with no stress-testing against political or conflict scenarios, a gap too obvious for the model to miss regardless of how the rest of the document is read.

RF2 — the distributional analysis flag — showed one flip: nine runs left it clear, one run (run 7) triggered it. This reflects a genuine interpretive edge: the PAD mentions resettlement and business displacement, but these are handled through standard safeguards instruments rather than a conflict-sensitivity lens. Whether this constitutes a "missing distributional analysis" depends on how strictly the flag is applied. A flip rate of 10% — exactly at our stability threshold — suggests the model is capturing genuine ambiguity rather than producing noise.

What this means

The scores are trustworthy for classification purposes

The most important finding is not the variance numbers themselves — it is that no run produced a different rating or a different gap matrix classification. All ten runs placed this project in the same quadrant ("Low FCV integration") and gave it the same categorical verdict ("Not Addressed" on both composites). The practical implication for portfolio analysis is that a single screening run is sufficient to classify a project reliably.

Low-scoring projects are the most stable

This makes intuitive sense: a project with essentially no FCV content offers little ambiguous evidence for an AI model to weigh differently across runs. The harder stability test will come with a project that sits near the classification thresholds — where a shift of 0.5 in a composite score could change a "Partially Addressed" to a "Substantially Addressed", or flip a project from one quadrant of the gap matrix to another. That test is planned next.

Two dimensions warrant monitoring

D6 (Implementation Flexibility) and D7 (Results Framework) showed marginally higher variance than the rest. Both are dimensions where assessors — human or AI — face genuine interpretive challenges: the line between "standard project management" and "FCV-adapted flexibility" is not always bright. These findings suggest that the scoring rubric for these two dimensions may benefit from tighter calibration guidance before the methodology is used for formal cross-country comparisons.

The RF2 edge case is analytically useful

The single RF2 flip is not a problem to be fixed — it is a signal worth noting. The PAD does have distributional content (resettlement, business displacement) that is handled through safeguards compliance rather than conflict-sensitivity. Whether that counts as a "missing analysis" is a legitimate interpretive question, and the 10% flip rate reflects exactly how marginal the evidence is. If this project were being reviewed in a formal process, an assessor would note the ambiguity and exercise judgment. The AI's behaviour here is doing the same thing.

How the FCV screener works
The FCV Sensitivity and Responsiveness Screener reads the key sections of a World Bank project appraisal document and scores it across eight dimensions grouped into two composites. Sensitivity (dimensions D1–D3) measures how well the project diagnoses and understands the FCV context. Responsiveness (dimensions D4–D8) measures how well the design, targeting, implementation arrangements and results framework adapt to that context. Each dimension is scored 1–10 using a detailed rubric, with five binary red flags applied on top. The methodology is designed to be replicable and transparent — scores derive from evidence in the document, not from background assumptions about the country.

Stability test design
Each of the ten runs was executed by an independent AI agent with no access to previous results. The test used P148850 as a "floor case" (very low FCV scores, limited interpretive ambiguity) to establish a baseline. Stability thresholds: composite SD ≤ 0.5 is Stable; 0.5–1.0 is Marginal; >1.0 is Unstable. Zero categorical rating changes and zero gap matrix flips are required for full stability clearance.

What comes next

This test covers only one project and one type of stability challenge (floor stability). Four further projects have been selected to complete the test:

  • P148586 (Djibouti) — low scorer, floor stability confirmation
  • P162901 (Djibouti) — high scorer, ceiling stability
  • P166220 (Djibouti) — the critical case: scores of 5.83 and 5.50, both directly at the classification thresholds
  • P177233 (Ethiopia) — very high FCV-integrated project, ceiling stability

P166220 is the most analytically important. A standard deviation of 0.5 on either composite could cause it to flip between two different gap matrix quadrants — meaning the AI could classify the same project as either "Responsive but underanalysed" or "Partially integrated" depending on the run. If that test also passes, the methodology is robust across the full score range.

Results from all five projects will be compiled into a formal methodology note validating the screener for use in OPCS processes, results reporting, and cross-country portfolio comparisons.
