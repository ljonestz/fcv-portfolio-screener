I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT 
 
A. Country Context  
1. 
Ethiopia is at a historic moment as it faces an array of concurrent crises and a new inflow of refugees. 
The Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic, climate shocks (locusts, drought, and flooding), civil 
conflict, Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, and high inflation have severely impacted the wellbeing of Ethiopia’s 
people. Ethiopia has the second largest number of COVID-19 cases and fatalities in Sub-Saharan Africa with 
almost half a million cases and 8,000 deaths since March 20201. Severe repeating droughts and flooding have 
further impacted the livelihoods and food security of close to a quarter of the population2. Recent estimates by 
the United Nations (UN) Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) show 24.1 million Ethiopians 
are affected by the drought, of which 11 million are severely food insecure. A total of 13 million Ethiopians are 
estimated not to be able to access enough water for drinking, cooking, and cleaning or improved sanitation and 
hygiene due to the ongoing drought. As a result, water-borne and other infectious diseases are on the rise, with 
increases in cholera, measles, diarrhea, and pneumonia cases reported among drought-affected communities. 
The domestic conflict, which started in November 2020 in Tigray and spread to Amhara and Afar regions, has left 
9.4 million people in dire need of humanitarian assistance3. Following peace talks in South Africa, the 
Government of the Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia and the Tigray People’s Liberation Front recently 
signed an agreement for lasting peace through a permanent cessation of hostilities. If fully implemented, the 
deal is expected to create a conducive environment for the implementation of the proposed Ethiopia Human 
Capital Operation. Conflicts in neighboring countries (mainly South Sudan, Somalia, and Eritrea) continue to drive 
up a number of refugees and asylum seekers in Ethiopia, which hosts the second largest refugee population in 
Africa4. This has placed increased pressure on host regions Gambella, Somali, Afar, and Benishangul-Gumuz, 
which already faced severe challenges in providing services to children and women, and the recent developments 
in Sudan could worsen the situation. In addition, Russia’s invasion of Ukraine is negatively impacting the Ethiopian 
economy and particularly food prices. As of September 2022, the year-on-year general inflation rate in Ethiopia 
was 31 percent5. The World Bank estimates that the growth rate for Ethiopia in Fiscal Year (FY) 2022 will be 3.3 
percent, a slight increase from FY21 (2.4 percent), but below FY20 (6.1 percent).  
2. 
Urgent action is needed to return children to and keep them in school following the pandemic, 
droughts, and domestic conflict. Between 2005 and 2019, Ethiopia tripled its school enrollment from less than 
10 million to approximately 28 million.6 However, in 2020, the United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF) estimated 
that over 26 million children lost at least six months of school due to school closures as a result of the pandemic.7 
Conflict in parts of the country has undermined efforts to mitigate the impact of COVID-19, resulting in the 
destruction of schools and hospitals and subjecting many children, teachers, and communities to trauma and 
uncertainty. Education has been further affected as drought-affected communities exercise negative coping 
strategies of removing children from school - 1.4 million children are presently estimated to be out of school 
directly due to the ongoing drought – creating a risk of a long-term learning gap, and in turn reducing resilience

3. 
Ethiopia’s concurrent crises are placing new pressure on refugees and host communities. Ethiopia hosts 
890,309 refugees and asylum seekers as of March 20239 . Most refugees live in 24 camps across five regional 
states (43 percent in Gambella, 28 percent in Somali, 8.6 percent in Benishangul Gumuz, 6.5 percent in Afar, and 
2.5 percent in Amhara) while 8.4 percent live in Addis Ababa (Figure 1). Oromia, Somali, and the Southern 
Nations, Nationalities, and Peoples (SNNP) region, some of the areas hit hardest by the drought, are hosting 
261,498 refugees. Although the latest United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) data do not 
provide a breakdown by gender, the January 2023 report shows that 23 percent of refugees are women, and 57 
percent are children; together, women and children constitute some 80 percent of the refugee population10. The 
unexpected arrival of 100,000 Somali refugees to the Somali region in February 2023 have exacerbated 
socioeconomic challenges that had already become significant, with the UNHCR making a supplementary appeal 
of US$41.7 million to cover the needs of these refugees and host communities for food, water, health, and 
education, among others11.

4. 
Minimizing the impacts of widespread food insecurity, particularly among children, requires urgent 
action. Each of the crises plaguing Ethiopia, coupled with the new inflow of refugees has a negative impact on 
the availability of food. The pandemic, climate shocks, and domestic conflict hinder the delivery of critical services 
to prevent undernutrition among children, while the arrival of new refugees increases the demand for scarce 
food resources. Food inflation alone is a major concern, outpacing price increases of other goods and reaching 
almost 34 percent in January 202312. The International Red Cross/Red Crescent estimates that, resulting from the 
combined crises, 23 million people in Ethiopia need food assistance and that almost 7 million people face acute 
food insecurity.13 Funding shortfalls also resulted in the monthly food assistance for over 750,000 refugees in 
Ethiopia cut by 50 percent since June-November 2022.14 New arrivals of refugees in the Somali region are 
expected to aggravate food insecurity among refugees. This prevalence of undernutrition will have major 
implications on child morbidity and mortality and on future productivity and economic growth.  
5. 
The Human Capital Operation (HCO) is a response to help Ethiopia address the impact of these multiple 
shocks on human development by focusing on improving nutrition and learning outcomes for children, while 
building social cohesion. The operation takes a people-centric approach to deliver results at scale, benefitting 
an estimated 97 million Ethiopians and 800,000 refugees, with a particular focus on drought-affected areas, 
refugee-hosting communities and low performing areas. The HCO will employ a people-centric approach by 
supporting the deployment of frontline delivery staff at the woreda/district level, thus making services available 
to poor people. Working with a common objective of putting people at the center and by promoting social 
accountability, the operation will strive to generate better outcomes for its target beneficiaries wherever they 
are. People centricity also requires social accountability by directly involving citizens in decision-making processes 
and by collecting and analyzing data that can be used both to evaluate the performance of policies and services 
against people’s needs and expectations and to anticipate these needs. Through this approach, the Government 
of Ethiopia can rebuild trust in the public administration, improve the effectiveness of public action and better 
respond to the global and domestic challenges the country is facing.  The operation builds on lessons from 
previous World Bank financed programs such as the Enhancing Shared Prosperity through Equitable Services 
(ESPES) program, which demonstrated the impact of the results-based financing on empowering frontline staff 
to achieve national results, expanding coverage and quality of services, and enhancing citizen engagement. 
Investment through the national Intergovernmental Fiscal Transfer (IGFT) system brings government and services 
closer to people at the local level. The operation also incentivizes the Government of Ethiopia (GOE) to ensure 
sustained domestic social and economic investments at local levels. Other development partners such as Austria

Box 1. Evolution of Ethiopia’s Decentralization Program 
Over the past three decades, Ethiopia has devolved increasing responsibility to lower levels of government to promote 
progressive development and enhance political participation and transparency. Decentralization reforms aim to: (a) 
move government closer to the people to improve service delivery, (b) increase participatory governance, and (c) 
promote economic development at the grassroot level. Complementary investment in government systems for 
transparency provide concrete entry points for citizen engagement and feedback. Extensive research of the 
decentralized structures shows that these reforms have resulted in pro-poor outcomes in health and education.  
Ethiopia has had two main phases of decentralization, first decentralizing power to the regions and then to woredas 
and districts. The first phase of devolving power to the regions was codified in the 1995 Federal Democratic Republic 
of Ethiopia Constitution, which divided the country into nine regions and established Addis Ababa and Dire Dawa as 
city administrations reporting to the federal government. Since then, Sidama region and South West Ethiopia Peoples' 
region separated from the SNNP region and were established as separate states in 2020 and 2021, respectively.  
The second phase of decentralization, commonly known as woreda or district level decentralization, began in 2002. As 
per regional constitutions, regional governments devolve powers and give the responsibility of basic service provision 
to zonal structures in some regions and woreda and kebele administrative units. In practice, this means that woredas 
assume the bulk of service delivery responsibilities and receive GPGs from regional governments. The transfers are 
determined by formulas set by regional governments, which are mainly based on expenditure need assessments and 
revenue generation capacity of regions and woredas.

crosscutting issues, such as Public Financial Management (PFM), procurement, citizen engagement, 
environmental and social standards, and gender reforms.

The operation’s resources are protected through the established mechanisms that ensure resources 
are used for the intended purpose. There are four established mechanisms for the GPG that ensure resources 
are allocated to their intended purpose of financing basic services at local levels. First, during resource allocation, 
the World Bank closely tracks resources the MEFF allocated to social sectors against the budget execution. The 
MEFF allocation to social and poverty sectors is reviewed during the annual Joint Review and Implementation 
Support (JRIS) mission each May. The annual budget against the MEFF allocations is also reviewed annually at 
the November JRIS. Second, the operation will track that woreda-level GPG allocations, on average, for basic 
services/human capital sectors do not decline year on year from the baseline of 64 percent in Ethiopia Fiscal Year 
(EFY) 2015. Third, on the spending side, quarterly interim financial reports (IFR) and annual audit reports provide 
regular tracking of budget expenditures in the health, education, water, and agriculture sectors. Lastly, in 
addition to engagement in high risk, conflict-affected areas, the IPF instrument allows for the provision of critical 
capacity building support and risk mitigation measures to achieve the objectives set forth in the PforR.

The IPF component is being used to allow more direct investment in areas affected by conflict, 
droughts, and high inflow of refugees and capacity building across the country. The IPF instrument will ensure 
that all Ethiopian citizens and refugees benefit equitably from the operation by focusing on high risk conflict-
affected areas, as well as areas affected the most by droughts and the new inflow of refugees. In conflict-affected 
areas where social infrastructures are damaged or destroyed and where implementation capacities are
