A. 
Country Context 
 
1. 
Ethiopia, a large and diverse country with a population estimated at 109 million, is experiencing 
profound political and economic change. Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed came to power in April 2018 during 
Ethiopia’s worst political and social crises in decades, which had forced the Government to declare a state 
of emergency twice in three years. Since his appointment, the Prime Minister has signed a peace 
agreement with Eritrea and reopened the border following two decades of conflict, for which he received 
the Nobel Peace Prize. The administration has opened new political space for dialogue, released political 
prisoners, lifted bans on political parties and media outlets, actively engaged in regional diplomacy, and 
announced a range of economic reforms designed to revitalize the Ethiopian economy by expanding the 
role of the private sector. Given Ethiopia’s size and location, this shift has transformed the economic and 
political landscape in the Horn of Africa.  
2. 
The country has been one of the world’s fastest-growing economies over the past ten years, 
making important strides in poverty reduction, but significant progress remains to be made. Gross 
domestic product (GDP) grew at an average annual rate of approximately 10 percent between 2007 and 
2019, and this strong growth translated into substantial poverty reduction, with the share of the 
population living in extreme poverty decreasing from 37 percent in 2005 to 23.5 percent in 2016 (Ethiopia 
Household Consumption Expenditure Survey, HCES). Human development outcomes also improved from 
a low base. However, despite the progress made, Ethiopia remains among the world’s poorest countries 
with an annual per capita income of US$740 in 2017, with low human development, reflected in a score 
of 0.38 on the World Bank’s Human Capital Index.  
3. 
The outbreak of the Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic is seriously threatening 
Ethiopia’s gains in growth and poverty reduction, with urban areas affected the most by the resulting 
economic slowdown. The Government of Ethiopia (GoE) declared a state of emergency under Article 93 
of the constitution on April 8, 2020. Since then, the spread of COVID-19 has accelerated in Ethiopia and 
negative economic impacts are increasing rapidly. As of the end of August 2020, the country is hitting daily 
highs in reported COVID-19 cases (over a thousand). The economic and social impact of COVID-19 in 
Ethiopia is expected to be significant and prolonged, and authorities are facing an unanticipated financing 
gap of 1.5 percent of GDP (about US$1.5 billion) in FY21. More than half of recently surveyed households 
reported their incomes were either reduced or had totally disappeared. There are additional challenges 
of unemployment, as more people, particularly in urban areas, are losing jobs, as well as food security, 
increasing the need to expand safety nets. Over the next three months, an estimated 1.4 million jobs, 
accounting for 19 percent of current employment, are threatened due to the crisis. The worst of the 
COVID-19 crisis seems yet to come, both on the health and economic fronts, and authorities do not have 
enough funding for the warranted expansion of safety nets and employment support. 
4.

seen a structural transformation toward industry and services, but the contribution of the 
manufacturing sector to GDP and employment remains weak. Public investment, particularly in 
construction, has been the principal driver of Ethiopia’s recent economic growth. Construction 
contributed over 20 percent of Ethiopia’s GDP in 2017–18. The output shares of different sectors of the

5. 
Ethiopia is undergoing significant demographic and spatial transitions with large projected 
increases in working-age population combined with rural-to-urban migration, and sustainable job 
creation has emerged as a key policy challenge. On the demographic side, the working-age population is 
projected to grow from 54.7 million in 2015 to 74.8 million by 2025 and 85.3 million by 2030, an average 
increase of over 2 million a year. At the same time, the country is urbanizing fast from a low base. The 
urban population is expected to grow from an estimated 22.8 million in 2019 to 31 million by 2025 and 
39.3 million by 2030. These twin transitions will significantly affect urban labor markets already 
characterized by high levels of unemployment. Resources available for the provision of social services and 
support to the urban will also get further strained. Less educated youth face the highest unemployment 
rates in urban Ethiopia owing to limited experience as well as social and financial capital, and it is precisely 
this demographic that is expected to grow rapidly owing to the twin transitions. A defining challenge for 
Ethiopia, therefore, will be to integrate the fast-growing youth population into the urban labor market.  
6. 
Ethiopia is one of the most vulnerable countries to climate variability and change due to its high 
dependence on rain-fed agriculture and natural resources and its low adaptive capacity to deal with 
these expected changes. Additional challenges include the underdevelopment of water resources, low 
health service coverage, a high population growth rate, low economic development, inadequate road 
infrastructure in drought-prone areas, weak institutional structures, and lack of awareness. Ethiopia has 
frequently experienced extreme events like droughts and floods and other climate-related hazards, in 
addition to rainfall variability and increasing temperature, which contribute to adverse impacts to 
livelihoods. Future climate variability and changes are expected to worsen these conditions, potentially 
accelerating already high levels of land degradation, desertification, recurrent floods, and water scarcity. 
Without appropriate planning and further support to climate adaptation, these irregularities may disrupt 
economic activity and increase fiscal risks. 
7. 
Ethiopia has the third largest refugee population in Africa, hosting 733,125 refugees.2 Ethiopia 
has a long-standing history of hosting refugees and asylum seekers over several decades. Refugees are 
mainly from South Sudan, Somalia, Eritrea, and Sudan. Ethiopia is one of the most camp-reliant countries 
with the majority of refugees residing in 26 camps across five regional states (Gambella, Somali, Tigray, 
Benishangul-Gumuz, and Afar). Several refugee camps (for example, Kebribeyah camp and Sherkole 
camp) are located near urban centers. Close to 28,000 refugees reside in the capital city of Addis Ababa. 
The country has both protracted and recent refugee situations, with a large number of Somali refugees 
living in Ethiopia for more than 25 years while others mostly South Sudanese in Gambella arriving at end-

B. 
Sectoral and Institutional Context 
 
The Social Protection Sector 
8. 
Social protection has played a key role in Ethiopia’s reduction in poverty in recent years and is 
a critical component of the country’s long-term development strategy, as articulated in the second 
Growth and Transformation Plan 2016-20. The country’s National Social Protection Policy (NSPP) from 
2014 and the accompanying strategy and action plan for the sector have charted a significant expansion 
of social protection to cover a larger proportion of the population and a wider range of risks, thereby 
ensuring that Ethiopia’s strong economic performance is accompanied by a sustained reduction in poverty 
and vulnerability. The GoE recognizes the contribution of social protection to the development goals of 
the country and commits substantial human and financial resources to maximize the reach of such 
programs to its poorest and most vulnerable citizens. The NSPP includes five focus areas with most 
resources invested in the first area: (a) productive safety nets; (b) employment promotion and livelihood 
support; (c) social insurance; (d) access to basic services; and (e) addressing abuse, violence, and 
exploitation. Focus area 1 accounted for 71.4 percent of social protection spending between 2012/13 and 
2015/164 and is financed predominantly by external sources. Today, the GoE is aiming at financing about 
one-third of the social protection expenditures.  
9. 
Being a priority sector for the Government, the country has an extensive landscape of safety 
net programs in both rural and urban areas. Ethiopia has one of Africa’s largest and oldest rural safety 
net programs, the rural Productive Safety Net Program (PSNP, supported by the Productive Safety Net 
Project 4 (P146883) and the Rural Productive Safety Net Project (P163438)), which was established in 2005 
as a scalable rural safety net and today covers upward of eight million people. Its cash for work approach, 
based on multi-year landscape management planning, is being emulated the world over. The Urban 
Productive Safety Net Program (UPSNP) was created in 2016 and is serving over 600,000 urban poor and 
destitute. Furthermore, the country has developed a community-based health insurance, which includes 
more than five million beneficiaries and is partially covering urban and rural safety net beneficiaries. These 
significant and large investments in the country’s poor and vulnerable populations are supported by a 
diverse group of development partners including multi- and bilateral donors as much as United Nations 
(UN) and humanitarian agencies.  
10. 
Social service provision is rather limited, leaving significant portions of the elderly, disabled, 
and homeless vulnerable. While only about 5 percent of the population is above the age of 60, Ethiopia’s 
elderly are vulnerable to poverty, food insecurity, and limited access to social and health services. 
Furthermore, 15 million people in the country have mental or physical disabilities, an overwhelming 
majority of whom live in poverty. The traditional care and support system that the elderly and disabled 
rely upon is often no longer able to provide support due to urbanization, migration, and changes in social

11. 
The UPSNP was designed to address urban poverty and has been successfully implemented in 
11 major cities. Given the accelerated growth and growing opportunities in urban areas, the program has 
a strong focus on economic integration while helping selected households stabilize their income over 
three years. The project is implemented in 11 cities including Addis Ababa where 75 percent of the 
activities are implemented. The UPSNP includes (a) Cash-for-Work Program with monthly cash payments 
over three years; (b) Livelihoods Grants to promote self-employment and wage employment; (c) 
Permanent Direct Support (PDS) cash transfers for labor-constrained households (elderly, disabled, and 
pregnant and lactating mothers); (d) Services for Urban Destitute (homeless) with the objective of social, 
economic, and educational reintegration of homeless children and adults; and (e) Institutional 
Strengthening and Project Management. The program has been successfully scaled up and reaches 
600,000 beneficiaries, about 60 percent of whom are women. A total of 152,703 bank accounts have been 
opened for previously financially excluded households, and beneficiaries have already saved US$8.7 
million. The midline impact evaluation shows that public works beneficiaries have increased their incomes 
and savings, improved mental health, and improved children’s human capital development (for more 
details on the impact of the UPSNP, see annex 5).  
12. 
In response to the COVID-19 pandemic and economic downturn, both the rural and urban PSNPs 
adapted their modalities and are expanding their coverage. Both programs, together covering over 8.5 
million Ethiopians, suspended the public works requirement to adhere with social distancing practices, 
making cash payments to all beneficiaries without any conditionalities. The UPSNP also advanced the 
payments for three months in one single payment. Furthermore, both programs increased the level of 
benefit starting in August 2020, to account for food price increases. While the rural PSNP plans to expand 
the coverage of beneficiaries in rural hot spot areas that are particularly affected by the virus, the UPSNP 
is planning to set up a Temporary Income Support Program, targeting the urban poor who have lost their 
jobs and livelihoods due to the COVID-19 situation with temporary cash transfers.  
Ethiopia’s Refugee Situation 
13. 
The GoE maintains an open-door policy to refugees. Ethiopia is a state party to the 1951 
Convention and the 1967 Protocol relating to the Status of Refugees.5 It has also ratified the 1969 
Convention Governing the Specific Aspects of Refugee Problems in Africa. The Agency for Refugees and 
Returnees Affairs (ARRA), currently housed under the Ministry of Peace, is a designated government body 
overseeing day-to-day refugee status with the support of the UNHCR and other humanitarian partners.

14. 
The GoE has articulated its progressive approach toward a more comprehensive refugee 
response. During the 2016 Leaders' Summit, the then Prime Minister committed to nine pledges to start 
operationalizing this vision, subject to the availability of external resources. Ethiopia became one of the 
first countries to implement the Comprehensive Refugee Response Framework (CRRF). More recently, the 
GoE made additional pledges during the first Global Refugee Forum in December 2019, which include the 
(a) creation of up-to 90,000 socioeconomic opportunities through agricultural and livestock value chains 
that benefit both refugees and host communities, (b) provision of quality and accredited skills training to 
20,000 hosts and refugees on an equitable basis, (c) provision of market-based and sustainable household 
and facility-based energy solutions for 3 million hosts and refugees, and (d) strengthening of the GoE's 
asylum system and social protection capacity.  
15. 
Since Ethiopia became eligible for the IDA18 Regional Sub-Window in 2017, the GoE made 
significant progress in promoting sustainable management of refugee situations. The GoE recognized 
the need for significant revisions to its 2004 refugee law and enacted a new Refugee Proclamation (No. 
1110/2019) in February 2019. The legislation creates an enabling legal and protection environment to 
promote self-reliance for refugees, including freedom of movement, rights to work, and access to basic 
services such as education and health. In December 2019, ARRA, in partnership with the UNHCR, finalized 
a series of directives, one of which outlines the implementation of rights to work for refugees. The GoE is 
also finalizing a 10-year National Comprehensive Refugee Response Strategy (NCRRS). The NCRRS, which 
is currently tabled for approval by the Council of Ministers, will be a guiding document for a whole-of-
government approach enhancing self-reliance and resilience for refugees and host communities. The 
ongoing IDA18 Regional Sub-Window financed Economic Opportunities Program (P163829) contributes 
to the enactment of the proclamation. The program continues to support the GoE with its implementation 
of the proclamation and ARRA’s capacity to continuous refugee protection services. The GoE plans to 
continue this approach with the aim of sustainably managing refugee situations with a medium-term 
perspective. The Government continues to focus on (a) gradually providing out-of-camp opportunities for 
the socioeconomic integration of refugees (for example, freedom of movement, work permits, skills 
development, and jobs), (b) improving living standards for host communities (economic opportunities and 
services), (c) building human capital among refugees (including education and health/nutrition), and (d) 
transitioning away from humanitarian aid where relevant.  
16. 
The World Bank, following consultation with the UNHCR, confirms that the protection 
framework for refugees continues to be adequate in Ethiopia. To date, Ethiopia has no history of 
systemic or systematic refoulement or denial of access to territory and has practices broadly consistent 
with the international refugee protection standards.  
Urban Labor Markets and the Jobs Agenda 
17. 
Urban poverty reduction has accelerated in the recent years, owing in part to improved labor 
market outcomes, but some of this progress may be reversed by the COVID-19 pandemic. Urban poverty 
dropped from 26 percent in 2011 to 15 percent in 2016 (the corresponding drop in rural poverty was 30 
percent to 26 percent). This has been driven, in part, by improved labor market outcomes and fairly stable 
food prices. Between 2011 and 2016, urban labor force participation increased, and unemployment 
decreased, indicating strong job growth. The number of employed people in urban areas increased from 
4.5 million in 2011 to 7 million by 2016. Since then, however, youth unemployment in urban areas 
increased from 22 percent in 2016 to 26 percent in 2020, likely a reflection of limited recruitment by firms

18. 
In contrast to many low-income countries, wage employment dominates Ethiopia’s urban labor 
market. Overall, wage employment accounted for 55 percent of employment in 2018, compared to 41 
percent for self-employment. Wage employment rates are the highest in Addis Ababa at 67 percent, with 
other smaller cities having higher rates of self-employment. While increased engagement in and returns 
to self-employment have driven a fair share of urban poverty reduction since 2011, wage employment 
remains strongly correlated with higher living standards. Households with a wage-employed head have 
significantly lower poverty rates (below 10 percent) than the overall urban population (15 percent).  
19. 
Young people, women, and the less-educated face significant disadvantages in Ethiopia’s urban 
labor markets. Over 30 percent of youth ages 18 to 24 years in urban Ethiopia are unemployed, compared 
to the overall unemployment rate of 19 percent. Unemployment rates decrease sharply with age, 
highlighting the importance of initial work experience, which simultaneously is important and difficult to 
obtain. Of all the unemployed young people ages 18–25 years in urban Ethiopia, over 63 percent have 
never worked before, and this demographic tends to stay unemployed for a long time, creating the risk of 
persistent scarring effects on longer-term employment and earnings.7 Detailed data on firms and workers 
in Ethiopia’s formal labor market from 2011 to 2018 suggest high rates of worker turnover, particularly 
among younger workers due to firings or voluntary separations highlighting the importance of matching 
frictions in the labor market. The low quality of job matches in the urban labor markets may be driven at 
least in part by the difficulty young job seekers face in signaling their abilities and skills to prospective 
employers. Women face additional constraints to accessing the labor market including gender norms, 
occupational segregation, and limited mobility, resulting in an unemployment rate of 26.4 percent, more 
than double that for men (12.2 percent). Unemployment rates also vary by education levels, with college 
graduates facing the lowest unemployment rates (14 percent) and secondary school educated making up 
almost 70 percent of the unemployed population. Improving urban living standards and reducing poverty 
therefore require a more inclusive labor market through the design of interventions and policies aimed at 
addressing the specific constraints faced by the youth, women, and the less educated. 
20. 
The GoE is increasingly focusing on the country’s job creation agenda as a complement to social 
protection and recently set up the JCC under the Prime Minister’s Office to this effect. In the past, the 
Government support for job creation primarily focused on micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) 
development and cooperative support in the agricultural sector. Further, most beneficiaries of 
government job creation initiatives were university graduates with relatively low levels of unemployment. 
More recently, the GoE has established a Youth Revolving Fund, aimed at providing capital to groups of 
unemployed youth who are willing to start collective businesses. The GoE also set up the JCC which seeks 
to coordinate, innovate, and promote job creation in the country, with a strong public-private partnership 
approach. Several development partners, including the World Bank, are supporting the JCC in its efforts. 
The GoE is also advancing the job creation agenda through industrialization policies, urban and rural job 
creation schemes, and broader economic reforms which are also supported by the World Bank. The 
proposed Urban Productive Safety Net and Jobs Project (UPSNJP) plays a significant role in these efforts

21. 
Under the leadership of the JCC, the GoE has approved the National Plan for Job Creation 2020–
2025. The National Plan for Job Creation proposes holistic interventions to solve the employment and job 
creation challenges and provides a new vision of employment in Ethiopia. The plan aims to foster the 
business environment and conditions necessary to create 14 million jobs by 2025, to absorb the currently 
unemployed, and to ensure that jobs are waiting for new entrants to the labor force. The plan has been 
developed through an important and lengthy dialog with relevant stakeholders from the public and 
private sectors. A primary consideration in drafting the new National Plan for Job Creation has been an 
important paradigm shift from state-led to private sector-led growth. The six main strategic pillars of the 
plan are (a) adopting job-rich macro policies, (b) building a vibrant local private sector by revamping the 
current support to MSMEs, (c) developing human capital to meet the changing needs of the labor market, 
(d) strengthening labor market intermediation and links, (e) improving the inclusiveness of the labor 
market by providing targeted services to populations excluded from the labor market, and (f) realizing the 
job creation potential of prospective high-yield sectors. 
22. 
The landscape of job-related services is fragmented with public employment agencies having 
limited capacity, and the GoE intends to streamline the jobs agenda institutionally. Jobs search services 
are provided by multiple agencies, including the Ministry of Urban Development and Construction 
(MoUDC), the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs (MoLSA), the Ministry of Agriculture (MoA) through its 
Department of Rural Job Creation, and the JCC. Public employment service provision is coordinated within 
the Federal Ministry and Regional Bureaus of Labor and Social Affairs (BoLSAs) as well as certain other 
public agencies. These agencies provide free services to both employers as well as job seekers but lack 
the capacity for effective service delivery. Shortages in human and financial resources as well as a lack of 
information technology (IT) tools and clear processes to establish links with the private sector have 
hindered their functioning. An estimated 73 percent of the unemployed lack information about both 
vacancies as well as government services that can provide relevant information. Private employment 
agencies have had greater success in job placements but have limited reach and leave job seekers 
vulnerable to exploitative agencies and practices.8 The GoE is therefore aiming to streamline service 
provision in this sector through institutional reforms which are expected be implemented after the 
upcoming national elections (which have been postponed due to the COVID-19 crisis). The envisioned 
reforms could indeed enable the establishment of more efficient and effective labor market intermediary 
services.

Building on the experience of the UPSNP, this component would provide labor-intensive public 
work opportunities for selected urban poor in selected cities, assist in business development, and 
provide livelihood grants for beneficiary households. The public work and livelihood program 
beneficiaries would include poor urban households with able-bodied household members selected using 
a combination of geographic targeting, community preselection, a Proxy Means Test (PMT), and

community validation (see annex 4 for a detailed description of the targeting methodology). Selected 
beneficiaries would participate in the program for three years during which they get income support 
through public works during 12 months of each year but in a decreasing manner in the third year to ensure 
they have time to develop their livelihood activities. As under the UPSNP, this component implements a 
variation of the ‘graduation’ model which provides an integrated development pathway for livelihoods 
and has been tested and implemented in multiple countries. It provides three years of comprehensive 
support for the public works beneficiaries. In the first year, beneficiaries participate in public works as 
well as life skills and financial skills training. They are encouraged to start savings from the beginning so 
that they can later invest in their business. In the second year, beneficiaries receive support for business 
planning, technical training as needed, and job search support from woreda-level One Stop Shop Centers 
(OSSCs) which are specialized in supporting micro- and small business development. Based on the training 
and business plan, the beneficiaries receive a livelihood grant in the third year during which they work 
less time in public works and thus have more time for building their business. During this final year, they 
also receive mentoring/coaching advice from the OSSCs. The approach is equally implemented for host 
communities and refugees, with some small adaptations as described below.  
Subcomponent 1.1: Public Works (US$188.3 million equivalent) 
38. 
Public work beneficiaries would receive monthly payments, based on a daily wage rate 
calibrated so as not to undercut the labor market. The maximum monthly support for a household of 
four (or more) would be about US$55 for approximately 20 hours of work each week. Cash payments will 
be made monthly through the banking system and increasingly through mobile banking. The daily wage 
rate is determined considering the market wage rate for similar or comparable unskilled labor. The cash-
for-work activities would be complemented by training sessions providing financial literacy skills and 
information on essential family practices, reproductive health, parenting, gender-based violence (GBV) 
issues, and availability of referral services. Public works program will be rolled out in up to 83 cities based 
on an independent assessment that would ensure regions’ and cities’ readiness for implementation. Cities 
would roll out the number of beneficiaries over time, depending on the number of beneficiaries in each 
city, planning for either one, two or three waves of targeting.  
39. 
Public works will be identified and planned by city governments through a participatory process 
involving local communities. They would encompass five areas: (a) urban greenery and beautification, (b) 
urban integrated solid waste management, (c) urban integrated watershed management, (d) social 
infrastructure (as part of a larger plan such as environmental, disaster prevention, and human 
development enhancing initiatives), and (e) creating a conducive environment for urban agriculture. The 
activities would be planned for three years and implemented on a rolling basis. Work site management 
and coordination will be provided by the JOBFSA’s woreda/kebele offices. The implementation of PW will 
adhere to Occupational Health and Safety standards agreed for the Project. Among others, the JOBFSA 
has developed guidelines for covering the medical costs for injuries at the work place for public work and 
youth employment beneficiaries upon the submission of their injuries with evidence that indicate that the 
accident occurred while they were on site. Procurement of necessary goods, protective equipment and 
material will be carried out at the city level with support from the regional liaison offices.  
40.
