A. Country Context 
1. 
Ethiopia remains one of the fastest growing economies in Sub-Saharan Africa, but internal conflict and severe 
climate events have dampened its growth. For over a decade, Ethiopia exhibited double-digit growth rates and made big 
strides in reducing poverty. However, poverty remains widespread and the slowdown of economic growth due to conflict, 
natural disasters, and the COVID pandemic could reverse advances. Following the outbreak of the pandemic, employment 
rates plunged in 2020.1 As the impact of COVID receded, the country was hit by natural disasters and internal conflict. The 
country was confronted with prolonged droughts, frequent floodings, and a locust upsurge over the last few years. While 
a peace agreement with Tigray was reached in 2022, the conflict-induced humanitarian crisis has not abated and. internal 
conflicts within regions are still ongoing.  
2. 
To tackle structural distortions of and accelerate a shift to a private sector driven economy, Ethiopia has 
embarked on an ambitious reform agenda, but its macroeconomic outlook remains challenging. An overvalued 
exchange rate, limiting regulations, a large state footprint with an overhang from debt-financed state-owned enterprises 
investments, and the financial situation have resulted in deep structural distortions in the economy. These distortions 
have held back productivity and job creation, undermined external competitiveness, and crowded out the private sector. 
The Home Grown Economic Reform Agenda aims to sustain high growth rates achieved over the last decade, addressing 
macroeconomic balances and fostering competition in key sectors, including energy. Yet, Ethiopia’s macroeconomic 
outlook remains difficult. Besides local challenges, high global commodity prices have contributed to sustained inflation 
of more than 30 percent over the past year. Amid growing foreign exchange (FX) shortages and external financing 
pressures, and with macroeconomic buffers depleted, Ethiopia requested debt relief through the G20 Common 
Framework in early 2021; in November 2023, it reached agreement on a debt suspension deal with official creditors, and 
in late December 2023, officially defaulted on US$33 million in debt service to Eurobond creditors. The country remains 
in discussions on a new loan with the International Monetary Fund. 
3. 
Integrating climate resilience into development planning is imperative for Ethiopia, which remains highly 
vulnerable to climate related disasters that disproportionally affect women and girls. Over the last 20 years, Ethiopia 
has experienced severe flood and droughts almost annually, affecting millions of people. In November 2023, torrential 
rains led to flash floodings in the Gambella and Afar region, bringing about a surge in cholera cases, while parts of Tigray 
and Amhara region are still battling a severe drought with six consecutive failed or poor rainy seasons. Climate-induced 
displacement has become increasingly prominent. Gender-based violence (GBV) has escalated due to the conflict. 
Humanitarian assessments note the vulnerability of displaced women and children to food insecurity, loss of livelihood, 
increased care burden, and disruption in access to education and basic health.  
B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
4.

National Energy Policy. The Council of Ministers Proclamation No.302/2013 unbundled the sector institutional structure 
into (a) the Ethiopian Electric Power (EEP), responsible for generation, transmission, and system operations, and (b) the 
Ethiopian Electric Utility (EEU), responsible for power distribution, sales, and customer services. It established a regulatory 
agency, the Petroleum and Energy Authority (PEA), formerly known as Ethiopian Energy Authority. The Ministry of Water 
and Energy (MoWE) is responsible for sector policy and overall development, while multiple ministries and agencies are 
involved in various roles, including the Ministry of Finance (MoF), Ministry of Mines (MoM), Ministry of Trade and Regional

5. 
Ethiopia’s electricity sector is no longer able to  accelerate access expansion. As of 2020, only 51 percent of 
Ethiopians have access to electricity, leaving close to 57 million people without electricity services.  About 93 percent of 
urban households are connected to the grid (99.9 percent in Addis Ababa), while only 40 percent of rural households have 
access, a big gaining access through off-grid solutions. Annual per capita electricity consumption is 69 kWh compared to 
the world average of 3,131 kWh2. Launched in 2005, the Universal Electricity Access Program brought the electricity grid 
within 2.5 km of 65 percent of all households by 2015. The 2017 National Electrification Plan (NEP) and 2019 NEP 2.0 
present an investment roadmap for achieving universal access by 2025 through grid and off-grid solutions3. However, the 
broader NEP ambition is expected to be delayed by nearly a decade and is constrained by limited funding, weak 
implementation capacity, and the need to import key electrification materials. New approaches needed to accelerate the 
delivery of electricity access are expected to be reflected in the revised NEP 3.0. 
6. 
Weak financial performance of the electricity sector utilities affects their ability to efficiently operate, maintain 
assets, and provide adequate quality of service. Electricity tariffs remained nominally constant between 2006-2017 and 
are among the lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa4. A four-year tariff trajectory during 2018-2021 took the average retail tariffs 
to US$0.0326 equivalent per kWh in 2022 against the initial expectation of US$0.067 equivalent per kWh. EEU has 
consistently shown a marginal profit, while EEP has been able to cover its operating costs each year but has consistently 
shown overall losses. The large financial costs for EEP are due to significant debt overhang and currency devaluation. A 
US$10 billion debt was restructured in 2021, with US$3.5 billion equivalent transferred to the Liability and Asset 
Management Company. Also, the revenue sharing between EEU and EEP has been evolving from a fixed 37:63 ratio, to a 
Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)-based on boundary metering of energy, and going forward to separate end-user charges 
for generation, transmission, and distribution under the new tariff directive. A new four-year tariff proposal is under 
preparation.  
7. 
Significant technical and commercial losses and poor quality of service need to be urgently addressed to 
improve utility performance and increase resilience of an aging grid infrastructure toward climate change. Utilities are 
faced with inadequate tariff revenues, high technical and commercial losses, suboptimal investment prioritization, delays 
in project implementation, and weak institutional capacity. This has forced the utilities to compromise on regular 
maintenance of assets, ensuing dilapidated infrastructure and leads to poor quality and reliability of supply to end 
consumers and higher technical losses. EEU also reports a high transformer failure rate indicating overloading and poor 
maintenance. More than half of the grid connected household are not formally registered as consumers with the utility. 
About 65 percent of the households experienced electricity interruptions twice or more and 22 percent four or more 
interruptions per week5. 
8. 
To improve the sector’s financial sustainability, Ethiopia embarked on a journey of sectoral reforms and 
continued tariff rationalization but has yet to institutionalize structured and periodic tariff determination processes. 
The mandate for several sector reform actions is already reflected in the prevailing legal, policy, and regulatory statutes 
but implementation is constrained by limited agency focus, delayed processing, lack of appropriate tools, and inadequate 
institutional capacity. The MoF and MoWE have therefore constituted a Power Sector Reform Task Force to develop a 
medium-term reform vision and establish key preparation milestones for a phased and time-bound implementation. The 
medium-term trajectory for structural reforms in the form of an Electricity Sector Reform Roadmap will mandate time-
bound preparatory steps toward the establishment of a market-based structure conducive to greater private participation

9. 
Ethiopia is emerging as a regional energy hub with electricity exports that provide additional revenue and 
generate much needed FX. In the fiscal year ending July 2022, Ethiopia secured over US$95 million in revenue from 
electricity exports to Sudan and Djibouti. The World Bank-funded 2,000 MW Ethiopia-Kenya line became operational in 
December 20226. The Second Djibouti-Ethiopia line is being funded jointly by the World Bank and the African Development 
Bank (AfDB)7. Ethiopian electricity exports are crucial not only for clean and affordable power supply but also to maintain 
demand-supply balance, cross-subsidize domestic consumption, adapt to climate-induced hydrology variations, and earn 
precious FX. 
10. 
To increase resilience and reliability of electricity supply and further grow electricity exports in the context of 
climate change, the country needs to diversify its generation mix and modernize its grid networks. The total installed 
capacity has more than quadrupled between 2009 and 2022 and will reach about 13,000 MW by 20288. Yet less than 1 
percent of its solar, wind, and geothermal resources have been exploited to date. With 98 percent of the generation 
coming from clean sources, Ethiopia is leading the region in its share of renewables. However, an almost complete reliance 
on hydropower makes it extremely vulnerable to hydrological variations likely to be further exacerbated by climate 
change. Ethiopia is therefore aiming to diversify its generation mix through private sector led development of solar, wind, 
and geothermal power to reduce its high exposure and vulnerability to hydro-meteorological disasters. The existing grid 
network is not dimensioned to handle an expected increase in extreme weather events, likely efficiency losses due to 
rising temperatures, and the demand growth leading to equipment damage and forced outages. Moreover, outdated 
management systems prevent utilities from effectively operating the grid. Yet, resiliency of the grid is increasingly critical 
in a context of rising temperatures and increased frequency of extreme weather events.  
11. 
Progress is being made towards tapping into the country’s large geothermal potential, but greater efforts are 
needed to scale up its exploration and utilization. Geothermal power is identified in the government’s development plan 
as one of the renewable energy sources to meet the country’s growing energy demand. It carries important value for grid 
stability by providing baseload power.9 To leverage the vast geothermal power opportunity, previous success with public-
financed upstream resource exploration at Aluto-Langano need to be replicated at other geothermal sites to help de-risk 
downstream private sector investments. EEP is expected to finalize a Geothermal Strategy, which will include a list of 
prioritized geothermal sites and a roadmap for the exploration drilling and the downstream utilization of geothermal with 
private sector participation. 
12. 
Private sector participation in renewable energy generation could be game-changing, but significant challenges 
remain. Further to the 2018 public-private partnership (PPP) Proclamation of the Government of Ethiopia (GoE), the World 
Bank offered IDA payment guarantees to support the GoE’s pathbreaking efforts to establish an independent power 
producer (IPP) program for wind and solar projects under the Renewable Energy Guarantees Program (REGREP - P162607). 
The overall program aims to mobilize over US$1.5 billion in private investment for at least 1,000 MW of solar and wind 
IPPs. The Gad and Dicheto solar IPP projects (125 MW each) which were competitively bid in 2019 discovered one of the 
lowest solar PPA prices in Africa at US¢2.526 per kWh. However, the program suffered a setback when the first set of 
competitively selected IPP arrangements had to be terminated as the developers were unable to reach financial close—
primarily due to lack of assured FX availability, convertibility, and transferability. Consequently, a second round of

13. 
The proposed multiphase approach aims to support the Government’s vision toward achieving universal access 
to modern, affordable, reliable, and high-quality electricity services in Ethiopia. To address the challenges in the 
electricity sector, the program is designed to address critical investment needs immediately to mitigate the risk of the

14.

strengthen grid network to absorb higher variable renewable energy, and establish cross-border transmission links for

D. Multiphase Programmatic Approach (MPA) 
(i) 
Rationale for Using MPA 
18. 
Accelerating the transformation of the electricity sector in Ethiopia requires a medium-term approach, to 
address intertwined structural challenges and to have a strong signaling effect toward the private sector. Substantial 
private sector participation and capital can only be unlocked once the sector is on a clear pathway toward financial 
viability, which will require the implementation of a suite of interlinked activities: operational reforms to cut commercial 
losses and maximize revenues, smart infrastructure investments to modernize an aging grid and meet growing demand, 
structural reforms to introduce competitive efficiency toward least cost generation, and institutional capacity building to 
handle an increasingly complex sector. To ensure sustained impact and to send a strong signal to the private sector, there 
needs to be a strong visible commitment of the GoE to introduce solutions in a coordinated and sequential manner. 
Individual projects or a series of projects would fall short of the needed level of commitment for the signaling effect and 
not offer a strong enough mechanism for the well-coordinated implementation of solutions.  
19. 
The MPA allows the opportunity to pursue and monitor sector reforms to sustain parallel investments into grid 
infrastructure and generation capacity. The urgently needed investments planned under the program will not be 
sustainable without accompanying reforms. The MPA allows for a medium-term reform trajectory that will benefit from 
technical assistance and capacity building as well as deploy performance-based conditions (PBCs) and disbursement linked 
indicators (DLIs) to pursue implementation of the agreed reform trajectory. While the PBCs in PRIME 1 will support and 
track the implementation of key reform actions that have been already mandated in the prevailing policy framework, DLIs 
in PRIME 2 and ELEAP will ensure continued utility performance improvement and further reform actions which might 
require longer upfront preparation and an evolving political environment. The design and implementation of both 
structural and operation reforms will be supported by the ongoing Programmatic Advisory Services and Analytics (PASA) 
on Energy Sector Reforms (P180247) and other TAs and capacity building activities with the aim to ensure 
complementarity of the broader sector reform.

Program Description and Phasing  
24. 
Structured as an MPA and implemented in synergy with other sector engagements, PRIME will deliver a leap 
forward in the electricity sector through advancement of all elements of the value chain, thereby underpinning 
Ethiopia’s electricity access agenda and climate resilience. It will help Ethiopia in climate adaptation by diversifying the 
generation mix toward non-hydro. Through targeted upstream and downstream de-risking of the private sector, it will 
help unlock at least 600 MW of IPPs. The MPA will support the strengthening and expansion of the transmission network 
to address the problems of line overload, transformer overload, under voltage, over voltage, and overarching challenges 
in managing systems dynamics and short circuits. Improvements in quality and reliability of supply will be achieved 
through distribution rehabilitation in over 150 key towns. Further, with the generation capacity set to expand, the 
transmission carrying capacity and reliability (through built-in redundancies) will be strengthened by constructing new 
lines and substations as well as upgrading the existing ones. This upstream network infrastructure is necessary to enable 
a tripling of the formal utility connections.  
25. 
In parallel, PRIME will support a reform trajectory encompassing structural and operational reforms. PRIME will 
help implement the reform mandate through a combination of reform-focused technical assistance and capacity building 
for the sector utilities, relevant ministries, and the regulator while leveraging PBCs for Investment Project Financing (IPF) 
and DLIs for Program-for-Results (PforR) financing to pursue continued progress on implementation of structural and 
operational reforms. 
26. 
PRIME-1 (IPF with PBCs) will prioritize critical infrastructure investments and set the stage for structural and 
operational reforms. Implementation of specific reform actions and performance improvement steps will be supported 
through a combination of technical assistance and PBCs. Building on the Access to Distributed Electricity and Lighting 
Project (ADELE) (P171742), investments under PRIME-1 will cover distribution rehabilitation and transmission 
strengthening critical to mitigating the risk of a widening access gap. In addition, the project will help lay the foundation 
toward exploiting the country’s vast geothermal potential.  
27. 
Continuing to support reform actions, PRIME-2 (PforR) will help improve operational performance and financial 
sustainability of the distribution utility. Building and expanding on ELEAP, this phase will support activities necessary for 
reduction in technical and commercial losses; improved billing efficiency using enhanced energy accountability and 
collections through deployment of smart meters and prepaid meters; stronger utility IT systems; evolved utility 
Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition systems; improved customer management system; and other relevant measures. 
PRIME-2 will be structured as a PforR with DLIs related to various performance improvement measures and key reform 
actions to broaden and deepen the reform activities under PRIME 1.  
28. 
PRIME-3 (IPF with IDA Guarantees) will provide continued credit enhancement for private sector participation 
in the country’s power sector. Sector reforms and upstream risk mitigation measures for IPP projects under PRIME-1 and 
improvements in the financial sustainability of the utilities under PRIME 2 and other planned World Bank engagement are 
expected to enable the scaling-up of private sector participation supported by IDA guarantees, MIGA or other risk 
mitigation mechanisms. Along with solar and wind IPP projects supported under the Renewable Energy Guarantees 
Program (REGREP) (P162607), PRIME-3 will also cover geothermal IPPs, Independent Power Transmission Companies, and 
PPPs in other subsectors. Key pre-requisites for PRIME-3 include improved macro and sector financial situation.  
29. 
PRIME-4 (IPF) will support the rehabilitation of old hydropower capacity as well as upstream development of 
renewable energy parks. Rehabilitating dilapidated hydropower plants is a least cost generation expansion solution. This 
phase will also support upstream resource establishment; site development; and transmission infrastructure for solar, 
wind, and geothermal IPP projects. It will also support the establishment of a new Geothermal Resource Identification 
Agency.

Learning Agenda 
30. 
The PRIME MPA provides a unique opportunity for continuous learning, including learning by doing and 
leveraging complementary technical assistance which are particularly critical for sector reforms such as tariff and 
institutional reforms, a long-term engagement. The learning agenda will focus on distilling early lessons on how to 
support the government’s reform program especially on tariffs and restructuring, improving power sector financial 
viability, mobilizing private sector participation in a volatile economic and political setting, and ensuring findings are 
applied to enhance the design of future phases. To this end, its main objectives are to identify the most effective (a) 
measures to improve financial performance in the Ethiopia context; (b) ways of building institutional capacity and 
readiness for robust power sector planning, timely implementation of reforms, and renewable scale‐up; and (c) activities 
to enhance implementation capacity. The success of the learning agenda can be assessed in quantitative terms based on 
the pace of program implementation and achievement of PrDOs and in qualitative terms through improved and informed 
design of future phases.  
31. 
Close monitoring and discussion of results with various stakeholder groups will inform the design of subsequent 
phases, thereby strengthening their design and identifying evolving opportunities for private sector participation and 
capital mobilization. Results will be closely monitored to understand the effectiveness of various activities undertaken, 
particularly in relation to PBCs. Based on insights, the design of the future phases will be adapted. This will entail (a) 
discussion of results with key stakeholders to assess progress on a continuous basis and identify corrective measures if 
needed, (b) periodic review of the implementing capacity and identification of needs, and (c) ongoing dialogue with the

C. Project Beneficiaries 
 
41. 
The beneficiaries of PRIME-1 are the existing and new electricity consumers, including industrial, commercial, 
and residential customers. The MPA - together with other ongoing and planned programs - can potentially support the 
entire population of the country over the lifetime of the MPA. The strengthening and extension of the electricity network 
will provide people with improved electricity services, while the geothermal capacity enabled in the project will help 
diversify the generation mix and increase electricity generation to meet growing demand. Local employment opportunities 
will emerge though construction and rehabilitation project activities. The implementing agencies will benefit from capacity 
building and technical assistance. Consequently, they will be able to deliver new and improved services to customers. The 
project will also provide clear signals to the private sector on renewable energy investments and incentivize local industrial 
development and capacity building. The project will also help ministries and government agencies to reach their national 
targets.  
D. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners 
42. 
The World Bank has a long-standing engagement in Ethiopia’s electricity sector and is uniquely positioned to 
support the GoE. The World Bank has supported the GoE through the past decades on sector development and reforms 
through various investments, policy dialogues, technical assistance, and an ongoing dialogue. The project builds on this 
engagement to help the GoE make progress toward its longer-term goals and vision for the sector. The World Bank also 
has an extensive and robust dialogue with the donor partners to facilitate additional financing. Finally, the project enables 
the World Bank to support the Government in building on the recent progress and convert it into action. The World Bank 
therefore adds value as a long-standing financing partner, knowledge partner, and convener.

E. Lessons Learned  
44. 
Comprehensive sector reforms require long term engagement supported by technical assistance and capacity 
building. A broad sector reform roadmap with specific reform and investment actions will be identified through 
implementation and lessons learned, including identifying decrees, policies, cabinet decisions, and other directives needed 
to improve the sector’s financial viability, efficiency, and adequacy. A high-level reform task force will spearhead the 
government’s efforts to develop the reform roadmap and facilitate its final adoption, which will inform the design and 
implementation of the reform actions. 
45. 
Ensure flexibility and simplicity in results-oriented design, in close coordination with development partners. 
Given the complex political and economic environment, it is important to ensure that the design of activities is simple with 
flexibility in implementation and in approach to monitoring and evaluation (M&E). A key benefit of the MPA is its ability 
to adapt the design of subsequent phases to changing circumstances and create synergies with development partners 
when the opportunity arises.  
46. 
Strong Project Implementation Units (PIUs) are a critical success factor. Experience has shown that a strong PIU 
with adequate capacity in technical, procurement, financial management (FM), environmental and social risk 
management, and results monitoring is needed for ensuring the smooth and timely implementation of projects. The 
project builds on the already established PIU capacity and will continue to be further strengthened as needed. 
47. 
The project design incorporates lessons and experiences from the World Bank’s ongoing engagement in PPP 
and geothermal development in Ethiopia, including the lessons from the failed attempt of solar auction and the 
importance for public sector participation to mitigate geothermal exploration risks and strengthening of both institutional 
and technical capacity. Geothermal energy is a localized energy resource and therefore the location plays an important 
role in geothermal development’s cost and utilization, not only from a social and environmental standpoint but also for 
grid connection for transmission.
