A. Country Context 
 
1. In Ethiopia there is an urgent need to support the health system; the devastation due to the conflict has 
had a significant and adverse impact on access to health services. People who are impacted are in dire 
need of services, as health care delivery mechanisms have been reduced and new approaches are needed 
to reach populations. The proposed operation is built on the recently closed Ethiopia Health Sustainable 
Development Goals Program-for-Results (P123531) that performed well and built strong institutional 
arrangements while bringing consistency to the health sector.  
2. The conflict in the Tigray, Amhara, Afar, and other parts of the country resulted in significant disruptions 
in the delivery of essential health services (EHS) including key reproductive, maternal, neonatal, child, 
and adolescent health plus nutrition (RMNCAH+N) services. It is estimated close to 24 million people have 
been impacted in the conflict-affected areas. The conflict has had devastating impacts on people’s lives, 
both from direct physical harm and the collapse of the health system. The conflict has displaced more than 
5.5 million Ethiopians and is overwhelming an already strained health system1. Maternal, child, and nutrition 
health outcomes have deteriorated, and delivery of basic and emergency health services in the conflict- 
affected areas are crippled.  
3. The Ethiopia Program for Results (Hybrid) for Strengthening Primary Health Care Services aims to improve 
essential and equitable health care services, especially for women and children, who are the most 
vulnerable. The operation is fully aligned with the World Bank Group Strategy for Fragility, Conflict, and 
Violence (FCV) 2020–2025, recognizing that inclusive and effective social sector service delivery is central to 
improving state legitimacy and trust in institutions. The framework for engagement in FCV countries 
underlines improving human and social capital; equitably scaling up private sector engagement for impact; 
strengthening core institutions; and working across the humanitarian development nexus. 
4. This people-centric operation has been prepared as both a crisis response and a commitment to human 
capital development. In addition, the operation is aligned with six key modalities linked to the FCV Strategy: 
(i) systemization of partnerships of actors; (ii) incorporating security situations; (iii) enhancing monitoring 
and evaluation frameworks; (iv) engaging with civil society, including for service delivery; (v) enhancing 
operational flexibilities; and (vi) systematizing use of digital solutions.  
The operation will have two components: A Program-for-Results (PforR) component which will be 
complemented by an Investment Project Financing (IPF) component mainly for the provision of essential 
health services in conflict areas. The PforR is a continuation of the Health Sustainable Development Goals 
PforR, which closed in June 2021.Support to the health sector in Ethiopia is needed to continue improving 
maternal and child health outcomes. The support from development partners working in the health sector 
through the Sustainable Development Goals Pool Fund (SDGPF) as the main source of financing for maternal 
and child health, will continue to sustain the gains that have been made so far. The SDGPF has been financed

5. Ethiopia achieved robust economic growth and social and human development over the past decade, yet 
the country remains one of the poorest. The country’s Gross National Income (GNI) per capita has increased 
significantly over the past 15 years, reaching US$9603 in 2021 using the Atlas method (current US$), but it 
remains substantially lower than the Sub-Saharan average of US$1,578. Ethiopia is ranked 173 out of 189 
countries on the Human Development Index (2019) of the United Nations Development Program4. 
Moreover, the 2020 World Bank Human Capital Index estimates that a child born in Ethiopia today will only 
reach 38 percent of his or her potential, against a benchmark of complete education and full health.5 Despite 
declining fertility (from 5.5 to 4.6 children per woman between 2000 and 2016), about 45 percent of the 
population is estimated to be under 15 years of age, which poses significant challenges to the health system 
in providing infant and adolescent care and to the economy.6 Ethiopia faces a future with a growing labor 
supply, rapid urbanization, and an opportunity for faster economic growth with potential for a demographic 
dividend.

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context 
 
10. Ethiopia’s health care progress over the past two decades is a result of a consistent strategic framework 
implemented through the Health Sector Development Programs 1996–2015 and followed by Health 
Sector Transformation Plan (HSTP) I and II, 2015–2025. This framework is a key component of both phases 
of Ethiopia’s Growth and Transformation Plans I and II, which demonstrate the government’s support for 
human development and direct the allocation of resources to evidence-based primary health care (PHC) 
interventions and robust community outreach services programs. 
 
11. The macroeconomic impact of the conflict could affect government resource allocation for health at the 
national and local levels.19 Between 2000 and 2018, the increase in public spending on health in Ethiopia 
was driven mainly by economic growth. Ethiopia’s economy is dependent on agriculture, which accounts 
for 40 percent of GDP and 80 percent of exports. However, agriculture productivity has declined in the 
conflict-affected areas in the country; for example, in Amhara, 12 percent of crop production in 2021 was 
lost. The combined impacts of conflict, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the war in Ukraine are projected to 
slow economic growth to 3.3 percent in 2022 from 6.1 percent in 202120.

decrease in child stunting22 from 58 percent in 2000 to 37 percent in 2019, inequities persist: four regions 
had rates above 40 percent in 2019. Stunting was substantially higher in rural areas (41 percent) compared 
to urban areas (26 percent)23. The key drivers of stunting24 are poor health and nutritional status of women

13. Maternal health remains a challenge, but there are positive trends. Despite the difficulty in measuring 
maternal mortality and conflicting results from different sources, there is a consensus that Ethiopia has 
made progress in reducing maternal mortality in the last two decades. Maternal mortality ratio declined by 
more than half over 16 years between 2000 and 2016 (from 871 to 412 maternal deaths per 100,000 live 
births).28 The country has implemented a set of high-impact interventions including skilled birth services, 
antenatal care (ANC), and postnatal care.29 In recent years, the government has made concerted efforts to 
improve the coverage of maternal health services through community mobilization and service promotion 
via the health extension program, the expansion of health facilities, the increased availability of supplies, 
and the deployment of appropriately skilled health professionals.  
 
14. ANC coverage increased significantly in the past 25 years. The proportion of pregnant women who have 
had one or more ANC visits increased over 20 years from 28.2 percent in 1995–1997 to 72.1 percent in 
2016–201930. During 2016–2019, only 40.6 percent of pregnant women received the recommended four or 
more visits, and 26.8 percent were seen in their first trimester. A comparison of data for women attending 
ANC facilities between 1995–1997 and 2016–2019 shows that blood tests increased from 27 percent to 80.1 
percent, provision of iron folic acid (IFA) tablets from 34.1 percent to 76.9 percent, and urine tests from 
23.6 percent to 74.5 percent. However, the percentage of pregnant women taking the full IFA regimen (90+ 
tablets) is still very low (11 percent in 2019). Pregnant mothers are usually given the entire 90-day supply 
of IFA during their initial ANC visit, but IFA adherence is not routinely monitored throughout pregnancy.31  
 
15. Facility delivery coverage has increased by close to eightfold in the past 25 years, from 6.5 percent to over 
50 percent. The increase has been more apparent in the past five years, with health facility deliveries 
increasing fourfold, from 12.2 percent in 2010–2012 to 50.5 percent in 2016–2019. However, significant 
regional differences remain; for example, 26.5 percent of women in Somali delivered in a health facility 
compared to 97 percent in Addis Ababa. Postnatal care remains low. Postnatal care for women within two 
days of delivery has increased from 2.2 percent in 1995–1997 to 25.7 percent in 2016–2019 (ranging from 
10.3 percent in Somali to 59 percent in Addis Ababa)32.

20. Health expenditure in Ethiopia remains too low to achieve UHC. Although the HSTP I target of US$32.20 per 
capita expenditure has been achieved, it is still below the US$86 per capita recommended by the World 
Health Organization (WHO) to provide a basic package of health services in low- and middle-income 
countries. From 1990 to 2017, government spending covered only 33 percent of total health expenditure, 
while external sources and households’ out-of-pocket (OOP) expenditure accounted for 35 and 31 percent, 
respectively. In 2019, 20 percent of the population was covered by community-based health insurance (CBHI) 
compared to 1.2 percent in 201540. CBHI’s contribution to the total health expenditure has reached only 0.9 
percent in 2019 compared to 0.3 percent in 2016/2017.   
21. The government developed a Health Care Financing Strategy (2020–2024), which proposes a major shift in 
the health financing architecture. The overall objective of the Health Care Financing Strategy is to accelerate 
Ethiopia’s progress toward UHC by defining a framework for strategic actions to achieve the best health 
outcomes, financial protection against catastrophic illness, and client satisfaction in the health system. The 
Health Care Financing Strategy has five strategic objectives: (i) mobilize adequate resources, through 
traditional and innovative approaches, from domestic and external sources; (ii) reduce OOP spending at point 
of use; (iii) enhance efficiency and effectiveness; (iv) strengthen public-private partnerships; and (v) develop 
capacity for improved health care financing. This strategy is in line with the aim of HSTP II: improving the 
health of the Ethiopian population through the realization of accelerating progress toward UHC, protecting 
people from emergencies, creating woreda transformation, and making the health system responsive to 
people’s needs and expectations. Health system efficiency improvement is one of the priorities in HSTP II, 
and one of the strategic objectives of the Health Care Financing Strategy.

25. The Program for Results (PforR) instrument (referred to as ‘the Program’) is considered a good instrument 
to support Ethiopia’s HSTP in areas not affected by the conflict, for several reasons.  First, the PforR 
instrument allows the World Bank to effectively harmonize its support with key development partners and 
leverage an enhanced focus on results while supporting the government’s program using country systems 
and health sector strategies already in place, and with attention to system strengthening and enhancing 
impact and sustainability. Second, , the PforR instrument is particularly amenable to Ethiopia's health sector 
priorities and needs through the provision of non-earmarked funds that will be harmonized with support 
coming from other health sector donors.  
26. The Program is a continuation of the Health Sustainable Development Goals PforR and is considered 
appropriate in providing continued support to HSTP II. Since 2013, the World Bank has supported the health 
sector through the Ethiopia Health SDGs PforR (P123531). The Program was financed with a US$100 million 
credit from the International Development Association (IDA) and a US$20 million grant from the Health 
Results Innovation Trust Fund. It was approved by the Executive Directors of IDA on February 28, 2013. An 
additional financing (AF) comprising an IDA credit of US$150 million and grants of US$60 million from the 
Global Financing Facility (GFF) and US$20 million Trust Fund (TF) from the Power of Nutrition was approved 
on May 9, 2017. The Program achieved remarkable success toward the Program development objective 
(PDO), and all Program targets were fully achieved as verified by the 2019 mini-EDHS41. Both the parent 
Program and the AF contributed significantly toward the improvement of maternal, child, and nutrition 
health services and health system. 
27.  The Program will be complemented by an Investment Project Financing (IPF) instrument to provide 
targeted support for vulnerable groups in conflict-affected areas including the Tigray region; the IPF 
component will also support health systems development. The IPF instrument is needed for channeling 
financial and technical support to the conflict areas, and to support and enhance the development of a 
resilient health service delivery system. The support will rehabilitate health centers and facilities that are no 
longer functional to ensure improved health care and nutrition services for women, children, and 
adolescents. Furthermore, capacity building, technical assistance (TA), and information systems will be 
developed to improve the provision of quality health services, infrastructure, medicine, workforce, and 
governance, as well as financial protection.

28. The operation will coordinate with the Response, Recovery, and Resilience for Conflict-Affected 
Communities in Ethiopia Project (P177233) and the Human Capital Project (HCP, P172284) which is still 
under preparation. The operation will align with the Response, Recovery, and Resilience for Conflict-Affected 
Communities Project in providing the multisectoral linkages to ensure GBV and maternal and nutrition health 
services are provided in the conflict-affected woredas/districts. The operation will also provide clinical 
support and counseling for GBV and maternal and child health services at all health facilities, including those 
in conflict affected areas. The operation will also align with the planned HCP to increase access to quality 
services delivered through the health system. The HCP will increase demand for health services and 
strengthen multisectoral linkages with nutrition-sensitive interventions outside of the health sector. The 
operation will reinforce this effort by focusing on the supply side.

A. Government Program 
 
29. The overall objective of HSTP II is to improve the health of Ethiopia’s people by accelerating progress 
toward UHC, protecting communities during health emergencies, transforming woredas, and improving 
the health system’s responsiveness. HSTP II renews the strong priority given to RMNCAH+N and sets 
ambitious targets for maternal and child health outcomes to be achieved by 2025, which would ensure 
delivery of the country’s SDG commitments. 
30. Five priority issues were identified as part of the transformation agenda for HSTP II. Key interventions will 
be implemented to address these priority issues to transform the health system. The transformation agenda 
includes: (i) ensuring quality and equity in services by creating high-performing primary health care units 
(PHCUs); (ii) digital transformation to significantly improve methods and practices for collecting, analyzing, 
presenting, using and disseminating information that can influence decisions; (iii) ensuring equitable 
distribution and availability of an adequate number and skill mix of health workers who are motivated, 
competent, and compassionate; (iv) reforming public financial management and health financing to improve 
efficiency and accountability, while pursuing the agenda of sustainable domestic resource mobilization for 
health; and (v) enhancing leadership and governance mechanisms to drive attainment of the national 
strategic objectives through activities to ensure alignment and harmonization, and ultimately, results. 
31. The expenditure framework of the Program is defined by the Program boundary, which is the SDGPF that 
fills the gaps within top priority areas of HSTP II (Table 2) and follows the overall fiscal decentralization of 
health service delivery. The SDGPF is financially managed by MOH. There is a revised joint financing 
agreement (JFA) between MOH, the Ministry of Finance (MOF), and the 12 contributors to the planning, 
budgeting, reporting, and accountability in the use of SDGPF resources. At the federal level, expenditures of 
the pool fund are primarily derived from activities defined in the annual health sector comprehensive plan. 
The development of the comprehensive plan is informed by the HSTP targets for the respective year and 
annual woreda-based indicative core plans.

33. The PDO is to improve access to and equitable provision of high-quality PHC services, with a focus on 
RMNCAH+N, while strengthening health systems. The PDO is designed to be met through a chain of 
interventions, outputs, and outcomes, using resources from the PforR  focusing on RMNCAH+N and the 
health system exclusively in non-conflict areas, and from the IPF instrument focusing on emergency health 
and nutrition response in conflict-affected areas.  
34. Improvements in the health and nutritional status of women, children, and adolescents in Ethiopia will be 
measured through overall reductions in maternal, neonatal, and child mortality and reductions in 
unplanned pregnancies using strategies suitable for areas not affected by conflict and conflict-affected 
areas. For example, to contribute to the reduction in maternal and infant mortality at the national level, 
disbursements will be made against results in non-conflict-affected woredas to increase the number of 
pregnant women attending four or more ANC visits and receiving IFA (90+) and to increase deliveries 
attended by skilled birth providers for the four low-performing regions. At the same time, maternal health 
and nutrition services in conflict-affected areas will be supported through IPF to increase the outcome of the 
same indicators. The results framework provides baselines and targets for similar outcome indicators for 
conflict- and non-conflict-affected areas).

B.  Operation Scope  
 
35. HSTP II provides the overarching strategic framework for the health sector and reflects the government of 
Ethiopia’s commitment to achieve the national transformation plan and global health SDGs. The scope of 
the PforR component of this operation will be within the main sector framework provided by HSTP II through 
the SDGPF which is a non-earmarked harmonized support to the sector using country systems (Table 4). HSTP 
II is a continuation of HSTP I and part of the long-term UHC vision that has provided strong guidance, resulting 
in consistent progress in health outcomes over the last two decades. The sector strategy has translated the 
national Growth and Transformation Plan II goals into concrete directions for the improvement of coverage, 
equity, and quality of EHS. The strategy’s focus on quality and equity requires a shift in the status quo to 
maintain the maternal, child, and adolescent health and nutrition outcomes that have been attained and to 
ultimately drive improvements at the national scale over the next five years.  The PforR will be complemented 
by an IPF instrument which will finance the provision of essential health services in the conflict area through 
Sub-component I; Strengthening the CRVS system through Sub-component II; Provision of technical 
assistance capacity building through Sub-component III; and Contingent Emergency Response Component 
(CERC) through Sub-component IV. The financing will be through resources from IDA and GFF. Overall, the 
operation is expected to benefit 19.3 million women (approximately one-third of entire female population, 
and 58 percent of women aged 15-64) and 3.3 million children aged 12-24 months (close to 100 percent of 
all children aged 12-24 months).  
B1. PforR Component Scope (US$321 million) 
 
36. The IDA and GFF support through the SDGPF will be linked to the achievement of agreed upon results 
under the government’s control. The Program will support activities under the SDGPF, except for high-value 
procurement contracts and salary payment for government staff. Synergies will be made with other donor-
funded and government programs supporting HSTP II. The priority areas for IDA/GFF support include 
improved coverage of evidence based RMNCAH+N and select health system interventions in non-conflict. 
These initiatives will help Ethiopia maintain the results gained and accelerate progress toward achievement 
of better maternal and child health.  
37. The SDGPF supports financing gaps in maternal and child health. This support includes the following: 
maternal health (equipment and commodities for emergency obstetric care, postnatal care, and 
contraceptive support); child health (climate-friendly cold chain strengthening, supply of vaccines, 
immunization campaigns); nutrition support (commodities such as IFA and deworming); health systems 
strengthening (CBHI scale up, supply chain system strengthening, etc.); and capacity building for the 
implementation of a national quality and equity strategy at the national and regional levels and the 
strengthening of health systems (procurement of medical equipment for hospitals and health centers, and 
renovation/rehabilitation of health centers). The SDGPF-financed activities are determined through an 
annual consultative process involving all key stakeholders that make up the Joint Consultative Forum (JCF). 
Consistent with the mandate of the MOH, most expenditures under the SDGPF will be made at the federal 
level, with goods and services transferred to the regions.
