A. CONTEXT AT APPRAISAL 
 
Country Context 
 
1. Djibouti is a small low-income country strategically located in the Horn of Africa with significant geopolitical 
importance. It is a gateway to East Africa, a maritime crossroad for trade, and a refueling and transshipment center. 
Djibouti’s strategic proximity to conflict areas in the Middle East, and piracy activity zones, coupled with its relative 
political stability, has made it an important location for foreign military bases. Due to its relative political stability 
and open economic policies, the country benefits from regional economic importance and has comparative 
advantage in external trade. As a result, the economy has long relied on port services, logistics and rents from military 
bases, but faces the constraints of most small states, including a heavy dependence on foreign direct investment, 
limited scope of diversification, and limited production capacity.  
 
2. At the time of appraisal, the country faced severe childhood malnutrition, unemployment, poverty, and food 
security challenges. In 2013, 22.7 percent of the population was living in poverty with less than US$1.90 per day (in 
2011 PPP terms). This had dropped to 17 percent by 2017. Gross Domestic Product per capita rose from USD2,313 
in 2013 to USD3,083 in 2018. The standard of living was much lower in rural areas as manifested by the high incidence 
of poverty, sub-standard housing and limited access to basic services. 
 
3. A severe drought in 2012 resulted in a spike in malnutrition among newborns and children, which further 
intensified vulnerability and poverty, and left 20 percent of the Djibouti-Ville population and 75 percent of the rural 
population exposed to severe and moderate food insecurity. Malnutrition was prevalent throughout the country: 
with one third of children under the age of 5 years suffering from stunted growth; 29.6 percent of children under 5 
years underweight; 29.7 percent chronically malnourished; and 17.8 percent were acutely malnourished. 
 
Rationale for Bank Assistance 
 
4. Following the drought of 2012, the Social Safety Net Project (SSNP, the Project) was prepared to deliver a quick 
response to the urgent needs of the poor and vulnerable households in Djibouti, and to contain the loss of human 
life and preserve human capital. In this context, it was prepared as an emergency operation under Operational 
Policy/Bank Policy (OP/BP) 8.00 – Rapid Response to Crises and Emergencies, complementing and building on the 
Employment and Human Capital Safety Net project (EHCSN) (P120588; TF097217), financed through a Japan Social 
Development Fund (JSDF)1. 
 
5. The Project aimed to support the government’s efforts to reduce poverty and unemployment, improve access 
to basic services with a special focus on newborn and child malnutrition as indicated in the country’s National 
Initiative for Social Development (2008-2012) (Initiative Nationale de Développement Social). The objectives of this 
Initiative were to reduce poverty, improve institutional capacity and access to basic services, and develop human

pregnant/lactating women.  These objectives were later modified as described in Section B below.

Original Components and Costs 
 
11. Component 1: Community-based labor-intensive public works and services (Original US$2.08 million). This 
component financed a workfare program selected by communities, with the objective of providing short-term 
employment opportunities to able-bodied members of the vulnerable/poor households targeted by the program. 
The aim was to allow for the upgrading of the existing assets in the selected areas. The activities included: (i) small 
civil works at the community level, such as the pavement of existing footpaths and the construction of stairs and 
small bridges to improve pedestrian circulation between key community facilities; (ii) services such as garbage 
collection and routine/periodic maintenance of footpaths; and (iii) provision of training for skills development of 
project beneficiaries. To facilitate the labor-intensive works, a menu (standard design, sketches and cost estimates) 
of eligible small works (less than US$ 50,000 per community works) was prepared and used as a basis for the 
participative prioritization of the works by the beneficiary communities. To ensure that the community works were 
labor intensive, a minimum of 60 percent of the total cost was earmarked for payments for labor. 
 
12. Component 2: Nutrition-based social assistance to support investments in human capital (Original US$1.22 
million). This component supported innovative community interventions aimed at preventing malnutrition in pre-
school children and pregnant/lactating women through training and community-based sensitization sessions. The 
original planned activities included: (i) conducting sensitization sessions and home visits by community workers; (ii) 
distributing food supplements during the lean season; (iii) developing and conducting training on the prevention of 
malnutrition and promotion of growth and child development through sensitization, in partnership with the Ministry 
of Health (MoH); and (iv) providing support to the MOH for malnutrition prevention at the community level. The 
sensitization sessions included topics such as the importance of good nutrition practices and appropriate utilization 
of key micronutrients (some sessions will consist of cooking demonstrations), improved care practices for children, 
and the importance of education. 
 
13. This component depended greatly on the local community and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) to 
conduct the nutrition session and raise the awareness of pregnant and lactating women on nutrition practices, 
including diverse diet and exclusive breastfeeding. In each community/neighborhood a mère conseillère (mother 
advisor) was selected who identified the pregnant/lactating women in her neighborhood and gathered them at her 
place to conduct the nutrition sessions. These advising mothers were well respected in the community and had 
previously received basic training on maternal and child health. They also had access to nutrition supplements that 
they gave to the children as needed and referred serious cases of malnutrition to the nearest health facility. 
 
14. Component 3: Targeting, monitoring and evaluation (Original US$1.2 million). This component aimed to 
develop a national registry of poor and vulnerable households and enhance the monitoring and evaluation capacity 
of Djibouti’s social safety net programs by building a unified database of the poor and vulnerable that can be used 
as a targeting mechanism across the different social assistance programs. Activities to be implemented under this 
component included: (i) development of a national registry of beneficiaries; (ii) establishment of an integrated

Assessment of Achievement of Each Objective/Outcome 
 
34. As shown in the theory of change presented in Table 4, the PDO statement can be divided into three outcome 
areas which support the country’s safety net system: (i) improved targeting; (ii) improved nutrition practices; and (iii) 
access to short-term employment for the poor and vulnerable. These three areas are examined in order below in the 
context of the project theory of change. 
 
Objective 1: Support a social safety net system for improved targeting 
 
35. The first objective of improving the targeting performance of the safety net system was achieved. The Project 
supported the government in developing a PMT that allows for the creation of a poverty score for each household.  
This approach uses a limited number of variables (such as ownership of assets, materials used in housing, education 
levels, and household size) that are closely correlated with poverty to estimate each household’s poverty level, given 
that administrative or tax information on household income levels does not exist. This method comes at a significantly 
lower cost than administering a traditional household survey with its lengthy consumption module that permits 
measuring poverty status.  The Project supported the administration of the PMT questionnaire to 62,752 households, 
resulting in the registration of 126,741 individuals with a unique social identification number. This data was housed 
in the social registry for which the design and development were supported by the Project. The social registry included 
the capacity for including biometric information on individuals benefitting from programs, which was used to verify

C. EFFICIENCY 
 
Assessment of Efficiency and Rating 
 
36. The efficiency rating is deemed substantial. As the Project was designed and implemented as an emergency 
response within a tight time frame, a cost-benefit analysis was not conducted at appraisal. However, the Project was

diversity and nutritional supplements); and (iii) the impact of the social registry in terms of the improvement of service

delivery model, the robustness of the system, and reduced the overall cost of targeting intended beneficiaries. The 
original budget of the project allocated 10 percent to cover the expenses of the registry, and with the two additional 
financing, this share was dropped to about 7 percent of the total project budget.

of the total project amount, less than the nine percent estimated at appraisal.

E. OTHER OUTCOMES AND IMPACTS (IF ANY) 
 
Gender 
 
41. The Project had a positive impact on gender, and the activities were notable for taking into account the gender 
gaps and addressing them to the benefit of female beneficiaries. In total, 94 percent of beneficiaries of safety nets 
programs were women (10,800 beneficiaries). The results framework included a core indicator on the number of 
female beneficiaries, which was relevant to the scope of the operation. Through its activities, the Project tackled the 
stereotypes around women participating in public works, and included specific incentives, such as adapting working 
hours, for female participation and empowerment with some activities targeting women (street cleaning, artisanal 
activities), which improved/enhanced their voice and sense of agency as members in the community. Before the 
recruiting period, door-to-door information sessions took place to give the space for women to ask their questions 
and allow them time to discuss the decision with their husbands. The Project also provided entrepreneurial activities 
for women on handicrafts products, especially those who were unable to participate in the labor-intensive activities  
 
42. In addition, the majority of participants in the labor-intensive work activities were women. The participants 
received bank accounts into which earnings were deposited. For the majority of women, it was also the first time they 
opened a personal bank account at the Caisse populaire d'épargne et de crédit (CPEC). The process evaluation showed 
that 96 percent of the women were empowered to decide how to spend the money they earned, with the majority 
of it invested in child nutrition and health. 
 
43. Prior to the Project, beneficiary women, who are the primary care-givers for infants and children, generally lacked 
information on beneficial practices related to child nutrition, parenting practices, and child simulation. The uptake 
rates for exclusive breastfeeding of children below 6 months of age was also very low due to social and cultural norms 
and a lack of awareness, not only about the benefits of breastfeeding, but also of methods and practices that are 
critical to establishing breastfeeding over the long term. Post implementation, the results of the Project indicate that 
61 percent of children under 6 months of age have been exclusively breastfed. Thus, the Project enhanced mothers’ 
knowledge of nutritional and parenting practices. 
 
44. Djiboutian women are also disproportionately vulnerable to poverty and economic shocks and face a lack of 
economic opportunities. Prior to implementation, discussions with the local community revealed how meaningful 
participation of women in public works activities could be achieved. The Project adopted these measures which 
increased the participation of women in the workfare program, and included: (i) involving women in the selection of 
public works activities and in the community management structure, where 30 percent of members were women; (ii) 
implementing the work activities in close proximity to the beneficiaries’ houses; (iii) including light and appropriate 
work for women in the work activities, including consideration for pregnant and lactating mothers (who engaged in  
handicraft activities including basketry and beading); and (iv) the selection of work hours taking into account women’s 
household obligations.

B. KEY FACTORS DURING IMPLEMENTATION 
 
62. 
Scalability and agility of design. Since its approval in 2012, the Project’s scope was expanded with two

B. ENVIRONMENTAL, SOCIAL, AND FIDUCIARY COMPLIANCE 
 
77. Environmental and social compliance. The Project complied with the World Bank’s safeguards requirements. 
The Project was classified Environmental Assessment Category B. Although the environmental risk associated with the 
Project was low due to the small scale of the civil works, potential environmental impact was identified as a possibility 
due to the physical nature of those works, and hence required the preparation of an Environmental and Social 
Screening Assessment Framework. At the initial phase of implementation, a catalogue of the approved community-
based public work activities was prepared and used throughout the life of the Project. All safeguards measures were 
designed to ensure compliance with the World Bank safeguard policies and procedures, and all project documents, 
operational manual, and the individual community-works operation sheet included the selection criteria to avoid 
inclusion of non-eligible community works. As for social safeguards’ compliance, the public works activities were 
undertaken and implemented on public lands and did not cause the involuntary taking of land nor did it lead to 
involuntary displacement of people and/or loss of habitat, resources, sources of revenues or their access to them. In 
2017, the safeguards team noted deficiencies related to the availability of first aid kits on Project sites and problems 
with the preparation of environmental and social management plans for the sites. These temporary problems were 
quickly resolved, including through organization of a training workshop in May 2017.  
 
Fiduciary compliance

D. RISK TO DEVELOPMENT OUTCOME 
 
85. Overall, the Project had a transformational and sustainable impact on Djibouti’s social safety net system and 
hence on its ability to reduce poverty among poor and vulnerable households. The systems and capacity building 
supported by the Project, including the social registry and the Social Protection Strategy, led to Government financing 
for a new cash transfer program beginning in 2016. This program is being co-financed by the European Union and the 
new IDA PITCH project, the successor to this project. The social registry is now being used for nine programs.  
 
86. The workfare activities had the goal of providing short-term employment following a crisis and were not meant 
to provide longer-term employment. The workfare activities have not been continued by Government due to the 
commitment to move to provision of cash transfers for poor and vulnerable households.  Still, the workfare activities 
provided timely assistance to poor and vulnerable households in a difficult period, preventing further declines in their 
living standards and likely avoiding the selling of assets for survival.  
 
87. The experience with the project nutrition sessions is being carried over into the accompanying measures 
(conditionalities) for the cash transfer program. Like the arrangement under this Project, the mères conseillères will 
deliver behavioral messages on nutrition as part of the cash transfer program accompanying measures. The messages 
will be expanded to cover not only nutrition, but also other messages related to building human capital, especially 
during the early years of a child’s life.

88. Systems building in a crisis context. The Project was designed under the IDA Crisis Response Window to provide 
a quick response to a drought and to contain the loss of human life and preserve human capital. Without losing sight 
of this objective, the Project also set in place over time the foundations of a permanent safety net system in Djibouti 
that would not only strengthen the country’s ability to respond to future shocks, but also to build human capital and 
fight chronic poverty. This approach should be considered in similar crisis response projects as a means to bridge the 
gap from shorter-term emergency assistance to longer-term development. 
 
89. Incremental and scalable design. At design, Djibouti was facing natural disasters, mainly the flood and its 
implications on poverty and food security. The World Bank team was determined to design a project to act as an 
emergency response to support the Djiboutian people to go through the economic shocks. The team capitalized 
initially on the design of another project operation, and then introduced changes and modifications during the two 
rounds of additional financing. This strategy helped the Djiboutian counterparts in implementing the initial activities 
in a relatively timely manner, and to introduce new activities and expanding coverage as the project matured. The 
flexible and adaptive nature of the Project has proven effective in responding to changing and growing demands and 
needs. 
 
90. Government commitment and vision. Scaling up safety nets required an efficient system for targeting the poor 
and vulnerable, and a key element to strengthening social safety nets is the creation of a social registry of poor and 
vulnerable households. A PMT score was used to determine the household’s’ poverty score, and the government has 
used this targeting tool for its social programs to target the poorest, rather than the reliance on targeting the rural 
households based on geographic location. SEAS has championed this effort and was able to capitalize on the social 
registry to better target the poor beneficiaries. 
 
91. Institutionalization of the community-based approach and social cohesion. Nutrition related activities of the 
Project relied on community sessions to promote positive behaviors. These sessions have provided tailored sessions 
on pre-natal care and for mothers with children under two years of age (up to age of 5 years in rural areas). The 
sessions have also provided a platform to detect potential stunting of participating children by triggering a subsequent 
home visit to provide more individualized counseling, and/or referral to the nearest health clinic.  The positive impact 
on the community-level behavior change sessions has led to the inclusion of such approach in the new PITCH project 
by tailoring these sessions around themes related to human capital development. The labor-intensive works was also 
prepared and used in participatory approach with the involvement and ownership of local communities. 
 
92. Role of mères conseillères. The positive behavior change in nutrition aspects was due the role of the mères 
conseillères. They were trusted by young mothers, had respected positions in the communities, and had the ability to 
communicate key messages in the local context, using simple tools and practical training materials. As mentioned 
earlier, this experience has proven to be effective and under the national cash transfer program the mères conseillères 
will deliver messages covering not only nutrition, but also other messages related to building human capital, especially 
during the early years of a child’s life.    
 
93. Switch to Cash Transfers. Labor intensive public works were meant to provide a cash transfer to the beneficiary 
to provide income and improve livelihoods. These were an appropriate response to the crisis context at project 
appraisal and for the implementation period. However, the Government has now shifted its focus to a longer-term 
poverty reduction approach of social assistance. For this goal, cash transfer without a work conditionality can deliver

ANNEX 4. EFFICIENCY ANALYSIS 
 
 
1. As the Project was designed and implemented as an emergency response within a tight time frame, a cost-
benefit analysis was not conducted at the appraisal stage. However, the Project was able to contribute to 
three types of economic returns: (i) impact on beneficiary households in terms of increased incomes from 
work for cash programs through community works; (ii) improvement in health and nutrition among 
beneficiary households due to increased income effect, and less food insecurity (increased food 
consumption, dietary diversity and nutritional supplements); and (iii) the impact of the social registry in terms 
of the improvement of service delivery capacity and accountability, and thereby lowering the cost of 
coverage. 
 
2. The social registry established under the project allowed for identification of individuals and efficient 
targeting, and it enabled the social programs to effectively reach beneficiaries in a timely manner, which 
improved the service delivery model, the robustness of the system, and reduced the overall cost of targeting 
intended beneficiaries.  
 
3. Social safety nets can help reduce poverty in the country when they efficiently reach the poor, and focusing 
specifically on the first 1,000 days to prevent undernutrition is crucial as the effects of undernutrition during 
this critical window can have detrimental effects on health, brain development, educational attainment, and 
productivity in the long-run.  
 
4. Results from the evaluation of the project indicated that the workfare program had a significant effect on 
the employment of women and labor force participation and there was no substitution effect of labor supply 
observed within the household. Also, results show a significant net increase in household income in the short-
term and the income gain translates into shifts in household expenditures and food diversity. As a result of 
the workfare offer, children are more likely to be eating food rich in vitamins: an increase of six percent (from 
85.9 to 91.1 percent) for children below five years of age, and an increase of 8.5 percent (from 85.2 to 92.5 
percent) for the youngest child (below two years of age). Results also show that the youngest child is more 
likely to have a diversified diet (an increase of 10 percent, from 69.4 to 76.3 percent).

ANNEX 5. BORROWER, CO-FINANCIER AND OTHER PARTNER/STAKEHOLDER COMMENTS 
 
 
1. This section presents a summary of the Borrower’s completion report6. 
 
2. Context and justification of the project. The project was designed to provide a minimum level of protection 
to the poor and vulnerable groups of the population in face of recurrent drought and natural disasters. The 
design and concept were first applied through the Employment and Human Capital Safety Net Project, 
financed through a grant by Japan Social Development Fund as a pilot in December 2010, This grant 
supported piloting an innovative integrated safety net for poor and vulnerable households by combining a 
workfare program with a social assistance for nutrition using a community-driven and participatory approach 
to serve as a basis for a national safety net in Djibouti. It improved the efficiency of nutrition interventions 
and encouraged the government to develop a social protection strategy where social safety net was a 
cornerstone. Building on the success of the pilot, the government received an IDA grant in 2012 (H779-DJ), 
and two additional financings followed to expand the scope of the project.  
 
3. In 2016, the government put in place a national cash transfer program called Programme National de 
Solidarité Famille (National Program for Family Solidarity - PNSF) to address extreme poverty and provide 
social protection to the most vulnerable segments of the population.  
 
4. The parent project and the first additional financing had four components: (i) Community-based labor-
intensive public works and services; (ii) Nutrition-based social assistance; (iii) monitoring and evaluation; and 
(iv) project management and administration. The original objective of the project and the first additional 
financing did not include the building of the social safety net through institutionalization of the social registry 
and capacity building for the staff at SEAS and ADDS. For this purpose, the project was restructured during 
the second additional financing to have three components: (i) community-based labor-intensive works and 
services for the poor and vulnerable households; (ii) strengthening the social safety system; and (iii) project 
management and administration.  
 
5. At the beginning of project implementation, the target population was pregnant women, lactating mothers, 
children aged 0-24 month, and children aged 0-59 months (for Obock and Dikhil), and the areas of 
interventions were determined according to the prevalence of malnutrition, food insecurity, and extreme 
poverty rates.  
 
6. The project has achieved its objectives, surpassed most of its target indicators, and was rated satisfactory by 
the Bank in its aide memoires. The intermediate results of the impact evaluation showed positive impacts on 
households’ income with an increase of 40% from the cash transfer, and an improved food diversity with an 
increase in total consumption of fruits and vegetables.  
 
7. The social registry established by the Ministry of Social Affairs was used to identify poor households and have 
them registered in a biometric database with a special social number for each member of the households. 
This information was later used to coordinate the national efforts to deliver social programs to the poorest
