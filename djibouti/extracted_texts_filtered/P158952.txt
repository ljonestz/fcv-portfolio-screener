A. CONTEXT AT APPRAISAL 
 
Context 
1. In 2013, the gross domestic product (GDP) rate of Djibouti grew by an estimated 5 percent in alignment with the 
country’s acceleration of economic growth driven primarily by its transport, port-related, and capital-intensive 
activities. This acceleration of growth in the 2000s came after a decade of significant output decline and a period of 
political turmoil. Djibouti port’s strategic geographical location boosted transit trade with Ethiopia and inter-country 
shipments, attracting large public and foreign direct investments. Substantial inflows of foreign investment to 
finance the construction of a new port and hotel infrastructure generated growth but provided limited outputs that 
were insufficient to alleviate the country's high levels of poverty and unemployment. 
2. This one-sector-driven economic growth in Djibouti led to little change in inequality, unemployment, and extreme 
poverty especially in rural areas. Because of limited job opportunities around port activities, unemployment rate 
remained high especially for youth under 30, hovering between 60 and over 70 percent. At the time of appraisal, 
government data indicated that more than one-fifth of the population was living in extreme poverty, unable to cover 
basic food needs. The national poverty rate was estimated at 40.7 percent, with higher figures (62.5 percent) in rural 
areas. This situation called for a focus on private sector development (PSD)  to encourage growth in other sectors, 
create jobs, and promote shared prosperity.  
3. Despite the recent growth, Djibouti’s private sector remained embryonic and poorly organized, crippled by many 
issues including significant fiscal constraints, an unfavorable business environment, and an underdeveloped legal 
and judiciary system. Existing businesses were mainly small and medium enterprises (SMEs) concentrated in the 
tertiary sector. According to the World Bank's 2012 ‘Djibouti New Growth Model Report’, most of these smaller 
companies were operating in the services (29 percent), trade (27 percent), and transit (19 percent) sectors. Business 
climate remained very difficult and burdensome for most enterprises, and the costs for starting a new business were 
very high (184.7 percent of per capita income). These difficulties faced by entrepreneurs were worse for female 
business owners. Even though the Government initiated several reforms with regard to some of the commercial 
courts in 2011—to address the gaps in services to support private sector development—the judicial system was still 
ill-fitted. It was characterized by slow procedures resulting from insufficient resources, training, independence, and 
impartiality. In addition to these constraints, the regulatory framework did not facilitate enterprise creation, nor did 
it provide incentives for new entrepreneurs who also faced limited access to market information.  
4. In this context, the Government released in 2014 the ‘Djibouti Vision 2035’ which laid out priority actions for 
private sector development. These priorities include: (a) the diversification of the economy, including port activities 
such as transit trade and transshipment; (b) the reduction of poverty and unemployment for a more equitable 
growth; and (c) leveraging of Djibouti’s strategic geographical position to link the Middle East and Africa. To achieve 
these goals, the Government requested technical assistance to develop and support relevant actions aiming to foster 
a business environment conducive to enterprises creation and facilitation of private investment.  
5. On the financial sector side, the Financial Sector Assessment Program (FSAP) conducted in 2008 (along with the 
update completed in 2010) identified important weaknesses in the country's financial infrastructure, leading the 
Central Bank of Djibouti (Banque Centrale de Djibouti CBD) to request assistance to develop a holistic and

6. The country lacked a reliable payment system to support service delivery. It was expected that an efficient, secure, 
and reliable payment system, such as the one supported under the project, will help reduce the cost of exchanging 
goods and services. The payment system, with its wide and cost-effective range of financial instruments, will be an 
essential tool for the effective implementation of monetary policy. It will also allow a channel for settlement of all 
types of private transactions, including cross-border financial flows. With the support of the World Bank, the 
Government of Djibouti designed the GoPro to improve the business environment, enhance its capacity to attract 
private investors in key promising sectors, and strengthen the capacity of local SMEs to be able to respond to demand 
and market needs.

B. SIGNIFICANT CHANGES DURING IMPLEMENTATION (IF APPLICABLE) 
 
Revised PDOs and Outcome Targets  
15. Following a request by the Government in 2015, the project undertook a Level 1 restructuring and received AF, 
leading to a revision of the PDO and the components. The parent project was then scaled up and a new component 
related to the establishment of a National Automated Payment System was added. The restructuring led to a revision 
of the Results Framework to introduce new indicators for the new component and revise existing indicators related 
to the initial components. The PDO was changed to focus on outcomes that are more directly linked to the project 
and  the revised PDO became: "to improve the efficiency of business regulation and establish an automated National 
Payment System." The revision also resulted in an initial extension of the closing date by 12 months, which was then 
prolonged by an additional 6 months following the Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic and its related 
disruptions. The final project closing date was thus set to June 30, 2021.

Revised Components 
20. The following changes were applied to the project components.  
a. Component 1 was renamed ‘Investment Climate Reform’ to encompass the scale-up in scope and 
magnitude of the reforms initially planned. These actions were to be performed in collaboration with the 
new Economic Development Council of Djibouti (Conseil de Développement Economique de Djibouti, CDED) 
established in 2016. This component was also expected to identify additional interventions in the areas 
measured by the Doing Business report.   
b. A new Component 2 was introduced: Establishment of an Automated National Payments System. This 
component was expected to modernize and automate check processing while creating the foundation for 
the development of an interbank market. Activities under this component included (i) the acquisition of a 
technical payments system solution called ‘Automated Transfer System plus’ (ATS +) by the CBD (software, 
hardware, and system integration services); (ii) technical assistance and capacity building for system 
implementation; and (iii) rehabilitation and equipment of physical infrastructure to host the system (primary 
and secondary centers). Here, activities were organized around three subcomponents:  
 Subcomponent 2.1. Development of the Core Payments System Infrastructure 
 Subcomponent 2.2. Setting Data Centers, Systems Integration Services, and Capacity Building of 
the CBD 
 Subcomponent 2.3. Technical Assistance for the Operationalization of the Payment Systems 
Infrastructure 
c. The initial Component 2 under the parent project for ‘Project Management’ became the new Component 3 
‘Project Coordination and Management’ and included scaling-up support to the ANPI to deliver the new 
activities. 
Other Changes 
21. The larger scope and AF of the project led to changes in project components and costs, disbursement estimates, 
procurement, and implementation schedule.  
Rationale for Changes and Their Implication on the Original Theory of Change 
22. During project implementation, the Government initiated a request for AF to leverage the existing positive 
momentum in the reforms area to expand reform actions and include the implementation of the new payment 
system. The proposed revision responded to the Government’s evolving needs and aimed to support further 
institutional and regulatory reforms of the business environment and the modernization of the country’s NPS. The 
request was also supported by the desire to improve Djibouti’s business environment especially for SMEs and 
benchmark processes against other countries. The restructured project was expanded to include greater focus on 
higher-level political commitment along with financial and technical assistance in line with international best practices 
to support these objectives.  
23. The changes also benefited from lessons learned during project preparation and the first year of implementation 
related to the lack of capacity of the project implementation agency. The parent project was showing limited 
progress due to the weak capacity of the Project Implementation Unit (PIU) and lack of coordination across various

A. RELEVANCE OF PDOs 
 
Assessment of Relevance of PDOs and Rating: High  
26. The PDO was highly relevant to the Government’s overarching Vision 20351 developed in 2014 and is still highly 
relevant to the strategy for accelerated growth and employment promotion2 as well as priorities of the new 
National Development Plan (NDP), Djibouti Institutions-Connectivity-Inclusion (ICI) for 2020–24. Developed in a 
participatory manner and within the framework of the Djibouti Vision 2035, this second NDP considered the 
international commitments to which Djibouti subscribed, in particular the African Union’s Agenda 2063 and the 
United Nation’s (UN’s) 2030 Agenda with the overall objective of making Djibouti “a stable, peaceful, clean, secure 
country providing a serene environment for private initiatives.” The PDO is particularly relevant for two out of the 
three strategic axes of the NDP:3 
a) ‘Strategic Axis 1: Inclusion’ aiming to: (i) rethink and operationalize the inclusive sustainable 
development model, with an increased role for private initiatives as an essential lever for the 
diversification of production and trade and (ii) ensure a better distribution of the fruits of growth to 
Djiboutians. This will be achieved through increased support for inclusive economic development but also 
improvement of living conditions and social inclusion. Better access to finance will also be provided to 
SMEs and households to foster their use of financial or payment services to start and expand businesses, 
invest, and so on. 
 
b) ‘Strategic Axis 3: Institution’ aiming to strengthen the country’s human and institutional capacities to: (i) 
guarantee the realization of rights and freedoms; (ii) provide Djibouti with democratic, stable, 
transparent, and effective institutions; and (iii) strengthen social cohesion. This axis particularly targets 
economic and financial governance, administrative governance, institutional capacity building in local 
governance, and improved judicial and political governance. 
 
 
27. The GoPro Project was the first private sector development operation in Djibouti, laying the foundation to improve 
conditions for private sector development and growth. With World Bank Group (WBG) support, Djibouti has made 
a considerable effort to improve its investment climate in recent years. The country has reduced the number of steps 
and the cost required for starting a business, enhanced the protection of minority investors, facilitated the process to 
register property as well as significantly reduce the related proportional fees, strengthened the resolution of 
insolvency and commercial disputes, and made it easier to leverage movable collateral for SMEs to access financing. 
The newly established automated payment system is already helping to facilitate the clearing, settlement, and 
recording of monetary and other financial transactions and contributing to the development of safe and efficient 
financial markets, which constitute a crucial source of financing for economic growth. It also fosters financial stability

done to render the PDO more specific and provide better links to previous and new results. The restructuring was 
combined with AF and the scope of the project actually got expanded: in such cases the split ratings method is not 
required. More precisely, the following modifications occurred and further justify the non-application of split ratings:

31. The project substantially achieved the two objectives of the revised PDO: improve the efficiency of business 
regulation and establish an automated National Payment System. Achievement of the first objective, inherited from 
the parent project, is measured by two PDO-level indicators: the average number of days to create a business and the 
average number of days to register a property. Achievement of the second objective is measured by the percentage 
of transfers processed through the ATS. The achievement of those objectives and the causal link is detailed below.  
a. By the closing date, the project substantially achieved the first objective by successfully reducing business 
creation procedures to 72 hours based on the One-Stop-Shop automated information system data which 
tracks in real time official statistics of business creation.5 The initial data source for the indicator is the Doing 
Business report which was discontinued. This specific indicator was under the process of a data challenge 
request discussion with the government due to misalignment with the data from the One-Stop Shop 
information system. To avoid data discrepancies, an official decree was signed to legally enforce mandatory 
business creation through the One-Stop Shop for typical legal forms relating to SMEs, sole proprietorships, 
and limited liability companies. This aimed to also avoid any parallel system used by notaries (and lawyers) 
that may increase costs and processing time for entrepreneurs.  
b. The second indicator of property title registration was partially achieved, exceeding the midpoint; it was 
reduced from 39 days to 24 days instead of the initially planned 10 days. This partial success was due to the 
analog land registry system still in place. Unless the system is digitalized, process improvement goals remain 
limited. This issue has been documented during implementation and a study has been completed in 
partnership with the Urban Global Practice to provide recommendations and steps for digitalization and 
modernization of the land registration system. Furthermore, some additional achievements in this area can 
be credited to the project and justify the positive rating, even though they are not captured by the official 
project indicators. More precisely, Djibouti made property transfer easier and more transparent by reducing 
registration fees, implementing strict deadlines to register sale agreements with tax authority, scanning the 
majority of land titles for Djibouti-Ville, and legally requiring all property sales transactions to be registered 
at the land registry to become opposable to third parties.  
c. By the closing date the project achieved the objective related to the new payment system by exceeding the 
initial target of 10 percent of transactions processed through the system. Despite COVID-19 delays in 
equipment shipment and installation, the system went live as scheduled and has processed an estimated 90 
percent of transactions by June 2021.  
d. The World Bank IDA guarantee and the financial and technical assistance allowed to bring in the relevant 
experts, build the capacity of the PIU and the Central Bank, and facilitate coordination among all 
beneficiaries’ agencies to achieve this role. Before the project scope was expanded, the trust-funded 
business environment reform program was barely making progress and no financing was available to 
implement the recommended payment system modernization activities specified by the FIRST-funded 
project. The GoPro was able to succeed in this area because it benefitted from stronger government

A. KEY FACTORS DURING PREPARATION 
 
52. The necessity to reinforce the technical capacity of the implementing agency was highlighted early on in 
the project. During discussions around the choice of the PIU, the initial project team raised the issue that the 
ANPI might be ill-fitted to support project implementation due to lack of adequate skills and appropriate 
motivation. Upon the client's insistence, the World Bank team agreed to build the ANPI’s capacity along the 
way to ensure successful project implementation. The project was approved by the board in June 2014 and 
there was an agreement on a number of actions that the implementing agency must undertake on time. The 
Client had 120 days to fulfill the effectiveness condition, recruit FM and procurement specialists no later than 
30 days after effectiveness and prepare and formally approve the final draft of the Project Operations 
Manual. In September 2014, there was very little progress in drafting the operations manual, drafting 
subsidiary agreements (effectiveness condition), and hiring the specialists.  
 
B. KEY FACTORS DURING IMPLEMENTATION 
53. The restructuring resulted from the evolving needs of the Government in terms of NPSs and the intensity 
of the level of support needed by the ANPI as evidenced during the early stages of implementation. First, 
the implementation of the new payment system requested by the government required more activities and 
new reforms to be implemented. Second, during the early stages of implementation, the ANPI's weak 
capacity and poor compliance prevented them from succeeding in handling the coordination of all 
beneficiary agencies of the initially planned reform agenda and successfully handling FM. During the 
restructuring, project components were revised to cover the ATS+ implementation activities and more 
resources were allocated to increase support and capacity building for the ANPI. 
54. Throughout the course of the project, even after the restructuring, the ANPI's performance contributed to 
important delays due to the lack of regularity in the implementation of the measures recommended during 
the previous missions. Early on, few activities planned were able to progress. A year into the project, the 
disbursement rate was 12 percent. The ANPI demonstrated difficulties particularly with regard to fiduciary 
and operational procedures. The FM specialist left in December 2015 and a new one was hired in February 
2016. The AF proposal submission to the board was pushed to December 2016, a year after the request, due 
to little progress resulting from these issues. These project management challenges lasted throughout the 
duration of the project until the ANPI's performance issues were raised with the CMU and high-level 
authorities toward closing. The agency argued that the latest delays are a result of lack of resources for staff

the Central Bank. The process of selecting the service provider responsible for setting up the network faced 
serious issues and delays during implementation due to Djibouti Telecom's demands. The monopolist agency 
charged high bandwidth costs and requested a large share in the financial offers of the potential service 
providers, resulting in extremely large financial offers which could not be funded by the project. In response, 
with the support of the international consultant and the ANSIE, the project explored the following options: 
(a) reviewing the specifications to limit the TORs of the network to be financed to the strict minimum 
required and (b) setting up a deadline and quickly relaunching the selection process within the set time 
frame. It was also agreed that the CBD and ANSIE would negotiate directly and quickly with Djibouti Telecom 
to significantly reduce the bandwidth costs. The discussions and negotiations between the CBD and Djibouti 
Telecom lasted over two years and required the intervention of high-level Government officials at the 
presidency and the World Bank's support. An agreement was finally reached to directly contract the telecom 
provider to put in place the optic fiber network at a competitive and subsidized price.   
57. The CBD suffered from weak capacity to perform activities under the new component of the automated

be commended for successfully completing these activities with the support of a dynamic staff, to the point 
of exceeding targets even with the COVID-19 pandemic-related delays. The CBD has recurrently raised their 
need for additional capacity building beyond the project to ensure proper management of the system.

The presence of the local consultant was critical during this period as remote supervision would have been insufficient 
given Djibouti's connectivity limitations.  
77. The WB was flexible during the COVID-19 pandemic and processed a six-month extension of the project closing 
date to give authorities additional time to implement project activities. Partial lifting of global health measures 
finally made travel and equipment delivery possible in 2021 and the NPS was implemented successfully. All banks are 
connected and interfaced either directly or manually with the NPS. During the first month of production of the ATS + 
system, the entire financial center, that is, 11 active institutions, carried out more than 1,205 transactions through 
the system, for a total time of nearly FDJ 50 billion, based on consolidated statistics from the CBD project.

D. RISK TO DEVELOPMENT OUTCOME 
 
80. The risk to development outcome is Substantial due to issues related to the sustainability of the good 
practices established by the project. While the project succeeded in improving practices and reforming the 
business environment with the One-Stop Shop, the NPS, and the regulations, it can already be seen that in 
many of the subcomponents the development outcomes are likely to have reduced long-term impact if 
reforms and support are not sustained. To illustrate, at the time of this ICR, statistics of decisions have not 
been published by the Commercial Chamber since 2018,  the data for business creation is not yet available for 
2020 at the one stop shop, and it is unclear at this stage how the capacity of the CBD will continue to be 
sustained to manage the ATS + system as traffic increases.  
81. To mitigate the risk of losing all the progress made in the investment climate, the WBG could continue 
engaging with the Government through technical assistance to complement the project activities. Recent 
engagement with the Government has launched discussions around the need to pursue more private sector 
development reforms and provide additional support to MSMEs. A new private sector development operation 
will need to factor in sustainability mechanisms for the existing reforms and practices, additional capacity 
building for institutions, and technical assistance to continue modernizing the payment system. The World 
Bank is also working with the European Union to mobilize resources to build on these achievements and 
support Djibouti by providing Business Development Services for MSMEs. Existing Finance Competitieveness 
and Innovation (FCI) and cross Global Practice (GP) operations can also be leveraged in that area.

82. Lesson 1: The choice of implementing agency for future implementation. At project design, three 
implementation mechanisms were considered: (a) direct implementation by the ministry, meaning 
using the Government system without an appointed PIU; (b) setting up of a new PIU; or (c) use of an 
existing entity (ANPI) that has a mandate of investment promotion to implement the project. The first 
option was ruled out due to low capacity and fiduciary issues/risks. The second option was not cost-
effective with the low IDA allocation. This left the third option of working with the ANPI as it would 
also help strengthen the institutional capacity of a key PSD entity in the country. Unfortunately, 
working with the ANPI became harder than expected. The agency's implementing capabilities were 
questioned early on as they were unequipped to lead such an important reforms agenda and 
coordinate a very complex inter-ministerial and inter-sectoral action plan. The weak performance was 
mainly due to the lack of appropriate skills and motivation. An effort was made to establish a steering 
committee whose role would have been to support inter-agency collaboration at a higher level and 
oversee the ANPI's work. After restructuring, with the leadership of the Ministry of Finance and close 
technical assistance of WB experts, the project team was able to organize and deliver the reforms 
action plan through sectoral technical work groups. During the preparation of future projects, the PIU 
choice must be based on a sound and thorough study of the required capacities to run the specific 
project, and consistent human, financial, as well as technical resources must be allocated to support 
their activities throughout all project phases.  
83. Lesson 2: Implementation support and monitoring requires local staffing and cannot be done 
through missions only. The limited capacity of the ANPI required constant support and 
micromanagement with recurrent follow-up and support from the local FCI consultant assigned to the 
project. In addition, when the COVID-19 pandemic hit with travel restrictions, remote monitoring 
became even more difficult due to the poor quality of telecom infrastructures and the Internet 
network. The presence of a dedicated expert in the field was crucial for the project’s successful 
closure. Future operations in Djibouti require establishing a stronger FCI presence through local 
staffing of private sector development specialists who can continually mobilize the Government and 
relay information on time to management.  
84. Lesson 3: CMUs play a key role in supporting project implementation in difficult contexts. One of 
the key agents of success of this project is the local CMU. The CMU often intervened with the 
Government and the ANPI to find practical solutions for the various project management challenges 
and ensure on-time delivery. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the CMU acted even more as a bridge 
between the client and World Bank headquarters to support communication and timely decision 
making. In similar contexts, CMUs’ relations and resources can be leveraged to facilitate engagement, 
and foster motivation at the country level.   
85. Lesson 4: More flexible results frameworks and indicators will better capture project's 
achievements and impacts. As discussed in previous sections of this ICR, the set goal for the reduction 
in property title registration was not reached due to the analog state of the existing registration 
system. The target of this indicator was deemed unrealistic under the existing conditions. Even though 
the project succeeded in reducing the registration process by more than a half, this indicator is shown 
as only partially achieved. Results achievements would have been better captured if this target had 
been revised and additional indicators capturing measurable improvements in the registration 
process had been included at the PDO level.

sustainability of these reforms. Future investment climate reforms must be designed following a

88. Lesson 7: The sustainability of actions undertaken through this project could be undermined if no

the One-Stop Shop.

related to interagency negotiations or all other crucial matters that can delay implementation. The 
issue with Djibouti Telecom speaks to the risks a project faces when certain prior informal agreements 
are reached with the country in the project preparation process but are not then upheld later. In this 
case, had the World Bank team known that Djibouti Telecom would renege on the deal, a signed 
contract should have been required with Djibouti Telecom and the CBD as part of effectiveness 
conditions, to really ensure it happened.

ANNEX 4. EFFICIENCY ANALYSIS 
 
1. Given the technical assistance nature of private sector development projects such as GoPro, a qualitative 
analysis of the project’s benefits and identifying catalyst levers that might induce further benefits was 
completed at project appraisal. The project was designed with the aim of facilitating business creation and 
strengthening commercial and civil justice to tackle governance and investment climate issues that constrain firm 
creation and operation. The project activities included the establishment of a One-Stop Shop for business entry; 
technical assistance supporting reforms of the regulatory framework associated with business entry; 
strengthening the civil and commercial judiciary system; and overall capacity building of the implementing 
agencies and entities in charge of business entry. The immediate economic benefits expected from these and 
other improvements in the country’s investment climate include: 
 
Increase in newly registered businesses 
 
Streamlined procedures and reductions in registration fees 
 
Reduction in time to start a firm 
 
2. Given the positive momentum of reforms and Government commitment to build on these reforms, the project 
scope was expanded, AF was added (more than tripling the projects initial allocation), and the project was 
restructured to strengthen M&E and implementation capacity at the PIU. Initiated at the request of the 
Djiboutian authorities, the AF scaled up the parent project to support further institutional and regulatory reforms 
of the business environment and modernize the country’s payment system. Financial infrastructure was a priority 
in the private sector-led growth and financial development agenda of the Government of Djibouti and a sound 
payment system infrastructure being the first building block in creating modern financial markets and improving 
access to financial services by MSMEs and households. The new component was to implement a modern, 
automated payments system in Djibouti to replace the manual processing of checks and create the foundation for 
the development of an interbank market in Djibouti. The economic benefits of this new component include: 
 
Decrease in loan processing time for loans and SMEs credit 
 
Enabling banks and other institutions to offer credit transfers and direct debits for retail payments 
(salaries, bill payments, tax payments) for greater inclusion  
 
Electronic recording of securities for potential use as collateral for liquidity 
  
Beyond immediate benefits of the project, the complementarity nature of project components helped to 
create synergies and spillovers in advancing the reform agenda and reaching a wider group of SMEs. 
Through project interventions, strong political support and buy-in for these reforms was created with a 
formal direct line to the Prime Minister’s office and Ministry of Finance and new Ministry of Investment 
established to drive the investment climate reforms. The project built capacity at both the CBD and the 
PIU within the Ministry of Investment, and other agencies through close supervision and sustained 
involvement from the team on the ground, strengthening procurement and financial expertise and 
establishing M&E systems to strengthen coordination across multiple ministries and agencies, and 
enhance efficiency and transparency of services from other agencies (land registry, firm registration, 
construction permits, and the courts) toward greater support for MSMEs beyond the life of the project. 
Overall, Djibouti made substantive advances in modernizing the commercial and civil justice system 
through (a) strengthening minority investor protections by requiring greater disclosure of 
transactions to interested parties, strengthening remedies against interested directors, extending access 
to corporate information before trial, increasing shareholders’ rights and role in major corporate 
decisions, clarifying ownership and control structures, and requiring greater corporate transparency; (b) 
 
The World Bank 
 
Djibouti: Governance for Private Sector Development and Finance Project (P146250) 
 
 
 
 
Page 44 of 50 
     
 
making the enforcement of contracts easier by establishing a dedicated division within the first-instance 
court to resolve commercial cases and by adopting a new Code of Civil Procedure that regulates voluntary 
conciliation and mediation proceedings, as well as time standards for key court events; and (c) making 
resolving of insolvency easier by making insolvency proceedings more accessible for creditors and granting 
them greater participation in the proceedings.  
3.  The modernization of payment infrastructure, critical for developing stable and efficient financial markets, 
helped improve the business environment for enterprises to conduct their business, and helped promote 
financial inclusion to the unbanked and financially underserved households and MSMEs. Through parallel 
financing from FIRST, the project supported technical assistance for the drafting of a new payment systems law, 
subsequently approved by the Parliament on June 29, 2016. A credit registry and Government Guarantee Fund 
was also established for SMEs. This improved environment will eventually contribute toward employment 
generation, poverty reduction, and shared economic prosperity.  
 
4. As the first Private Sector Development operation in the country, the project successfully coordinated across 
multiple ministries and agencies, with varying levels of capacity causing some delays. (1) Coordination issues: The 
project was initially set up by the Investment Climate Department and the Djibouti private sector (Chamber of 
Commerce, ODPIC, and ANPI) and then expanded to include several other agencies including the Ministry of 
Finance, Ministry of Investment, and Djibouti Telecom, which required close coordination among each other. (2) 
Bureaucratic issues: During project appraisal, the Government estimated no cost to the CBD for procurement of 
the interbank network from Djibouti Telecom. However, proposals received from the telecom company were very 
high, leading to an unexpected delay and cost to be addressed through negotiations with Djibouti Telecom that

5. Despite these challenges, the project was able to adapt with administrative and implementation efficiency. The 
project’s simple design, strong supervision and balanced risk assessment helped overcome implementation 
challenges and achieve program objectives in reasonable time. The project was implemented in six years. The 
parent project became effective in March 2015; the project expansion and AF became effective in June 2017; and 
the project closed in June 2021, with a modest six-month extension due to COVID-19-related delays. The project 
design was simple and well-defined with several inter-connected subcomponents under each PDO. This allowed 
for efficiency in implementation and facilitated the positive economic outcomes and overall cost-effectiveness, 
despite some setbacks due to disruptions caused by the pandemic. The major delay was around implementation 
of the NPS which required physical travel of international experts and physical acquisition and installation of 
systems, all delayed by the pandemic. The World Bank and the Government of Djibouti agreed on a revised 
timetable for the formal launch of the NPS by January 2021 and the extension also allowed fulfillment of some 
indicators affected by delays in activities because of COVID-19 and provided additional room for results as the 
pandemic continued.
