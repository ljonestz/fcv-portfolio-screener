What This Assessment Found

This report presents a systematic FCV (Fragility, Conflict and Violence) Portfolio Assessment of the World Bank Group's Djibouti portfolio — 22 operations approved between 2015 and 2024, all under Investment Project Financing (IPF) instruments. Djibouti occupies a strategically critical position in the Horn of Africa: it hosts military bases for the United States, France, and China, serves as the primary maritime gateway for landlocked Ethiopia, and shares borders with Ethiopia, Eritrea, Somalia, and Yemen. This combination of strategic rents, acute youth unemployment, governance fragility, and exposure to regional conflict spillovers places Djibouti firmly within the FCAS (Fragile and Conflict-Affected States) classification — a context that raises the bar for FCV integration in Bank operations.

Each project was assessed against the WBG FCV Sensitivity and Responsiveness Screener, which evaluates eight analytical dimensions across two composites. FCV Sensitivity (Dimensions 1–3) measures how well a project understands Djibouti's fragility dynamics — its conflict drivers, political economy, and do-no-harm risks. FCV Responsiveness (Dimensions 4–8) measures whether the operational design actually adapts to that context: the theory of change, targeting logic, implementation flexibility, results framework, and One WBG integration. Scores run from 1 to 10; 7.0 is the high-performance threshold.

The headline result is a portfolio that partially understands Djibouti's FCV context but inconsistently adapts to it. The average sensitivity score of 5.6/10 (Partially Addressed) indicates that FCV analysis is engaged — but rarely deeply. The average responsiveness score of 5.0/10 (Partially Addressed) indicates that this analysis is translating into operational design only sporadically. The 0.6-point gap between sensitivity and responsiveness is the central finding of this assessment. On trajectory, both scores show a positive trend (+0.16/yr sensitivity, +0.00/yr responsiveness), suggesting the WBG FCV Strategy is beginning to have an effect — but the baseline is low enough that further deliberate effort is needed.

The polarised distribution is itself a finding: it suggests that FCV quality in Djibouti operations is driven more by individual TTL orientation and sectoral norms than by portfolio-wide institutional standards. Where one project thoroughly analyses Djibouti's fragility drivers and designs adaptive delivery accordingly, the project next door may contain no FCV analysis at all. Closing this variance — not just raising the average — should be the primary objective of quality assurance reforms.

The Portfolio: 22 Projects, 10 Years

The Djibouti portfolio screened here spans a decade of World Bank Group engagement, from 2015 to 2024. All 22 operations are Investment Project Financing (IPF) — the Bank found no DPF or Programme-for-Results instruments in the Djibouti portfolio for this period. The portfolio covers a wide range of sectors: urban infrastructure and transport, social protection, health, education, water and sanitation, private sector development, and governance and public financial management. This sectoral breadth reflects Djibouti's ambitious development agenda under the 'Vision Djibouti 2035' strategy, which aims to transform the country into a regional logistics and services hub.

Approval activity peaked in 2022 with 5 operations, reflecting a period of intensified WBG engagement. Several projects in the portfolio are Additional Financing operations extending successful earlier interventions — particularly in health, social safety nets, and urban development — signalling a degree of continuity in sectoral engagement. One project (P165558, Women and Youth Entrepreneurship) was excluded from screening as no public PDF was available via the WB Documents API; the remaining 22 projects provide a comprehensive coverage of the active portfolio.

How FCV-Integrated Is the Portfolio?

The two composite scores — sensitivity and responsiveness — are the main analytical outputs of this assessment. Sensitivity captures the quality of FCV diagnostics and do-no-harm thinking embedded in project documents; responsiveness captures whether the operational design follows from that analysis. The portfolio average sensitivity of 5.6/10 and responsiveness of 5.0/10 both fall in the 'Partially Addressed' band (4.0–6.9), meaning FCV considerations are present but inconsistently applied. The 0.6-point sensitivity–responsiveness gap is smaller than in comparable high-intensity FCV portfolios, but its presence confirms a familiar pattern: analysis outpacing adaptation.

Where Strengths and Weaknesses Lie

At the dimension level, the portfolio's strongest area is D1: FCV Context and Diagnostics (avg 6.0/10) — the dimension where FCV thinking is most consistently applied across operations. The weakest is D8: One WBG Integration (IFC/MIGA) (avg 4.5/10), which is where the most systematic design gap lies and where targeted quality review would have the highest leverage. The pattern across all eight dimensions follows the expected sensitivity-responsiveness gradient: sensitivity-side dimensions (D1–D3) generally score higher than responsiveness-side dimensions (D4–D8), even though the gap is narrower here than in more acute FCV settings.

Red Flags: Where the Risks Are

Beyond composite scores, the screener flags specific design failures that carry programmatic risk in fragile settings. These red flags represent concrete gaps that could cause harm, undermine effectiveness, or erode community trust — regardless of how a project scores on average. A project can score adequately overall and still carry a serious red flag if, for example, it names a conflict pathway but provides no mitigation, or if its results framework has no FCV-adjusted indicators whatsoever.

Across the 22-project portfolio, 16 operations trigger at least one red flag. The most prevalent is RF5: Macro Framework Unrealistic, which appears in 13 of 22 projects (59%). The chart below shows the frequency of each flag across the portfolio. In Djibouti's specific context, the most concerning pattern is not which flags are triggered most often, but that a quarter of operations carry red flags at all — in a country where Djibouti's high inequality, centralised political economy, and ethnic diversity create genuine pathways to harm if project design does not explicitly account for them. TTL-level guidance on the most common flag types — particularly distributional analysis and results framework design — would be the most efficient intervention point.

Has FCV Quality Improved Over Time?

Since the entire Djibouti portfolio is IPF, the most analytically useful cut of the data — beyond sector-level breakdowns — is temporal: has the quality of FCV integration improved between the early cohort (2015–2019, n=9) and the later cohort (2020–2024, n=13)? The 2020 WBG FCV Strategy and its associated operational guidance raised analytical expectations for operations in fragile contexts, making the post-2020 period the natural treatment group for assessing strategy impact.

The results show a mixed picture across the two cohorts. The early cohort averages 5.4/10 on sensitivity and 5.1/10 on responsiveness. The later cohort averages 5.8/10 on sensitivity and 5.0/10 on responsiveness. The increase in both composites is consistent with strategy impact, though portfolio size limits statistical confidence. What This Means for Djibouti Operations

The Djibouti portfolio demonstrates moderate FCV sensitivity and moderate FCV responsiveness across 22 screened operations. The portfolio is not failing on FCV — but it is inconsistent in ways that suggest institutional factors (TTL orientation, sectoral norms, quality review standards) rather than context understanding drive FCV quality. Given Djibouti's FCAS classification and the specificity of its fragility pathways, this inconsistency is a risk to portfolio effectiveness.

Five priorities stand out from this analysis:

  1. Raise the floor, not just the ceiling. The most pressing issue in the Djibouti portfolio is variance — the gap between the best-performing and worst-performing operations is very wide. Portfolio management should focus on systematically raising the lower tail: operations that score below 4.0 on either composite in a recognised FCAS context should be flagged for mandatory FCV Country Coordinator review before concept note approval.
  2. Address Djibouti's specific fragility pathways explicitly. Youth unemployment, ethnic marginalisation (Afar communities in particular), climate-driven displacement, and the political economy of strategic rent distribution are Djibouti's defining fragility drivers. These should be named — not implied — in every PAD's risk and contextual analysis section. Generic 'fragility' language that applies to any FCAS context is insufficient.
  3. Strengthen do-no-harm and distributional analysis. The most prevalent red flags relate to missing distributional analysis and elite capture risks. Djibouti's concentrated political economy and the dominance of a single ruling family in resource allocation make elite capture a genuine design risk in social protection, infrastructure, and private sector operations. Explicit mitigation plans should be required, not optional.
  4. Build adaptive management into results frameworks. Dimension 7 (Results Framework and Adaptive Management) is consistently among the weakest dimensions. Given Djibouti's volatility — driven by regional conflict spillovers and climate shocks — results frameworks that cannot be adjusted to FCV-relevant shocks are not fit for purpose. Contingency clauses, FCV-adjusted indicators, and mid-term review triggers should be standard provisions.
  5. Exploit One WBG opportunities more systematically. Djibouti's positioning as a logistics hub, combined with IFC's presence in the energy and financial sectors, creates genuine opportunities for joint WBG programming that are not being captured in most IPF PADs. The CMU should develop a standing coordination mechanism with IFC to ensure that investment-side analysis in PADs reflects IFC's pipeline and risk assessments.

Methodology

Data collection

Project metadata was retrieved via the World Bank Open Data API (search.worldbank.org), filtering for Djibouti (country code: DJ), approval year 2015–2024, and IPF, DPF, or P4R lending instruments. No DPF or P4R operations were found in the portfolio for this period. Document metadata was retrieved using the World Bank Documents and Reports API. Because the project_id filter proved unreliable for Djibouti in the Documents API, Project Appraisal Documents were identified through a text search pre-fetch of all Djibouti-tagged PADs, with bidirectional title-recall matching (threshold: max recall ≥ 0.55, Jaccard ≥ 0.18) to link documents to projects. ISRs and Project Papers were retrieved via direct project_id lookup with country validation. Keyword fallback search was used for remaining unmatched projects. PDF text was extracted using PyMuPDF (fitz), capped at 120,000 characters per document.

FCV screening framework

Each document was screened against the WBG FCV Sensitivity and Responsiveness Screener, grounded in the WBG FCV Strategy (2025) and FCV Operational Manual for FCV Country Coordinators (June 2025). Each dimension is rated on a 4-point scale (Strong / Substantially Addressed / Partially Addressed / Not Addressed), converted to a 1–10 numeric scale. Composite scores are weighted averages of contributing dimensions, with red flag deductions applied to the Sensitivity composite (−0.5 per red flag, floor 1). Djibouti is classified as a Fragile and Conflict-Affected State (FCAS).

Scope and limitations

  • 1 of 23 portfolio projects (P165558, Djibouti Support for Women and Youth Entrepreneurship) excluded — only procurement plans available publicly, no PAD/ICR PDF URL accessible
  • P174461 (Digital Djibouti) matched to the Eastern Africa Regional Digital Integration Programme (EARDIP) PAD — a regional document that covers Djibouti among multiple countries; country-specific content is partial
  • Four Additional Financing operations (P172979, P174566, P181612, P181415) screened against parent project PADs; results reflect the quality of the original PAD, not the AF document
  • Text extraction capped at 120,000 characters; later sections of very long PADs may not be captured
  • One primary document screened per project; ISRs and Aide-Mémoires not included unless the ISR was the only available document
  • Gap matrix cell assignments from screening agents were independently recomputed from numeric scores to ensure consistency; three agent errors were corrected during normalisation
